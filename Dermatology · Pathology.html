1. Introduction to Dermatopathology
Dermatopathology is the study of skin diseases at the microscopic level. Common biopsy techniques:
  • Shave biopsy: Removes superficial lesion; good for exophytic growths.
  • Punch biopsy: Full‑thickness core; ideal for inflammatory and bullous diseases.
  • Excisional biopsy: Complete removal of lesion with margins.
Stains: Hematoxylin & eosin (H&E) is routine. Special stains: Periodic acid‑Schiff (PAS) for fungi and basement membrane; Fontana‑Masson for melanin; Giemsa for mast cells; immunohistochemistry (S‑100, Melan‑A for melanocytes; cytokeratin for epithelial tumors).
2. Benign Epidermal Lesions
Seborrheic Keratosis
  • Very common benign proliferation of basaloid keratinocytes.
  • Clinical: “Stuck‑on” waxy papules/plaques; often pigmented.
  • Histology: Acanthosis, papillomatosis, hyperkeratosis; horn pseudocysts; basaloid cells with no atypia.
  • Variants: Reticulated (adenoid), irritated, clonal.
Verruca Vulgaris (Common Wart)
  • HPV‑induced; usually HPV 2, 4.
  • Histology: Hyperkeratosis, acanthosis, papillomatosis, koilocytes (vacuolated cells with pyknotic nuclei), vertical tiers of parakeratosis.
Actinic Keratosis (premalignant)
  • Covered in next chapter.
3. Premalignant Epidermal Lesions
Actinic Keratosis (AK)
  • Solar damage → atypical keratinocytes confined to lower epidermis (partial thickness atypia).
  • Histology: Alternating orthokeratosis and parakeratosis, loss of granular layer, basal atypia, solar elastosis.
  • Can progress to squamous cell carcinoma.
Bowen Disease (Squamous Cell Carcinoma in situ)
  • Full‑thickness epidermal atypia with intact basement membrane.
  • Clonal appearance, dyskeratosis, mitoses.
  • Arsenic exposure, HPV association (genital).
4. Malignant Epidermal Tumors
Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)
  • Most common skin cancer; low metastatic potential.
  • Risk factors: UV, fair skin, age.
  • Histology: Nests of basaloid cells with peripheral palisading, retraction artifact from stroma.
  • Variants: Nodular (most common), superficial, micronodular, infiltrative, morpheaform, pigmented.
Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)
  • Second most common; metastatic potential higher than BCC.
  • Histology: Invasive nests of atypical keratinocytes, keratin pearl formation, intercellular bridges.
  • Grading based on differentiation.
  • Variants: Acantholytic (pseudoglandular), spindle cell, verrucous.
Merkel Cell Carcinoma
  • Rare, aggressive neuroendocrine tumor.
  • Merkel cell polyomavirus associated.
  • Histology: Small round blue cells, “salt and pepper” chromatin, dot‑like cytokeratin 20 positivity.
5. Benign Melanocytic Nevi
  • Junctional nevus: Nests of melanocytes at dermoepidermal junction.
  • Compound nevus: Junctional + intradermal nests.
  • Intradermal nevus: Nests within dermis, often maturing with depth.
  • Blue nevus: Spindle melanocytes in dermis, pigmented.
  • Spitz nevus: Epithelioid/spindle cells, often in children; can mimic melanoma.
  • Dysplastic nevus (atypical mole): Architectural disorder, cytologic atypia, host response.
6. Melanoma
Malignant tumor of melanocytes. Risk factors: UV, fair skin, multiple nevi, family history.
Histologic features
  • Asymmetry, poor circumscription.
  • Pagetoid spread (single cells in upper epidermis).
  • Nests not cohesive, irregular.
  • Cytologic atypia, mitoses.
  • Lack of maturation with depth.
Major subtypes
  • Superficial spreading melanoma: Most common; radial growth phase, pagetoid spread.
  • Nodular melanoma: Vertical growth only; no radial phase; worse prognosis.
  • Lentigo maligna melanoma: Elderly, sun‑damaged skin; lentiginous pattern.
  • Acral lentiginous melanoma: Palms, soles, nail bed; more common in darker skin.
  • Desmoplastic melanoma: Spindle cells in dermis, neurotropism, S‑100+.

Breslow thickness: Measured in mm from granular layer to deepest tumor cell; most important prognostic factor.

Clark level: Anatomic level of invasion (I‑V).

7. Adnexal Tumors

Differentiate toward sweat glands (eccrine, apocrine) or hair follicles.

Hair follicle tumors
  • Trichoepithelioma: Nests of basaloid cells with horn cysts, papillary mesenchymal bodies.
  • Pilomatricoma (calcifying epithelioma of Malherbe): Shadow cells, basaloid cells, calcification.
Sweat gland tumors
  • Poroma: Broad anastomosing bands of cuboidal cells connecting to epidermis.
  • Syringoma: Small comma‑shaped ducts in dermis; common on eyelids.
  • Hidradenoma: Solid and cystic nodule with clear cells.
  • Cylindroma: “Jigsaw puzzle” pattern of basaloid cells with hyaline sheaths.
Malignant adnexal tumors (rare)
  • Microcystic adnexal carcinoma: Deeply infiltrative, perineural invasion.
  • Porocarcinoma, hidradenocarcinoma.
8. Psoriasis & Spongiotic Dermatitis
Psoriasis
  • Chronic, immune‑mediated (Th1/Th17).
  • Histology: Regular acanthosis, elongated rete ridges, thinning of suprapapillary plates, parakeratosis with Munro microabscesses (neutrophils in stratum corneum), loss of granular layer, dilated capillaries.
Spongiotic dermatitis (eczema)
  • Intercellular edema (spongiosis) within epidermis.
  • Lymphocytic exocytosis, vesicle formation in acute stages.
  • Chronic: acanthosis, hyperkeratosis.
  • Types: atopic, contact, nummular, seborrheic.
9. Lichenoid & Interface Dermatitis
  • Lichen planus: Band‑like lymphocytic infiltrate at dermoepidermal junction, saw‑tooth rete ridges, hypergranulosis, Civatte bodies (apoptotic keratinocytes).
  • Discoid lupus erythematosus: Interface dermatitis with basement membrane thickening, follicular plugging, periappendageal inflammation.

11. Granulomatous Dermatitis
  • Sarcoidosis: Naked non‑caseating granulomas.
  • Tuberculosis/lupus vulgaris: Caseating granulomas.
  • Leprosy: Lepromatous (foamy histiocytes, numerous bacilli) vs tuberculoid (well‑formed granulomas, few bacilli).
  • Granuloma annulare: Palisading granulomas around necrobiotic collagen.
  • Necrobiosis lipoidica: Palisading granulomas with layered fibrosis; diabetes association.
12. Bacterial Infections
  • Impetigo: Subcorneal pustules with gram‑positive cocci.
  • Cellulitis/erysipelas: Dermal edema, neutrophils, strep or staph.
  • Folliculitis/furuncle: Inflammation centered on hair follicle.
  • Erythrasma (Corynebacterium): Coral‑red fluorescence.
13. Viral Infections
  • Herpes simplex/varicella zoster: Intraepidermal vesicle, acantholysis, multinucleated giant cells with nuclear molding, Cowdry type A inclusions.
  • Molluscum contagiosum: Lobular epidermal hyperplasia with molluscum bodies (Henderson‑Patterson inclusions) – large intracytoplasmic eosinophilic inclusions.
  • HPV (warts): Koilocytes, hyperkeratosis, papillomatosis.
14. Fungal Infections
  • Dermatophytosis (tinea): Septate hyphae in stratum corneum (PAS or GMS stain).
  • Candidiasis: Pseudohyphae and spores; often in moist areas.
  • Malassezia (pityriasis versicolor): Spaghetti and meatballs appearance; KOH prep.
15. Parasitic Infections
  • Scabies (Sarcoptes scabiei): Mite, eggs, or scybala in stratum corneum; eosinophilic spongiosis.
  • Leishmaniasis: Amastigotes within macrophages (Leishman‑Donovan bodies).
16. Connective Tissue Diseases
  • Lupus erythematosus: Interface dermatitis, basement membrane thickening, perivascular and periappendageal lymphocytic infiltrate.
  • Dermatomyositis: Similar to lupus but with less follicular involvement; dermal mucin.
  • Morphea/scleroderma: Thickened collagen bundles in dermis, entrapment of eccrine glands.
17. Vascular Lesions
  • Hemangioma: Benign proliferation of capillaries.
  • Pyogenic granuloma (lobular capillary hemangioma): Polypoid lesion with lobules of capillaries, inflammation.
  • Angiosarcoma: Malignant vascular tumor; atypical endothelial cells, dissecting collagen; seen in scalp, lymphedema (Stewart‑Treves).
  • Kaposi sarcoma: HHV‑8; slit‑like spaces, spindle cells, extravasated RBCs, hyaline globules.
18. Soft Tissue Tumors
  • Dermatofibroma (fibrous histiocytoma): Spindle cells in dermis, storiform pattern, entrapped collagen at periphery; overlying epidermal hyperplasia.
  • Dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans (DFSP): Storiform pattern, infiltrative, CD34+, t(17;22) COL1A1‑PDGFB.
  • Atypical fibroxanthoma: Pleomorphic spindle cells in sun‑damaged skin of elderly.
  • Lipoma: Mature adipose tissue; well‑circumscribed.
