1. Esophageal Pathology
Esophageal Atresia & Tracheoesophageal Fistula

Mechanism: Failure of the foregut to divide properly during embryogenesis. Most common type: proximal esophageal pouch with distal fistula connecting trachea to distal esophagus.

Clinical: Newborn presents with drooling, choking, cyanosis after feeding, inability to pass NG tube. Associated with polyhydramnios and other VACTERL anomalies.

Achalasia

Etiology: Idiopathic degeneration of inhibitory neurons (NO‑releasing) in the myenteric plexus of the esophagus → loss of LES relaxation and absent peristalsis. May be secondary to Chagas disease (Trypanosoma cruzi destroys ganglion cells).

Pathology: Grossly dilated esophagus (megaesophagus) with thickened muscularis propria. Microscopically, loss of myenteric ganglion cells, lymphocytic inflammation.

Clinical: Dysphagia to both solids and liquids, regurgitation, chest pain, aspiration pneumonia. Barium swallow shows "bird‑beak" tapering at LES.

Esophageal Diverticula
  • Zenker diverticulum (pharyngoesophageal): Pulsion diverticulum above cricopharyngeus; presents with dysphagia, regurgitation, halitosis.
  • Traction diverticulum (mid‑esophagus): Usually due to mediastinal inflammation (e.g., TB) pulling the wall outward.
  • Epiphrenic diverticulum: Pulsion just above LES, associated with motility disorders.
Esophagitis
  • Reflux esophagitis (GERD): Repeated acid exposure → basal cell hyperplasia, elongation of papillae, eosinophilic infiltration. Can lead to stricture, ulceration.
  • Eosinophilic esophagitis: Allergic, dense eosinophils (>15/HPF), furrows, rings. Presents with dysphagia, food impaction.
  • Infectious esophagitis: Candida (pseudomembranes, dysphagia), HSV (punched‑out ulcers, Cowdry A inclusions), CMV (linear ulcers, large cells with intranuclear inclusions).
  • Pill esophagitis: Doxycycline, KCl, bisphosphonates cause chemical injury.
Barrett Esophagus

Definition: Metaplasia of squamous to columnar epithelium (intestinal type with goblet cells) in distal esophagus due to chronic GERD.

Risk of adenocarcinoma: 0.5‑1% per year. Surveillance with biopsy recommended.

Pathology: Goblet cells (Alcian blue positive) are essential for diagnosis. Dysplasia can be low‑ or high‑grade.

Esophageal Tumors
Squamous Cell Carcinoma

Risk factors: Smoking, alcohol, achalasia, caustic stricture, Plummer‑Vinson syndrome, HPV (subset).

Location: Upper and middle third. May be polypoid, ulcerative, or infiltrative.

Adenocarcinoma

Risk factors: Barrett esophagus (major), GERD, obesity, smoking. Arises in lower third.

Pathology: Often arises from dysplastic Barrett mucosa. May be mucin‑producing.

Both types present with progressive dysphagia, weight loss. Diagnosis by endoscopy + biopsy.

2. Gastritis
Acute Gastritis

Etiology: Stress (shock, burns, trauma), NSAIDs, alcohol, bile reflux. Disruption of mucosal barrier → back‑diffusion of acid.

Pathology: Superficial erosions, hemorrhage, neutrophil infiltration.

Chronic Gastritis

Types:

  • H. pylori gastritis: Most common. Organisms adhere to surface epithelium → urease produces ammonia → mucosal injury, chronic inflammation (lymphocytes, plasma cells), lymphoid follicles.
  • Autoimmune gastritis: Antibodies against parietal cells and intrinsic factor → atrophic gastritis, achlorhydria, hypergastrinemia, pernicious anemia. Body and fundus involved.
  • Chemical gastropathy: Bile reflux, NSAIDs – foveolar hyperplasia, edema, no significant inflammation.

3. Peptic Ulcer Disease

Definition: Breach in mucosa extending through muscularis mucosae into submucosa or deeper.

Etiology
  • H. pylori infection (70% gastric, 90% duodenal) – disrupts mucosal defenses, increases gastrin.
  • NSAIDs – inhibit COX → decreased prostaglandins → reduced mucus and blood flow.
  • Zollinger‑Ellison syndrome – gastrinoma → massive acid → ulcers in atypical locations.
Pathology

Round, punched‑out ulcer with clean base (if chronic) or necrotic slough. Histology: zone of necrosis, inflammation, granulation tissue, fibrosis. Duodenal ulcers almost always benign; gastric ulcers require biopsy to rule out malignancy.

Complications
  • Bleeding (most common) – hematemesis, melena.
  • Perforation – acute abdomen, free air under diaphragm.
  • Penetration – into pancreas, liver.
  • Obstruction – due to edema/fibrosis near pylorus.
4. Gastric Tumors
Gastric Adenocarcinoma

Epidemiology: Most common gastric malignancy. Incidence declining in West (due to decreased H. pylori? improved diet).

Risk factors: H. pylori, smoking, diet high in smoked/salted foods, pernicious anemia, prior gastric surgery.

Lauren classification:

  • Intestinal type: Gland‑forming, associated with intestinal metaplasia, H. pylori. More common in men, older age.
  • Diffuse type: Poorly cohesive (signet‑ring cells), infiltrative (limits plastica), younger age, female. Associated with E‑cadherin (CDH1) mutations.

Spread: Direct extension, lymphatics, hematogenous (liver, lung), peritoneal seeding (Krukenberg tumor – ovarian metastasis).

Gastric Lymphoma

Most common extranodal lymphoma. MALToma (low‑grade B cell) strongly associated with H. pylori – often regresses after eradication.

Gastrointestinal Stromal Tumor (GIST)

Originates from interstitial cells of Cajal. Mutations in c‑KIT (CD117) or PDGFRA. Can be benign or malignant. Histology: spindle cells, epithelioid cells. Treated with tyrosine kinase inhibitors (imatinib).

Carcinoid Tumor

Neuroendocrine tumor, often in stomach (type I – autoimmune gastritis, type II – MEN1, type III – sporadic aggressive).

5. Small Intestine Pathology
Celiac Disease (Gluten‑Sensitive Enteropathy)

Mechanism: Autoimmune reaction to gluten (gliadin) in HLA‑DQ2/DQ8 individuals. Deamidation by tissue transglutaminase (tTG) enhances T‑cell response.

Pathology: Villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, increased intraepithelial lymphocytes. Marsh classification.

Clinical: Malabsorption, diarrhea, bloating, anemia. Associated with dermatitis herpetiformis. Diagnosis: anti‑tTG antibodies, biopsy.

Tropical Sprue

Chronic diarrhea, malabsorption in tropical regions, possibly infectious. Responds to tetracycline and folate.

Whipple Disease

Etiology: Tropheryma whipplei (gram‑positive actinobacterium).

Pathology: PAS‑positive foamy macrophages in lamina propria containing bacteria.

Clinical: Arthralgias, weight loss, diarrhea, neurological symptoms. Treated with antibiotics.

7. Colorectal Polyps & Cancer
Colonic Polyps
  • Hyperplastic polyp: Serrated architecture, no dysplasia, no malignant potential.
  • Adenomatous polyp (tubular, villous, tubulovillous): Dysplastic epithelium. Villous component increases risk of carcinoma. APC mutation in familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP).
  • Sessile serrated polyp: May progress to carcinoma via microsatellite instability (MSI).
Colorectal Carcinoma

Molecular pathways:

  • APC/β‑catenin pathway: Most common (85%). Chromosomal instability (CIN). FAP syndrome.
  • Microsatellite instability (MSI): Defective mismatch repair (MLH1, MSH2). Hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer (HNPCC / Lynch syndrome).
  • Serrated pathway: BRAF mutation, CpG island methylation.

Staging: Dukes (A‑C) and TNM. Prognosis correlates with depth of invasion, nodal status, metastasis.

8. Appendicitis & Appendiceal Tumors
Acute Appendicitis

Etiology: Luminal obstruction (fecalith, lymphoid hyperplasia, tumor) → increased pressure → ischemia → bacterial overgrowth → inflammation.

Pathology: Neutrophilic infiltration, ulceration, gangrene, perforation.

Appendiceal Tumors
  • Neuroendocrine tumor (carcinoid): Most common, often tip, usually benign.
  • Mucinous neoplasms: Can rupture and cause pseudomyxoma peritonei.

Autoimmune Hepatitis

Female predominance, hypergammaglobulinemia, autoantibodies (ANA, ASMA, anti‑LKM). Interface hepatitis (piecemeal necrosis), plasma cell infiltration. Responds to steroids.

10. Cirrhosis & Portal Hypertension

Definition: Diffuse hepatic fibrosis with nodule formation, disrupting liver architecture.

Etiology: Alcohol, viral hepatitis (B, C), NAFLD, autoimmune, biliary, hemochromatosis, Wilson, α1‑antitrypsin deficiency.

Pathology: Micronodular (alcohol) → macronodular (post‑necrotic). Fibrous septa, regenerative nodules.

Complications
  • Portal hypertension (varices, splenomegaly, ascites).
  • Hepatic encephalopathy (ammonia).
  • Hepatorenal syndrome.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma.
11. Liver Tumors
Benign
  • Hemangioma: Most common, cavernous vascular spaces.
  • Hepatocellular adenoma: Oral contraceptive use. May rupture or undergo malignant transformation.
Malignant
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC): Associated with cirrhosis, HBV, HCV, aflatoxin. Histology: trabecular, pseudoglandular. Serum AFP elevated.
  • Cholangiocarcinoma: Bile duct cancer; risk factors: PSC, liver flukes (Clonorchis, Opisthorchis).
  • Metastases: Most common liver malignancy (colon, lung, breast).
12. Gallbladder & Bile Duct Pathology
Cholelithiasis (Gallstones)
  • Cholesterol stones: Most common (Western). Due to supersaturation of bile, decreased bile salts, gallbladder hypomotility.
  • Pigment stones: Black (hemolysis, cirrhosis) or brown (infection, stasis).
Cholecystitis

Acute: Usually due to impacted stone → inflammation, edema, neutrophilic infiltration. Can progress to gangrene, perforation.

Chronic: Repeated attacks → thickened wall, Rokitansky‑Aschoff sinuses, lymphocytic infiltration.

Carcinoma of Gallbladder

Strongly associated with gallstones (80%), porcelain gallbladder (calcified wall). Most common in fundus. Usually adenocarcinoma, poor prognosis.

13. Pancreatitis
Acute Pancreatitis

Etiology: Gallstones (most common), alcohol, trauma, ERCP, drugs, infections, hypertriglyceridemia, hypercalcemia.

Mechanism: Premature activation of trypsinogen within acinar cells → autodigestion.

Pathology: Fat necrosis (saponification), hemorrhage, neutrophilic infiltration. Peripancreatic fat necrosis (calcium soaps).

Complications: Pseudocyst (walled‑off fluid collection), abscess, necrosis, ARDS, DIC.

Chronic Pancreatitis

Prolonged alcohol abuse (most common). Fibrosis, atrophy of acini, calcifications (pancreatic stones), chronic inflammation. Leads to exocrine and endocrine insufficiency.

14. Pancreatic Tumors
Ductal Adenocarcinoma

Most common (85%). Risk factors: smoking, chronic pancreatitis, diabetes. Head of pancreas (60‑70%) → obstructive jaundice (painless). Poor prognosis. Histology: glands embedded in desmoplastic stroma. Mutations: KRAS (90%), p53, SMAD4.

Neuroendocrine Tumors
  • Insulinoma: Whipple triad (hypoglycemia, symptoms with fasting, relief with glucose).
  • Gastrinoma: Zollinger‑Ellison syndrome (peptic ulcers, diarrhea). Often in duodenum.
  • VIPoma: Watery diarrhea, hypokalemia, achlorhydria (WDHA).
  • Glucagonoma: Diabetes, necrolytic migratory erythema.
  • Somatostatinoma: Diabetes, gallstones, steatorrhea.
15. Peritoneal Pathology
Peritonitis

Primary (spontaneous bacterial): Usually in cirrhotic ascites, single organism (E. coli, pneumococcus).

Secondary: Perforation of viscus (appendicitis, diverticulitis, ulcer) → polymicrobial (enteric flora).

Pseudomyxoma Peritonei

Mucinous ascites due to rupture of mucinous neoplasm (appendix, ovary). Neoplastic cells produce abundant mucin.

Peritoneal Metastases

Common from ovarian, gastric, colon cancer. May cause malignant ascites.
