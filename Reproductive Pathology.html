1. Disorders of Sexual Development

Disorders of sexual development (DSD) result from chromosomal, gonadal, or phenotypic abnormalities.

Klinefelter Syndrome (47,XXY)
  • Male phenotype with small firm testes, gynecomastia, tall stature, infertility, increased gonadotropins.
  • Pathology: Testicular atrophy, hyalinization of seminiferous tubules, Leydig cell hyperplasia.
Turner Syndrome (45,XO)
  • Female phenotype with streak ovaries, short stature, webbed neck, coarctation of aorta.
  • Pathology: Ovarian streaks – fibrous tissue without follicles.
XXY (Klinefelter) variant
Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (AIS)
  • Complete AIS: 46,XY, female phenotype, testes present, absent uterus, blind‑ending vagina. Caused by androgen receptor mutation.
  • Pathology: Testes may be intra‑abdominal; increased risk of germ cell tumors.
5α‑Reductase Deficiency
  • 46,XY, ambiguous genitalia at birth, virilization at puberty. Testosterone normal, DHT low.

Clinical pearl: In AIS, testes produce anti‑Müllerian hormone (regression of uterus) and testosterone (but no effect due to receptor defect).

2. Testicular & Scrotal Pathology
Cryptorchidism
  • Undescended testis; risk of infertility and germ cell tumors (seminoma).
Testicular Torsion
  • Twisting of spermatic cord → ischemia → infarction; emergency.
Orchitis
  • Often viral (mumps) or bacterial (epididymitis).
    • Testicular Tumors
    • Seminoma: Most common; cells resemble primordial germ cells; radiosensitive.
    • Embryonal carcinoma: Aggressive, solid/cystic, elevated AFP and hCG.
    • Yolk sac tumor: Most common in children; Schiller‑Duval bodies; elevated AFP.
    • Choriocarcinoma: Hemorrhagic; elevated hCG; poor prognosis.
    • Teratoma: Differentiated tissues (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm).
    • Mixed germ cell tumors: Common.
    • Leydig cell tumor: Secretes androgens/estrogens; may cause gynecomastia.
    • Sertoli cell tumor: Rare; may cause gynecomastia.

    Clinical pearl: Tumor markers: AFP (yolk sac, embryonal), hCG (choriocarcinoma, syncytiotrophoblast).

    3. Ovarian Pathology
    Non‑neoplastic Cysts
    • Follicular cyst: Thin‑walled, estrogenic; may cause precocious puberty.
    • Corpus luteum cyst: May cause pain or rupture.
    • Theca lutein cyst: Associated with high hCG (molar pregnancy, multiple gestation).
    • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS): Bilateral enlarged ovaries with multiple subcortical cysts; hyperandrogenism, oligo‑anovulation, insulin resistance.
    Ovarian Tumors (Surface Epithelial)
    • Serous cystadenoma: Benign; lined by ciliated epithelium.
    • Serous cystadenocarcinoma: Most common malignant ovarian tumor; psammoma bodies.
    • Mucinous cystadenoma: Large, multilocular; benign.
    • Mucinous cystadenocarcinoma: Pseudomyxoma peritonei possible.
    • Endometrioid adenocarcinoma: Associated with endometriosis.
    Sex Cord‑Stromal Tumors
    • Granulosa cell tumor: Secretes estrogen; may cause precocious puberty or postmenopausal bleeding; Call‑Exner bodies.
    • Thecoma: Benign, estrogenic.
    • Fibroma: Benign; may cause Meigs syndrome (ascites, pleural effusion).
    • Sertoli‑Leydig cell tumor: Androgenic (virilization).
    Germ Cell Tumors
    • Dysgerminoma: Ovarian counterpart of seminoma; radiosensitive.
    • Yolk sac tumor: AFP+, Schiller‑Duval bodies.
    • Choriocarcinoma: hCG+.
    • Teratoma: Mature (cystic) – benign; immature – malignant.
    • Struma ovarii: Monodermal teratoma with thyroid tissue; may cause hyperthyroidism.

    Clinical pearl: Mature cystic teratoma (dermoid cyst) contains hair, teeth, sebum.

    4. Uterine & Endometrial Pathology
    Endometrial Pathology
    • Endometritis: Acute (postpartum) or chronic (TB, Chlamydia).
    • Endometrial hyperplasia: Due to unopposed estrogen; risk of progression to carcinoma.
    • Endometrial polyp: Focal hyperplasia of glands and stroma.
    • Endometrial carcinoma: Type I (endometrioid) – estrogen‑related; Type II (serous) – aggressive, p53 mutations.
    Myometrial Pathology
    • Leiomyoma (fibroid): Benign smooth muscle tumor; estrogen‑sensitive; may cause bleeding, pain.
    • Leiomyosarcoma: Malignant; rare.
    Endometriosis & Adenomyosis
    • Endometriosis: Endometrial glands/stroma outside uterus; causes pain, infertility.
    • Adenomyosis: Endometrial tissue within myometrium; causes menorrhagia, dysmenorrhea.

    Clinical pearl: Tamoxifen (SERM) increases risk of endometrial hyperplasia and carcinoma due to estrogenic effect on uterus.

    5. Cervical Pathology
    Cervicitis
    • Acute (gonorrhea, Chlamydia) or chronic (lymphocytes, plasma cells).
    Cervical Intraepithelial Neoplasia (CIN)
    • HPV‑induced dysplasia (koilocytosis). Graded CIN I‑III.
    • Risk factors: HPV 16,18, early sexual activity, smoking, immunosuppression.
    Cervical Carcinoma
    • Squamous cell carcinoma: Most common (80%). Arises from transformation zone.
    • Adenocarcinoma: Less common, HPV‑related.

    Clinical pearl: Pap smear screens for cervical dysplasia; HPV vaccine prevents HPV 16/18.

    6. Vaginal & Vulvar Pathology
    • Bartholin cyst: Obstruction of Bartholin gland duct.
    • Vulvar dystrophy: Lichen sclerosus, squamous hyperplasia.
    • Vulvar intraepithelial neoplasia (VIN): HPV‑related.
    • Squamous cell carcinoma of vulva: Often HPV‑related in younger; keratinizing type in elderly.
    • Vaginal adenosis: Presence of glandular epithelium in vagina; associated with DES exposure in utero.
    • Clear cell adenocarcinoma of vagina: Rare, linked to DES.
    7. Placental Pathology
    • Placenta previa: Placenta overlies internal os.
    • Placental abruption: Premature separation of placenta.
    • Placenta accreta/increta/percreta: Abnormal adherence to myometrium.
    • Infarcts: May indicate maternal vascular disease.
    • Chorioamnionitis: Inflammation of fetal membranes; often ascending infection.
    8. Breast Pathology
    Benign Breast Disease
    • Fibrocystic changes: Fibrosis, cysts, apocrine metaplasia, adenosis; risk of carcinoma if proliferative with atypia.
    • Intraductal papilloma: May cause nipple discharge.
    • Fibroadenoma: Common benign tumor in young women; biphasic (stroma + glands).
    • Phyllodes tumor: Leaf‑like stromal overgrowth; can be malignant.
    • Gynecomastia: Male breast enlargement; ductal hyperplasia (no acini).
    Breast Carcinoma
    • Ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS): Proliferation of malignant cells within ducts; comedo type has high risk.
    • Lobular carcinoma in situ (LCIS): Marker of increased risk; often incidental.
    • Invasive ductal carcinoma (IDC): Most common (70‑80%); glandular formation, desmoplasia.
    • Invasive lobular carcinoma (ILC): Single‑file infiltration; loss of E‑cadherin.
    • Medullary carcinoma: Lymphocytic infiltrate, pushing borders; better prognosis.
    • Mucinous (colloid) carcinoma: Extracellular mucin; better prognosis.
    • Paget disease of nipple: Tumor cells in epidermis; associated with underlying DCIS/IDC.
    • Inflammatory breast cancer: Dermal lymphatic invasion; peau d'orange.
    Hormone Receptors & Prognosis
    • ER/PR positivity predicts response to endocrine therapy.
    • HER2/neu amplification (trastuzumab target).
    • Triple‑negative (ER‑, PR‑, HER2‑) – aggressive.

    Clinical pearl: BRCA1/BRCA2 mutations increase risk of breast and ovarian cancer.

    9. Reproductive Infections
    • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID): Often gonorrhea/Chlamydia; may cause tubal scarring, infertility, ectopic pregnancy.
    • Syphilis: Treponema pallidum – chancre, rash, gummas.
    • Herpes simplex: Genital ulcers; neonatal herpes risk.
    • HPV: Condylomata acuminata (low‑risk); CIN/cancer (high‑risk).
    • Chancroid: Haemophilus ducreyi – painful ulcer, bubo.
    • Lymphogranuloma venereum (LGV): Chlamydia trachomatis L1‑L3 – lymphadenitis, proctocolitis.
    • Granuloma inguinale (donovanosis): Klebsiella granulomatis – painless ulcer, Donovan bodies.
    10. Gestational Trophoblastic Disease
    • Hydatidiform mole:
      • Complete mole: 46,XX (all paternal); no fetal tissue; diffuse villous swelling, trophoblastic proliferation; high hCG; risk of choriocarcinoma.
      • Partial mole: Triploid (69,XXX); some fetal tissue; focal villous hydrops; low risk of malignancy.
    • Invasive mole: Invades myometrium; may metastasize.
    • Choriocarcinoma: Malignant (cyto‑ and syncytiotrophoblast); hematogenous spread; high hCG.
    • Placental site trophoblastic tumor: Rare, intermediate trophoblast.

    Clinical pearl: Complete mole: no fetal parts, theca lutein cysts, hyperemesis, preeclampsia before 20 weeks.
