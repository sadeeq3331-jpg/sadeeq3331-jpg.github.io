1. Physical Growth
Patterns of Growth: Growth velocity is highest in infancy and during puberty. Weight, length/height, and head circumference are key parameters.
  • Weight: Birth weight doubles by 5 months, triples by 1 year, quadruples by 2 years. Average birth weight ~3.5 kg.
  • Length: Increases by 50% in first year (average birth length 50 cm → 75 cm at 1 year).
  • Head circumference: Increases rapidly in first year (35 cm at birth → 47 cm at 1 year). Reflects brain growth.
Rule of thumb: weight at 1 year = 3× birth weight; at 2 years = 4×.

3. Nutrition & Feeding
  • Breastfeeding: Recommended exclusively for first 6 months. Contains IgA, lactoferrin, lysozyme, oligosaccharides.
  • Formula: Cow‑milk based, iron‑fortified. Standard 20 kcal/oz.
  • Caloric requirements: Infants ~100‑120 kcal/kg/day, decreases with age.
  • Vitamin supplements: Vitamin D (400 IU/day) for breastfed infants; iron supplementation after 4‑6 months if exclusively breastfed.
  • Introduction of solids: Around 6 months; first foods iron‑fortified cereals, then pureed fruits/vegetables.
4. Fetal to Neonatal Transition
At birth, critical changes occur:
  • Pulmonary: First breath → lung expansion → decreased pulmonary vascular resistance → increased pulmonary blood flow.
  • Cardiovascular: Closure of foramen ovale (increased left atrial pressure), ductus arteriosus (increased O2, decreased PGE), ductus venosus.
  • Endocrine: Surge in catecholamines, cortisol, thyroid hormones.

Persistent pulmonary hypertension of newborn (PPHN): Failure of pulmonary vascular resistance to decrease → right‑to‑left shunting through PDA/PFO → hypoxemia.

5. Neonatal Respiratory System
  • Surfactant: Produced by type II pneumocytes from ~24 weeks, sufficient by 34‑36 weeks. Deficiency causes respiratory distress syndrome (RDS).
  • Lung volumes: Higher respiratory rate (30‑60/min), tidal volume ~6 mL/kg, dead space proportionally larger.
  • Oxygen consumption: Higher per kg than adults.
6. Neonatal Cardiovascular System
  • Heart rate: 120‑160 bpm at rest.
  • Blood pressure: Increases with age; at birth ~60/40 mmHg, reaches 90/60 by 1 month.
  • Fetal shunts: Functional closure of ductus arteriosus occurs by 24‑48 hours (permanent fibrosis by 2‑3 weeks). Foramen ovale closes when left atrial pressure exceeds right.
7. Neonatal Renal Function
  • Glomerular filtration rate (GFR): Low at birth (~20 mL/min/1.73m²), doubles by 2 weeks, reaches adult levels by 1‑2 years.
  • Concentrating ability: Limited (max urine osmolality ~500 mOsm/kg) due to immaturity of loop of Henle and low medullary tonicity.
  • Sodium handling: Preterm infants may waste sodium; term infants generally conserve well.
8. Neonatal Hepatic Function
  • Bilirubin metabolism: Immature UDP‑glucuronosyltransferase → physiological jaundice (unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia) peaks at 3‑5 days.
  • Clotting factors: Vitamin K dependent factors low at birth → vitamin K prophylaxis to prevent hemorrhagic disease.
  • Glycogen stores: Limited; risk of hypoglycemia.
9. Respiratory System
  • Lung development continues until early adulthood (alveoli increase until 2‑8 years).
  • Infants are preferential nose breathers (until 4‑6 months).
  • Smaller airways → more susceptible to obstruction (e.g., bronchiolitis).
10. Cardiovascular System
  • Cardiac output is heart‑rate dependent (limited stroke volume reserve).
  • Myocardial compliance is lower, so preload reserve limited.
  • Blood pressure norms increase with age.
11. Renal System
  • GFR increases with age; formula for estimating creatinine clearance in children: Schwartz formula (eGFR = k × height / serum creatinine).
  • Acid excretion limited → prone to metabolic acidosis.
12. Gastrointestinal System
  • Gastric acid secretion low at birth, increases by 1 month.
  • Pancreatic enzymes (lipase, amylase) mature by 6‑9 months.
  • Liver glycogen stores limited; gluconeogenesis immature.
13. Endocrine System
  • Growth hormone: High levels in infancy; pulsatile secretion; mediates linear growth.
  • Thyroid: TSH surge at birth; thyroxine essential for brain development.
  • Puberty: Gonadarche (activation of HPG axis) and adrenarche (adrenal androgens).
14. Immune System
  • Passive immunity from maternal IgG crosses placenta (mainly third trimester). IgA in breast milk.
  • Neonatal T cells are naïve, Th2‑skewed; impaired Th1 responses.
  • Complement levels lower than adult.
  • Delayed response to polysaccharide antigens (<2 years explains susceptibility to encapsulated bacteria).
15. Nervous System
  • Brain weight doubles by 1 year, triples by 3 years.
  • Synaptogenesis and myelination continue through childhood.
  • Primitive reflexes (Moro, rooting, grasp) disappear by 4‑6 months.

17. Electrolyte Requirements
  • Sodium: 2‑4 mEq/kg/day
  • Potassium: 1‑3 mEq/kg/day
  • Chloride: 2‑4 mEq/kg/day

Maintenance IV fluids typically D5 1/4NS or D5 1/2NS with added KCl (except neonates).
