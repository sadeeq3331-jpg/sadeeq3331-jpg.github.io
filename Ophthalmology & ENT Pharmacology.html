1. Glaucoma Drugs
Glaucoma medications lower intraocular pressure (IOP) either by decreasing aqueous production or increasing outflow.
ClassExamplesMechanismAdverse Effects
Prostaglandin analogsLatanoprost, travoprost, bimatoprost, tafluprostIncrease uveoscleral outflowIris hyperpigmentation, eyelash growth, periocular skin darkening, conjunctival hyperemia
Beta‑blockersTimolol, betaxolol (cardioselective)Decrease aqueous productionBradycardia, bronchospasm, hypotension, fatigue (contraindicated in asthma, COPD, heart block)
Alpha‑2 agonistsBrimonidine, apraclonidineDecrease production + increase uveoscleral outflowOcular allergy, dry mouth, fatigue, hypotension (apraclonidine used for post‑laser IOP spike)
Carbonic anhydrase inhibitorsDorzolamide, brinzolamide (topical); acetazolamide, methazolamide (oral)Inhibit carbonic anhydrase in ciliary epithelium → ↓ aqueousTopical: bitter taste, stinging. Oral: paresthesias, metabolic acidosis, sulfa allergy
Miotics (cholinergic agonists)Pilocarpine, carbacholContract ciliary muscle → open trabecular meshworkMiosis, brow ache, induced myopia, retinal detachment risk
Rho kinase inhibitorsNetarsudilIncrease trabecular outflowConjunctival hyperemia, corneal deposits

First‑line: Prostaglandin analogs (once daily, evening) are often first‑line for open‑angle glaucoma. Beta‑blockers are also first‑line but have systemic side effects.

5. Ocular Antifungals
  • Natamycin 5%: Topical for fungal keratitis (Aspergillus, Fusarium) – first‑line.
  • Amphotericin B (0.15% topical or intravitreal): Candida, Aspergillus.
  • Voriconazole (topical or intravitreal): Broad‑spectrum, good penetration.
  • Fluconazole (oral): Candida endophthalmitis.
6. Ocular Corticosteroids
  • Prednisolone acetate 1%: Potent, used for uveitis, post‑operative inflammation.
  • Dexamethasone (drops, ointment, implant): Potent, used for inflammation, also in otic/ophthalmic combinations.
  • Fluorometholone: Less potent, less IOP elevation; used for milder inflammation.
  • Loteprednol etabonate: Designed to have lower IOP elevation; used for allergic conjunctivitis, post‑op.

Side effects: Increased IOP, cataract formation, delayed wound healing, secondary infection. Contraindicated in active HSV keratitis (unless with antiviral coverage).

7. Ocular NSAIDs
  • Ketorolac, diclofenac, bromfenac, nepafenac: Inhibit cyclooxygenase, reduce prostaglandin synthesis.
  • Uses: Allergic conjunctivitis, post‑operative inflammation, cystoid macular edema prophylaxis, pain relief after refractive surgery.
  • Side effects: Stinging, corneal complications with prolonged use.
8. Anti‑Allergy Agents
  • Antihistamines (topical): Emedastine, levocabastine – block H1 receptors.
  • Mast cell stabilizers: Cromolyn, lodoxamide – prevent degranulation (prophylactic).
  • Dual‑action agents: Olopatadine, ketotifen, azelastine, epinastine – antihistamine + mast cell stabilization. First‑line for allergic conjunctivitis.
  • NSAIDs (ketorolac): Also used for allergic conjunctivitis.
9. Dry Eye Treatments
  • Artificial tears (hypromellose, carboxymethylcellulose, hyaluronic acid): Lubricants, temporary relief.
  • Cyclosporine A (Restasis): Calcineurin inhibitor, reduces T‑cell mediated inflammation; increases tear production.
  • Lifitegrast (Xiidra): LFA‑1 antagonist, inhibits T‑cell activation.
  • Autologous serum tears: For severe dry eye.
  • Punctal plugs: Mechanical occlusion of tear drainage.
10. Anti‑VEGF Agents
  • Bevacizumab (Avastin): Monoclonal antibody, used off‑label intravitreally.
  • Ranibizumab (Lucentis): Fab fragment, approved for wet AMD, DME, RVO.
  • Aflibercept (Eylea): VEGF Trap, binds multiple VEGF isoforms.
  • Brolucizumab (Beovu): Single‑chain antibody fragment.

Intravitreal injection; side effects: endophthalmitis, retinal detachment, increased IOP, stroke risk (rare).

11. Nasal Decongestants
  • Topical (α‑agonists): Oxymetazoline, xylometazoline, phenylephrine. Cause vasoconstriction. Use ≤3 days to avoid rhinitis medicamentosa (rebound congestion).
  • Oral (α‑agonists): Pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine. Systemic effects: hypertension, tachycardia, insomnia. Contraindicated with MAOIs.
12. Intranasal Corticosteroids
  • Examples: Fluticasone propionate, fluticasone furoate, mometasone furoate, budesonide, beclomethasone, ciclesonide.
  • Uses: Allergic rhinitis (first‑line), nasal polyps, sinusitis.
  • Mechanism: Suppress inflammation, reduce cytokine release, decrease eosinophils.
  • Side effects: Epistaxis, nasal irritation, septal perforation (rare). Minimal systemic absorption.

14. Mast Cell Stabilizers
  • Cromolyn sodium (intranasal): Prevents mast cell degranulation; requires regular use for prophylaxis. Minimal side effects.
15. Leukotriene Antagonists
  • Montelukast: Blocks cysteinyl leukotriene receptors; used in allergic rhinitis and asthma. Side effects: neuropsychiatric events (rare).

17. Antivertigo Drugs
  • Antihistamines (vestibular suppressants): Meclizine, dimenhydrinate, promethazine – block H1 receptors, anticholinergic.
  • Benzodiazepines: Diazepam, clonazepam – enhance GABA, suppress vestibular responses.
  • Betahistine: Histamine analog, used in Meniere's (improves microcirculation, reduces endolymph pressure).
  • Scopolamine (transdermal): Anticholinergic, for motion sickness.
  • Antiemetics (ondansetron, prochlorperazine): Symptomatic for nausea.

19. Salivary Gland Drugs
  • Sialogogues: Pilocarpine, cevimeline – muscarinic agonists, stimulate saliva in xerostomia (Sjögren's, post‑radiation). Side effects: sweating, flushing, bradycardia.
  • Antibiotics for sialadenitis: Dicloxacillin, clindamycin (cover Staph aureus).
