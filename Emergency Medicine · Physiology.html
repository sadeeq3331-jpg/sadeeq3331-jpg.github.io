1. Cardiac Arrest Physiology
Cardiac arrest is the cessation of effective cardiac mechanical function, leading to absence of circulation and global ischemia.
  • Mechanisms: Ventricular fibrillation (VF), pulseless ventricular tachycardia (VT), asystole, pulseless electrical activity (PEA).
  • Time course: Irreversible brain damage begins after 4‑6 minutes of normothermic arrest.
  • Ischemia‑reperfusion injury: Restoration of flow triggers oxidative stress, inflammation, and apoptosis.

Phases of Cardiac Arrest

  • Electrical phase (0‑4 min): Defibrillation most effective.
  • Circulatory phase (4‑10 min): CPR essential to perfuse heart and brain.
  • Metabolic phase (>10 min): Global ischemia, metabolic acidosis; post‑resuscitation care critical.
2. CPR Physiology
  • Cardiac pump theory: Direct compression of heart between sternum and spine.
  • Thoracic pump theory: Increased intrathoracic pressure propels blood; valves prevent backflow.
  • Optimal compression depth: ≥2 inches (5 cm) at 100‑120/min.
  • Coronary perfusion pressure (CPP) = aortic diastolic – right atrial pressure. CPP >15 mmHg predicts ROSC.
  • Ventilations during CPR: minimize interruptions; 30:2 ratio (adult) or continuous compressions with asynchronous ventilation after advanced airway.
3. Post‑Cardiac Arrest Syndrome
  • Brain injury: Cerebral edema, impaired autoregulation, seizures.
  • Myocardial dysfunction: Stunning, low cardiac output.
  • Systemic ischemia/reperfusion: SIRS‑like response, multi‑organ dysfunction.
  • Targeted temperature management (TTM): 33‑36°C for 24h to reduce neurologic injury.
4. Shock Physiology
Shock is defined as inadequate tissue perfusion to meet metabolic demands.

Stages of shock:
  • Early (compensated): Tachycardia, vasoconstriction, normal BP.
  • Intermediate (decompensated): Hypotension, oliguria, altered mental status.
  • Late (irreversible): Multi‑organ failure, refractory hypotension.
5. Acute Coronary Syndromes
  • Pathophysiology: Plaque rupture → thrombus formation → myocardial ischemia.
  • Types: Unstable angina, NSTEMI, STEMI.
  • Determinants of ischemia: Supply (coronary flow, oxygen content) vs demand (HR, contractility, wall stress).
  • Biomarkers: Troponin I/T (most specific), CK‑MB, myoglobin.
  • ECG changes: ST elevation (transmural ischemia), ST depression (subendocardial), T wave inversion.
6. Cardiac Arrhythmias
Bradyarrhythmias
  • Sinus bradycardia: Rate <60; may be physiologic or pathologic (hypoxia, MI, drugs).
  • AV blocks: First‑degree (PR >200 ms), second‑degree Mobitz I (Wenckebach), Mobitz II (constant PR with dropped beats), third‑degree (complete heart block).
Tachyarrhythmias
  • Narrow complex (SVT): AV nodal reentry, AV reentry (WPW), atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter.
  • Wide complex: VT, VF, SVT with aberrancy.
  • Ventricular fibrillation: Chaotic electrical activity; requires immediate defibrillation.

Unstable patient: Synchronized cardioversion for unstable tachyarrhythmias; defibrillation for VF/pulseless VT.

7. Hypertensive Emergencies
  • Hypertensive emergency: Severe BP elevation (>180/120) with acute target organ damage (brain, heart, kidneys).
  • Pathophysiology: Failed autoregulation → end‑organ ischemia, endothelial injury, fibrinoid necrosis.
  • Manifestations: Hypertensive encephalopathy, intracranial hemorrhage, acute MI, pulmonary edema, renal failure.
  • Treatment: IV antihypertensives (labetalol, nicardipine, nitroprusside) with controlled reduction (not >25% in first hour).
8. Aortic Dissection & Aneurysm
  • Aortic dissection: Intimal tear allows blood to propagate within media, creating false lumen.
  • Stanford classification: Type A (ascending) – surgical emergency; Type B (descending) – medical management.
  • Pathophysiology: Hypertension, connective tissue disorders (Marfan, Ehlers‑Danlos).
  • Complications: Rupture, tamponade, aortic regurgitation, myocardial infarction (coronary ostia), stroke.
9. Airway Management
  • Anatomy: Oral cavity, pharynx, larynx (vocal cords), trachea.
  • Assessment: Look for signs of obstruction (stridor, drooling, paradoxical breathing).
  • Basic maneuvers: Head tilt‑chin lift, jaw thrust, oral/nasal airways.
  • Advanced airway: Endotracheal intubation (ETT), supraglottic devices (LMA), surgical airway (cricothyrotomy).
  • Rapid sequence intubation (RSI): Sedative + paralytic to facilitate intubation; pre‑oxygenation critical.
10. Respiratory Failure
  • Type I (hypoxemic): PaO₂ <60 mmHg, normal/low PaCO₂. Causes: V/Q mismatch, shunt, diffusion impairment.
  • Type II (hypercapnic): PaCO₂ >50 mmHg. Causes: hypoventilation, increased dead space.
  • ARDS: Acute inflammatory lung injury → increased permeability, non‑cardiogenic pulmonary edema, refractory hypoxemia.
  • Oxygen therapy: Goal SpO₂ 88‑92% (COPD) or 94‑98% (others).
11. Pulmonary Embolism
  • Pathophysiology: Thrombus (usually from DVT) occludes pulmonary artery → increased dead space, right ventricular afterload.
  • Massive PE: Hypotension, shock, RV failure.
  • Physiologic effects: ↑ pulmonary vascular resistance, ↓ cardiac output, V/Q mismatch, hypoxemia.
  • Diagnosis: CTPA, V/Q scan, D‑dimer (high sensitivity, low specificity).
12. Asthma & COPD
  • Asthma: Reversible airway obstruction, inflammation, hyperresponsiveness.
  • COPD: Chronic airflow limitation (emphysema, chronic bronchitis).
  • Acute exacerbation triggers: Infection, allergens, irritants.
  • Physiologic changes: Air trapping, hyperinflation, increased work of breathing, pulsus paradoxus.
  • Status asthmaticus: Severe, refractory bronchospasm; can progress to respiratory failure.
13. Stroke & TIA
  • Ischemic stroke: Thrombotic, embolic, or hypoperfusion. Penumbra (ischemic but salvageable tissue).
  • Hemorrhagic stroke: Intracerebral hemorrhage, subarachnoid hemorrhage.
  • Time is brain: 1.9 million neurons lost per minute.
  • NIHSS: Quantifies stroke severity.
  • tPA window: Within 3‑4.5 hours for eligible patients.
14. Intracranial Hemorrhage
  • Epidural hematoma: Arterial (middle meningeal) – lucid interval, lens‑shaped.
  • Subdural hematoma: Venous (bridging veins) – crescent‑shaped, often in elderly.
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage: Aneurysm rupture → sudden thunderclap headache.
  • Intracerebral hemorrhage: Hypertension, amyloid angiopathy.
15. Seizures & Status Epilepticus
  • Generalized tonic‑clonic: Loss of consciousness, tonic phase (rigidity) followed by clonic phase (rhythmic jerking).
  • Status epilepticus: Continuous seizure >5 min or recurrent without recovery. Medical emergency.
  • Complications: Hypoxia, lactic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, hyperthermia, neuronal injury.
  • Treatment: Benzodiazepines (first‑line), then second‑line agents (fosphenytoin, levetiracetam, valproate).
16. Altered Mental Status
  • Causes: AEIOU TIPS (Alcohol, Epilepsy, Insulin, Overdose, Uremia, Trauma, Infection, Psychiatric, Syncope).
  • Delirium: Acute, fluctuating, inattention, disorganized thinking.
  • Dementia: Chronic, progressive cognitive decline.
  • Coma: Unarousable unresponsiveness.
17. Acid‑Base Disorders
  • Respiratory acidosis: Hypoventilation → ↑ PaCO₂, ↓ pH.
  • Respiratory alkalosis: Hyperventilation → ↓ PaCO₂, ↑ pH.
  • Metabolic acidosis: ↓ HCO₃, ↓ pH. Anion gap = Na – (Cl + HCO₃). Normal gap (8‑12) vs high gap (MUD PILES).
  • Metabolic alkalosis: ↑ HCO₃, ↑ pH. Causes: vomiting, diuretics.
  • Winter's formula: PaCO₂ = 1.5 × [HCO₃] + 8 ± 2 (predicts appropriate respiratory compensation in metabolic acidosis).
18. Electrolyte Emergencies
  • Hyperkalemia: ECG: peaked T, wide QRS, sine wave. Treatment: calcium, insulin+glucose, albuterol, kayexalate.
  • Hypokalemia: ECG: U waves, flat T. Treatment: oral/IV potassium.
  • Hyponatremia: Risk of cerebral edema; correct slowly (risk of osmotic demyelination).
  • Hypernatremia: Free water deficit; correct slowly to avoid cerebral edema.
  • Hypercalcemia: IV fluids, bisphosphonates, calcitonin.
19. Overdose & Poisoning
  • Toxidromes: Opioid (sedation, miosis, bradypnea); cholinergic (SLUDGE); anticholinergic (tachycardia, mydriasis, dry skin, ileus); sympathomimetic (agitation, tachycardia, hypertension).
  • General management: ABCs, decontamination (activated charcoal), antidotes, enhanced elimination.
  • Common overdoses: Acetaminophen (NAC), opioids (naloxone), TCA (bicarbonate), organophosphates (atropine + pralidoxime).
20. Trauma Physiology
  • Primary survey (ABCDE): Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure.
  • Hemorrhagic shock: Class I‑IV based on blood loss.
  • Damage control resuscitation: Permissive hypotension, massive transfusion (1:1:1 plasma:platelets:RBC), tranexamic acid.
  • Head trauma: Cushing's triad (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respiration) – late sign of increased ICP.
  • Spine trauma: Neurogenic shock (loss of sympathetic tone → hypotension, bradycardia).
21. Burn Physiology
  • Rule of nines: Estimate TBSA burned.
  • Parkland formula: 4 mL × kg × %TBSA for first 24h; half in first 8h, half in next 16h.
  • Pathophysiology: Increased capillary permeability, massive fluid shifts, hypovolemia, hypermetabolism.
  • Inhalation injury: Carbon monoxide, cyanide, upper airway edema.
  • Electrical burns: Deep tissue injury, myoglobinuria, arrhythmias.
