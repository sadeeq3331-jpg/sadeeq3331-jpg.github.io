1. Principles of Antimicrobial Therapy

Antimicrobial drugs target specific microbial structures or metabolic pathways while minimizing toxicity to host cells. Key concepts include selective toxicity, bacteriostatic vs. bactericidal activity, spectrum of activity, and mechanisms of resistance.

  • Selective toxicity: Exploit differences between pathogen and human cells (e.g., bacterial cell wall, ribosomes).
  • Bacteriostatic: Inhibit growth; host immune system clears infection (e.g., tetracyclines, sulfonamides).
  • Bactericidal: Kill bacteria directly (e.g., beta‑lactams, aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones).
  • Spectrum: Narrow (e.g., penicillin G) vs. broad (e.g., carbapenems, tetracyclines).
  • MIC (minimum inhibitory concentration): Lowest drug concentration that prevents visible growth.
  • MBC (minimum bactericidal concentration): Lowest concentration that kills 99.9% of inoculum.

Clinical pearl: Combination therapy may be used for synergy (e.g., beta‑lactam + aminoglycoside) or to prevent resistance (e.g., tuberculosis).

2. Cell Wall Synthesis Inhibitors
Beta‑Lactams
  • Penicillins: Natural (penicillin G, V) – active against Gram‑positives, some Gram‑negatives; penicillinase‑resistant (methicillin, nafcillin) – anti‑staphylococcal; aminopenicillins (ampicillin, amoxicillin) – extended spectrum; anti‑pseudomonal (ticarcillin, piperacillin).
  • Cephalosporins: 1st generation (cefazolin, cephalexin) – Gram‑positives; 2nd (cefoxitin, cefuroxime) – some anaerobes; 3rd (ceftriaxone, cefotaxime) – Gram‑negatives, meningitis; 4th (cefepime) – broader, anti‑pseudomonal; 5th (ceftaroline) – MRSA.
  • Carbapenems: Imipenem, meropenem, ertapenem – very broad spectrum, including anaerobes.
  • Monobactams: Aztreonam – only active against Gram‑negatives (no cross‑allergy with penicillins).
Other Cell Wall Inhibitors
  • Vancomycin: Glycopeptide, inhibits cell wall synthesis by binding D‑Ala‑D‑Ala. Active against Gram‑positives (MRSA, C. difficile). IV for systemic infections; oral for C. difficile. Adverse effects: red man syndrome (infusion reaction), nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity.
  • Bacitracin: Topical, inhibits cell wall synthesis.
  • Fosfomycin: Inhibits peptidoglycan synthesis, used for uncomplicated UTIs.

Clinical pearl: Beta‑lactam allergy: cross‑reactivity between penicillins and cephalosporins is low (≈2‑10%). Avoid in severe reactions.

3. Protein Synthesis Inhibitors
30S Subunit Inhibitors
  • Aminoglycosides: Gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, streptomycin. Bactericidal, require oxygen for uptake; active against Gram‑negatives, synergy with beta‑lactams. Adverse effects: nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity, neuromuscular blockade.
  • Tetracyclines: Doxycycline, minocycline, tigecycline. Bacteriostatic, inhibit protein synthesis by binding 30S. Broad spectrum (including atypicals, intracellular). Contraindicated in pregnancy, children (teeth staining).
50S Subunit Inhibitors
  • Macrolides: Erythromycin, clarithromycin, azithromycin. Bacteriostatic, active against respiratory pathogens, atypicals. GI side effects (motilin agonist), QT prolongation.
  • Clindamycin: Active against anaerobes, Gram‑positives; risk of C. difficile colitis.
  • Chloramphenicol: Broad spectrum, but causes dose‑dependent bone marrow suppression, aplastic anemia (rare).
  • Linezolid: Oxazolidinone, active against MRSA, VRE. Myelosuppression, serotonin syndrome (MAOI effect).
  • Streptogramins: Quinupristin/dalfopristin – for VRE, MRSA.
4. DNA/RNA Synthesis Inhibitors
  • Fluoroquinolones: Ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin. Inhibit DNA gyrase (topo II) and topo IV. Broad spectrum, but resistance increasing. Adverse effects: tendonitis, QT prolongation, CNS effects.
  • Sulfonamides: Sulfamethoxazole (with trimethoprim – TMP‑SMX). Inhibit folate synthesis. Used for UTIs, MRSA, Pneumocystis jirovecii. Adverse effects: rash, Stevens‑Johnson syndrome, hemolysis in G6PD deficiency.
  • Metronidazole: Prodrug, activated in anaerobic organisms, damages DNA. Active against anaerobes, protozoa (Giardia, Trichomonas). Disulfiram‑like reaction with alcohol.
  • Rifampin: Inhibits RNA polymerase (bacterial). Used in TB, prophylaxis of meningococcal/Hib infection. Potent CYP inducer, orange discoloration of body fluids.
5. Antimycobacterial Drugs
First‑line Agents for TB
  • Isoniazid (INH): Inhibits mycolic acid synthesis. Hepatotoxicity, peripheral neuropathy (give B6).
  • Rifampin (RIF): Inhibits RNA polymerase; potent CYP inducer.
  • Pyrazinamide (PZA): Active in acidic environment (macrophages); hepatotoxicity, hyperuricemia.
  • Ethambutol (EMB): Inhibits arabinosyl transferase; optic neuritis (dose‑dependent).
Second‑line Agents
  • Streptomycin, fluoroquinolones, bedaquiline, linezolid, etc. Used for drug‑resistant TB.
Leprosy
  • Dapsone, rifampin, clofazimine.
6. Antiviral Drugs
Anti‑Herpes
  • Acyclovir, valacyclovir, famciclovir: Inhibit viral DNA polymerase. Used for HSV, VZV. Well tolerated.
Anti‑Influenza
  • Oseltamivir, zanamivir: Neuraminidase inhibitors. Reduce duration if started early.
Anti‑HIV
  • NRTIs: Zidovudine, tenofovir, emtricitabine – inhibit reverse transcriptase.
  • NNRTIs: Efavirenz, nevirapine – non‑competitive inhibitors.
  • Protease inhibitors: Lopinavir/ritonavir, atazanavir – inhibit viral maturation.
  • Integrase inhibitors: Raltegravir, dolutegravir.
  • Entry inhibitors: Enfuvirtide, maraviroc.
Anti‑Hepatitis
  • HBV: Tenofovir, entecavir, peginterferon.
  • HCV: Direct‑acting antivirals (sofosbuvir, ledipasvir, glecaprevir/pibrentasvir).

Clinical pearl: HIV requires combination ART to prevent resistance.

7. Antifungal Drugs
  • Polyenes: Amphotericin B – binds ergosterol, forms pores. Broad spectrum, but nephrotoxic, infusion reactions.
  • Azoles: Fluconazole (good for Candida, Cryptococcus), itraconazole, voriconazole (Aspergillus), posaconazole. Inhibit ergosterol synthesis (lanosterol 14α‑demethylase). Drug interactions, hepatotoxicity.
  • Echinocandins: Caspofungin, micafungin, anidulafungin – inhibit β‑glucan synthase. Active against Candida, Aspergillus. Well tolerated.
  • Flucytosine: Inhibits DNA/RNA synthesis; used with amphotericin for Cryptococcus.
  • Griseofulvin: For dermatophytes; deposits in keratin.
8. Antiparasitic Drugs
  • Antimalarials: Chloroquine (resistance widespread), quinine, artemisinin derivatives (artemether, artesunate), mefloquine, doxycycline, atovaquone‑proguanil.
  • Antiamoebic: Metronidazole, tinidazole, iodoquinol, paromomycin.
  • Anthelmintics: Albendazole, mebendazole (broad spectrum), ivermectin (strongyloides, onchocerciasis, scabies), praziquantel (schistosomiasis, cestodes), diethylcarbamazine (filariasis).
  • Other: Pentamidine (Pneumocystis, leishmania), nifurtimox (Chagas), suramin (African trypanosomiasis).
9. Antibiotic Resistance Mechanisms
  • Enzymatic inactivation: Beta‑lactamases (penicillinases, cephalosporinases, carbapenemases), aminoglycoside‑modifying enzymes, chloramphenicol acetyltransferase.
  • Target modification: Altered penicillin‑binding proteins (MRSA), ribosome methylation (macrolides, clindamycin – MLSB), DNA gyrase mutations (fluoroquinolones).
  • Efflux pumps: Tetracyclines, macrolides, fluoroquinolones.
  • Reduced permeability: Loss of porins (Gram‑negatives).
  • Metabolic bypass: Alternative pathway (e.g., vancomycin resistance – D‑Ala‑D‑Lac).

Clinical pearl: ESBL (extended‑spectrum beta‑lactamase) producers are resistant to penicillins and most cephalosporins; treat with carbapenems.

10. Immunomodulators & Vaccines
  • Vaccines: Live‑attenuated (MMR, varicella, yellow fever), inactivated (IPV, influenza), subunit (HepB, HPV), toxoid (tetanus, diphtheria), conjugate (Hib, pneumococcal).
  • Immune globulins: Passive immunity (IVIG, HBIG, rabies Ig).
  • Interferons: Peginterferon for HBV/HCV.
  • Cytokines: GM‑CSF, G‑CSF for neutropenia.
