1. Introduction to Musculoskeletal Pharmacology

Musculoskeletal pharmacology encompasses drugs used to treat pain, inflammation, autoimmune arthritis, gout, osteoporosis, and muscle spasms. Therapeutic approaches include symptomatic relief (analgesics, NSAIDs), disease modification (DMARDs, biologics), and management of metabolic bone diseases.

  • NSAIDs: Anti‑inflammatory, analgesic, antipyretic.
  • DMARDs: Slow progression of rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Gout drugs: Urate‑lowering, anti‑inflammatory.
  • Osteoporosis: Antiresorptive and anabolic agents.
2. NSAIDs & Acetaminophen

Nonsteroidal anti‑inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) inhibit cyclooxygenase (COX‑1 and/or COX‑2), reducing prostaglandin synthesis. They provide analgesic, anti‑inflammatory, and antipyretic effects.

Non‑selective COX Inhibitors
  • Ibuprofen, naproxen, ketoprofen: OTC, short‑acting.
  • Indomethacin: Potent, used for ankylosing spondylitis, gout; high CNS side effects.
  • Piroxicam: Long half‑life.
  • Diclofenac: Available as topical gel.
COX‑2 Selective Inhibitors
  • Celecoxib: Less GI toxicity; sulfonamide, may increase cardiovascular risk.
  • Rofecoxib, valdecoxib: Withdrawn due to CV events.
Adverse Effects of NSAIDs
  • GI: Gastritis, ulcers, bleeding (COX‑1 inhibition).
  • Renal: Decreased GFR, Na⁺ retention, papillary necrosis.
  • CV: Increased risk of thrombotic events (especially COX‑2 inhibitors).
  • Other: Asthma exacerbation (aspirin‑sensitive), hepatotoxicity.
Acetaminophen (Paracetamol)
  • Weak anti‑inflammatory, analgesic, antipyretic. Mechanism unclear (may involve COX inhibition in CNS).
  • Advantage: No GI ulcers, no antiplatelet effect.
  • Toxicity: Overdose causes hepatotoxicity (N‑acetylcysteine antidote).

Clinical pearl: NSAIDs increase cardiovascular risk; use lowest effective dose. Acetaminophen is safe for patients with GI or bleeding risk.

3. Disease‑Modifying Antirheumatic Drugs (DMARDs)

DMARDs slow progression of rheumatoid arthritis and other inflammatory arthritides. They are classified as conventional synthetic (csDMARDs) and targeted synthetic (tsDMARDs).

Methotrexate
  • First‑line csDMARD for RA. Inhibits dihydrofolate reductase, increases adenosine.
  • Given weekly (oral or SC). Supplement with folic acid to reduce side effects.
  • Adverse effects: Hepatotoxicity, bone marrow suppression, pulmonary fibrosis, stomatitis.
Leflunomide
  • Inhibits pyrimidine synthesis (dihydroorotate dehydrogenase).
  • Hepatotoxicity, diarrhea, teratogenic (washout with cholestyramine).
Sulfasalazine
  • Used in RA and inflammatory bowel disease. Metabolized to 5‑ASA and sulfapyridine.
  • GI upset, rash, oligospermia (reversible).
Hydroxychloroquine
  • Antimalarial; used in mild RA, SLE. Retinal toxicity (dose‑dependent) – baseline and annual eye exams.
Targeted Synthetic DMARDs
  • Tofacitinib, baricitinib, upadacitinib: JAK inhibitors. Oral; risk of infections, thrombosis, lipid changes.
4. Biologics in Rheumatology

Biologics target specific inflammatory cytokines or cells. They are used in moderate‑to‑severe RA, ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic arthritis, etc.

TNF‑α Inhibitors
  • Infliximab (chimeric), adalimumab (human), etanercept (receptor fusion), certolizumab (PEGylated Fab), golimumab (human).
  • Injection site reactions, increased risk of infections (TB reactivation – screen before use), demyelinating disorders, heart failure.
IL‑1 Inhibitors
  • Anakinra: Recombinant IL‑1 receptor antagonist; used in RA, cryopyrin‑associated periodic syndromes (CAPS).
IL‑6 Inhibitors
  • Tocilizumab, sarilumab: Block IL‑6 receptor. Can cause neutropenia, elevated LFTs, lipid changes.
T‑Cell Co‑stimulation Modulator
  • Abatacept: CTLA‑4 fusion protein; inhibits T‑cell activation.
B‑Cell Depleting Agent
  • Rituximab: Anti‑CD20; used in RA (after TNF failure). Risk of PML, infusion reactions.

Clinical pearl: Screen for latent TB before starting biologics. Do not combine biologics (high infection risk).

5. Drugs for Gout & Hyperuricemia
Acute Gout Therapy
  • NSAIDs: Indomethacin, naproxen – first‑line for acute attacks.
  • Colchicine: Inhibits microtubule polymerization, reduces neutrophil chemotaxis. Best if started early. GI toxicity (diarrhea, nausea), myelosuppression.
  • Corticosteroids: Oral, intra‑articular, or IM for patients intolerant to NSAIDs/colchicine.
Urate‑Lowering Therapy (Chronic)
  • Xanthine oxidase inhibitors:
    • Allopurinol: First‑line; inhibits uric acid synthesis. Start low, titrate. Risk of allopurinol hypersensitivity syndrome (rash, fever, hepatitis, eosinophilia – especially in renal impairment and thiazide use).
    • Febuxostat: Non‑purine xanthine oxidase inhibitor; used in allopurinol intolerance. Cardiovascular risk (higher mortality vs allopurinol).
  • Uricosuric agents:
    • Probenecid: Inhibits renal urate reabsorption (URAT1). Ineffective if CrCl <30. Avoid with aspirin (blocks effect).
    • Lesinurad: URAT1 inhibitor; used in combination with allopurinol/febuxostat.
  • Recombinant uricase:
    • Rasburicase: For tumor lysis syndrome; can cause hemolysis in G6PD deficiency.
    • Pegloticase: Pegylated uricase for refractory chronic gout; infusion reactions, anaphylaxis.

Clinical pearl: Do not start urate‑lowering during acute attack (can prolong flare). Start low, go slow, and use prophylaxis (colchicine or NSAID) for first 3‑6 months.

6. Osteoporosis Medications

Osteoporosis drugs are classified as antiresorptive (inhibit bone breakdown) or anabolic (stimulate bone formation).

Antiresorptive Agents
  • Bisphosphonates:
    • Alendronate, risedronate, ibandronate, zoledronic acid. Inhibit osteoclast activity. Oral bisphosphonates require strict dosing (empty stomach, upright, with water).
    • Adverse effects: Esophagitis, hypocalcemia, ONJ (osteonecrosis of jaw), atypical femur fractures.
  • Denosumab: RANKL inhibitor (monoclonal antibody); SC every 6 months. Risk of hypocalcemia, ONJ, atypical fractures. Rebound bone loss if discontinued.
  • Raloxifene (SERM): Estrogen agonist in bone, antagonist in breast/uterus; reduces vertebral fractures, but not hip. Hot flashes, DVT risk.
  • Hormone replacement therapy (estrogen): Used in menopause; increases risk of breast cancer, thrombosis.
  • Calcitonin: Nasal spray or SC; weak effect, used for pain in vertebral fractures.
Anabolic Agents
  • Teriparatide (PTH 1‑34): Daily SC injection; stimulates bone formation. Black box for osteosarcoma (rodent). Use limited to 2 years. Follow with antiresorptive.
  • Abaloparatide: PTH‑related protein analog; similar to teriparatide.

Clinical pearl: Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D intake with any osteoporosis therapy.

7. Muscle Relaxants

Muscle relaxants are used for acute musculoskeletal spasms, spasticity (CNS lesions), and chronic pain.

  • Cyclobenzaprine: Centrally acting, related to tricyclics; sedation, dry mouth.
  • Baclofen: GABAB agonist; used for spasticity (MS, spinal cord injury). Can cause sedation, weakness; abrupt withdrawal can cause hallucinations, seizures.
  • Tizanidine: α₂‑adrenergic agonist; reduces spasticity. Sedation, hypotension, hepatotoxicity (monitor LFTs).
  • Dantrolene: Directly acts on skeletal muscle ryanodine receptor, inhibiting Ca²⁺ release. Used for spasticity and malignant hyperthermia. Hepatotoxicity risk.
  • Carisoprodol, methocarbamol, metaxalone: Centrally acting; sedation, abuse potential (carisoprodol metabolized to meprobamate).
8. Corticosteroids in Musculoskeletal Disease

Corticosteroids are potent anti‑inflammatories used in RA, PMR, vasculitis, and gout (local injection).

  • Examples: Prednisone, methylprednisolone, triamcinolone (intra‑articular).
  • Mechanism: Inhibit cytokines, adhesion molecules, and inflammatory cell migration.
  • Adverse effects (long‑term): Osteoporosis, avascular necrosis, weight gain, hyperglycemia, cataracts, immunosuppression.
9. Drugs for Osteoarthritis & Topical Agents
  • Topical NSAIDs: Diclofenac gel, patch; fewer systemic effects.
  • Capsaicin: Depletes substance P from sensory neurons; burning sensation initially.
  • Intra‑articular injections:
    • Hyaluronic acid: Viscosupplementation for knee OA; questionable efficacy.
    • Corticosteroids: For flare of OA.
  • Acetaminophen: First‑line for mild OA pain.
10. Miscellaneous Agents
  • Bisphosphonates (covered in osteoporosis).
  • Strontium ranelate: Dual action (increases bone formation, decreases resorption). Now limited due to cardiovascular risk.
  • Vitamin D and calcium: Essential for bone health.
  • Glucosamine and chondroitin: Popular supplements for OA; evidence weak.
