1. Diuretics
Overview & Sites of Action

Diuretics increase urine output by inhibiting sodium reabsorption at specific nephron sites. They are used in hypertension, heart failure, edema, and certain renal disorders.

ClassSite of ActionExamplesMechanism
Loop diureticsThick ascending limb (Na‑K‑2Cl cotransporter)Furosemide, bumetanide, torsemideInhibit NKCC2 → ↑ Na, Cl, K excretion
ThiazidesDistal convoluted tubule (Na‑Cl cotransporter)Hydrochlorothiazide, chlorthalidoneInhibit NCC → ↑ Na, Cl excretion
K‑sparing (aldosterone antagonists)Collecting ductSpironolactone, eplerenoneAntagonize aldosterone → ↓ Na reabsorption, ↓ K secretion
K‑sparing (ENaC blockers)Collecting ductAmiloride, triamtereneBlock ENaC → ↓ Na reabsorption, ↓ K secretion
Carbonic anhydrase inhibitorsProximal tubuleAcetazolamideInhibit CA → ↓ HCO₃ reabsorption → alkaline diuresis
Osmotic diureticsProximal tubule, loop of HenleMannitolIncrease osmotic pressure of filtrate → water diuresis
Loop Diuretics
  • Pharmacokinetics: Oral/IV, highly protein‑bound, secreted into proximal tubule.
  • Uses: Pulmonary edema, heart failure, renal insufficiency, hypertension (add‑on), hypercalcemia (increase Ca excretion).
  • Adverse effects: Hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, metabolic alkalosis, ototoxicity (high doses), dehydration.
Thiazides
  • Uses: Mild‑moderate hypertension, heart failure, nephrolithiasis (hypercalciuria), diabetes insipidus (paradoxical antidiuresis).
  • Adverse effects: Hypokalemia, hyponatremia, hyperuricemia, hyperglycemia, hyperlipidemia, photosensitivity.
Potassium‑Sparing Diuretics
  • Spironolactone/eplerenone: Aldosterone antagonists; used in heart failure (improve survival), primary aldosteronism, cirrhosis. Side effects: hyperkalemia, gynecomastia (spironolactone).
  • Amiloride/triamterene: ENaC blockers; used for hypertension, often in combo with thiazides. Side effects: hyperkalemia, GI upset.

Clinical pearl: In heart failure, loop diuretics relieve congestion, but aldosterone antagonists reduce mortality. Monitor potassium closely.

2. ACE Inhibitors

ACE inhibitors block angiotensin‑converting enzyme, preventing conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II. This leads to vasodilation, reduced aldosterone secretion, and decreased degradation of bradykinin.

Examples
  • Captopril (short‑acting, sulfhydryl group, taste disturbances, rash)
  • Enalapril, lisinopril, ramipril, trandolapril, fosinopril (longer‑acting)
Clinical Uses
  • Hypertension (first‑line, especially in diabetics, CKD)
  • Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (reduce mortality)
  • Post‑MI (reduce remodeling, mortality)
  • Diabetic nephropathy, proteinuric CKD
Adverse Effects
  • Dry cough (due to bradykinin accumulation) – most common reason for discontinuation.
  • Angioedema (rare but life‑threatening).
  • Hyperkalemia (due to reduced aldosterone).
  • Acute kidney injury if bilateral renal artery stenosis.
  • Teratogenicity (contraindicated in pregnancy).

Pearl: ACE inhibitors are first‑line in diabetes and CKD to slow progression of nephropathy. Monitor renal function and potassium after initiation.

3. Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers (ARBs)

ARBs selectively block the AT₁ receptor of angiotensin II, resulting in vasodilation, reduced aldosterone secretion, and sympathetic inhibition. Unlike ACE inhibitors, they do not affect bradykinin metabolism.

Examples
  • Losartan, valsartan, candesartan, irbesartan, telmisartan, olmesartan, azilsartan
Clinical Uses
  • Hypertension (alternative to ACE inhibitors, especially when cough occurs)
  • Heart failure (reduces hospitalization, mortality)
  • Diabetic nephropathy, proteinuric CKD
  • Post‑MI (if ACE intolerant)
Adverse Effects
  • No cough, lower risk of angioedema.
  • Hyperkalemia, renal impairment, hypotension – similar to ACE inhibitors.
  • Teratogenic – contraindicated in pregnancy.

Memory aid: “ARBs – Angry Receptors Blocked – same benefits as ACE, without the cough.”

4. Calcium Channel Blockers

CCBs inhibit L‑type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle and myocardium. They are divided into dihydropyridines (primarily vasodilators) and non‑dihydropyridines (more cardiac‑active).

ClassExamplesEffects
DihydropyridinesNifedipine, amlodipine, felodipine, nicardipineVasodilation (↓ BP), minimal cardiac depression
Non‑dihydropyridinesVerapamil, diltiazem↓ HR, ↓ contractility, ↓ AV conduction; also vasodilation
Clinical Uses
  • Hypertension: All CCBs, especially dihydropyridines (amlodipine).
  • Angina: Verapamil, diltiazem (reduce O2 demand); nifedipine (may cause reflex tachycardia).
  • Supraventricular tachycardia: Verapamil, diltiazem (slow AV nodal conduction).
  • Raynaud phenomenon: Dihydropyridines (vasodilation).
Adverse Effects
  • Dihydropyridines: Flushing, headache, dizziness, peripheral edema (not due to heart failure), reflex tachycardia.
  • Verapamil/diltiazem: Bradycardia, AV block, constipation (verapamil), negative inotropy (avoid in heart failure).

Pearl: Avoid verapamil in patients with heart failure with reduced EF due to negative inotropy. Dihydropyridines are safe in combination with beta‑blockers for angina.

5. Beta‑Adrenergic Blockers

Beta‑blockers competitively antagonize catecholamines at β‑adrenergic receptors. They are classified as cardioselective (β₁), non‑selective (β₁ + β₂), or with additional α‑blockade.

TypeExamplesProperties
β₁‑selectiveMetoprolol, atenolol, bisoprolol, esmololLess bronchospasm, less peripheral vasoconstriction
Non‑selectivePropranolol, nadolol, timololBlock β₂ as well; risk of bronchospasm
With ISAPindolol, acebutololPartial agonist activity – less bradycardia
With α‑blockadeCarvedilol, labetalolVasodilation; used in heart failure, hypertensive emergencies
Clinical Uses
  • Hypertension: Reduce cardiac output, renin release.
  • Angina pectoris: Reduce O2 demand by ↓HR, ↓contractility.
  • Heart failure (HFrEF): Carvedilol, bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate – improve survival.
  • Post‑MI: Reduce mortality, prevent remodeling.
  • Supraventricular arrhythmias: Slow AV conduction.
  • Migraine prophylaxis, essential tremor, thyrotoxicosis, glaucoma (timolol).
Adverse Effects
  • Bradycardia, AV block, hypotension, fatigue, decreased exercise tolerance.
  • Bronchospasm (non‑selective; caution in asthma/COPD).
  • Mask hypoglycemia symptoms (tachycardia) – use cardioselective with caution in diabetics.
  • Abrupt withdrawal can cause rebound hypertension/angina.

Pearl: In heart failure, start low and titrate slowly. Never withdraw beta‑blockers abruptly.

6. Alpha‑Blockers & Central Sympatholytics
Alpha‑1 Blockers
  • Examples: Prazosin, doxazosin, terazosin, tamsulosin (uroselective).
  • Mechanism: Block α₁ receptors on vascular smooth muscle → vasodilation.
  • Uses: Hypertension (add‑on), benign prostatic hyperplasia (tamsulosin).
  • Adverse effects: First‑dose hypotension (syncope), dizziness, reflex tachycardia.
Central Alpha‑2 Agonists
  • Clonidine, methyldopa, guanfacine.
  • Mechanism: Stimulate α₂ receptors in brainstem → reduced sympathetic outflow.
  • Uses: Hypertension (especially in pregnancy – methyldopa), opioid withdrawal, ADHD (guanfacine).
  • Adverse effects: Sedation, dry mouth, rebound hypertension (clonidine withdrawal).
Vasodilators
  • Hydralazine: Direct arteriolar vasodilator; used in hypertension, heart failure (in combo with isosorbide dinitrate in African‑Americans). Side effects: reflex tachycardia, fluid retention, lupus‑like syndrome (slow acetylators).
  • Minoxidil: Potent arteriolar vasodilator; used for refractory hypertension. Side effects: hypertrichosis, pericardial effusion.
7. Nitrates

Nitrates are prodrugs converted to nitric oxide (NO) in vascular smooth muscle, activating guanylyl cyclase → ↑ cGMP → smooth muscle relaxation (venodilation predominates at low doses, arteriodilation at higher doses).

Examples
  • Short‑acting: Nitroglycerin (sublingual, spray, IV) – rapid onset (2‑5 min).
  • Long‑acting: Isosorbide dinitrate, isosorbide mononitrate – oral.
Clinical Uses
  • Acute angina (sublingual NTG).
  • Prophylaxis of angina (long‑acting).
  • Heart failure (especially with hydralazine in African‑Americans).
  • Acute pulmonary edema (IV nitroglycerin).
Adverse Effects
  • Headache, flushing, hypotension, reflex tachycardia.
  • Tolerance develops with continuous use – needs nitrate‑free interval (10‑12h).
  • Contraindicated with PDE‑5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil) – severe hypotension.

Memory aid: “Nitrates need a break – 10‑12 hours nitrate‑free interval to avoid tolerance.”

8. Beta‑Blockers in Angina

Beta‑blockers reduce myocardial oxygen demand by decreasing heart rate, contractility, and blood pressure. They are first‑line for chronic stable angina and post‑MI. They also reduce exercise‑induced tachycardia and improve exercise tolerance.

Preferred: cardioselective (atenolol, metoprolol) to minimize bronchospasm. Carvedilol also used in heart failure.

9. Calcium Channel Blockers in Angina

Verapamil and diltiazem reduce O2 demand by decreasing HR and contractility; also cause coronary vasodilation. Dihydropyridines (nifedipine) may cause reflex tachycardia; they are often combined with beta‑blockers to offset this.

Variant (Prinzmetal) angina: CCBs are first‑line because they relieve coronary vasospasm.

10. Ranolazine

Ranolazine is an antianginal agent that inhibits late sodium current in cardiac cells, reducing intracellular calcium overload and improving diastolic relaxation. It does not affect HR or BP. Used for chronic angina when other drugs are insufficient. Side effects: QT prolongation, dizziness, constipation.

12. Digoxin

Digoxin inhibits Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase, increasing intracellular Na⁺, which then reduces activity of Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger → ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺ → increased contractility (positive inotropy). It also increases vagal tone (negative chronotropy).

Clinical Uses
  • Heart failure with reduced EF (especially with atrial fibrillation).
  • Atrial fibrillation (rate control).
Pharmacokinetics
  • Narrow therapeutic window (0.5‑0.9 ng/mL).
  • Renal excretion; dose adjust in renal impairment.
Adverse Effects (toxicity)
  • Cardiac: arrhythmias (PVCs, VT, AV block, atrial tachycardia with block).
  • Extracardiac: nausea, vomiting, anorexia, visual disturbances (yellow‑green halos).

Management of toxicity: Stop digoxin, correct hypokalemia, consider digoxin‑specific Fab antibodies.

14. Class II – Beta‑Blockers

Beta‑blockers (e.g., metoprolol, propranolol) suppress arrhythmias by reducing sympathetic activity, slowing sinus rate, and prolonging AV nodal conduction. They are used for sinus tachycardia, atrial fibrillation (rate control), and prevention of sudden death post‑MI.

15. Class III – Potassium Channel Blockers
  • Amiodarone: Multichannel blocker (also I, II, IV); most effective antiarrhythmic but toxic (pulmonary fibrosis, thyroid dysfunction, hepatotoxicity, corneal deposits, blue‑gray skin).
  • Dofetilide: Pure class III; used in AF; requires hospital initiation due to torsades risk.
  • Ibutilide: IV for acute conversion of AF/AFL.
  • Sotalol: Combined β‑blocker + class III; used for AF, ventricular arrhythmias; risk of torsades.
16. Class IV – Calcium Channel Blockers

Verapamil and diltiazem slow AV nodal conduction, used for rate control in atrial fibrillation and for terminating AVNRT. Contraindicated in wide QRS tachycardia of unknown origin.

17. Miscellaneous Antiarrhythmics
  • Adenosine: Endogenous nucleoside, transiently blocks AV node via A₁ receptors. Used for acute termination of PSVT. Causes flushing, chest discomfort, bronchospasm.
  • Magnesium: Used in torsades de pointes, especially if hypomagnesemia.
18. Statins (HMG‑CoA Reductase Inhibitors)

Statins competitively inhibit HMG‑CoA reductase, reducing cholesterol synthesis in the liver, leading to upregulation of LDL receptors and decreased plasma LDL‑C. They also have pleiotropic effects (anti‑inflammatory, plaque stabilization).

Examples
  • High‑intensity: Atorvastatin, rosuvastatin.
  • Moderate‑intensity: Simvastatin, pravastatin, lovastatin, fluvastatin, pitavastatin.
Clinical Uses
  • Primary and secondary prevention of cardiovascular events.
  • Hypercholesterolemia.
Adverse Effects
  • Myalgia, myopathy, rhabdomyolysis (↑ risk with fibrates, niacin, gemfibrozil).
  • Hepatotoxicity (elevated transaminases) – monitor.
  • Increased risk of new‑onset diabetes (small).

Pearl: Statins are best taken in the evening (except atorvastatin, rosuvastatin) because cholesterol synthesis peaks overnight. Monitor for muscle symptoms.

19. Other Lipid‑Lowering Agents
  • Ezetimibe: Inhibits intestinal cholesterol absorption (NPC1L1); used alone or with statin. Minimal side effects.
  • PCSK9 inhibitors: Alirocumab, evolocumab – monoclonal antibodies increase LDL receptor recycling; potent LDL lowering; SC injection.
  • Bile acid sequestrants: Cholestyramine, colesevelam – bind bile acids in gut, interrupting enterohepatic circulation. May ↑ TG, constipation.
  • Fibrates: Fenofibrate, gemfibrozil – PPAR‑α agonists; ↓ TG, ↑ HDL. Risk of myopathy with statins (gemfibrozil contraindicated with statins).
  • Niacin: ↓ VLDL, ↑ HDL; side effects: flushing, hyperglycemia, hepatotoxicity.
  • Icosapent ethyl: Pure EPA; reduces CV events in high‑risk patients with elevated TG.
20. Antiplatelet Drugs
  • Aspirin: Irreversibly inhibits COX‑1 → ↓ thromboxane A₂. Used for secondary prevention of CV events. Side effects: GI bleeding, ulcers.
  • P2Y₁₂ inhibitors: Clopidogrel, prasugrel, ticagrelor. Block ADP receptor on platelets. Used in ACS and after stent placement. Clopidogrel is prodrug (CYP2C19); prasugrel more potent, contraindicated in prior stroke; ticagrelor reversible, direct‑acting.
  • GP IIb/IIIa inhibitors: Abciximab, eptifibatide, tirofiban – IV; block final common pathway of platelet aggregation; used in high‑risk PCI.
  • Dipyridamole: Inhibits phosphodiesterase; often combined with aspirin (Aggrenox) for stroke prevention.
21. Anticoagulants
Heparin & Derivatives
  • Unfractionated heparin (UFH): Activates antithrombin III, inhibiting thrombin and factor Xa. IV/SC; monitor aPTT. Side effects: bleeding, HIT.
  • Low molecular weight heparin (LMWH): Enoxaparin, dalteparin – more predictable, anti‑Xa > anti‑IIa, SC, lower HIT risk.
  • Fondaparinux: Synthetic pentasaccharide, selective anti‑Xa; no HIT.
Warfarin
  • Vitamin K antagonist; inhibits synthesis of factors II, VII, IX, X, proteins C, S. Monitor INR (target 2‑3 for most indications). Narrow therapeutic index, many drug/food interactions. Side effects: bleeding, skin necrosis.
Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)
  • Direct thrombin inhibitor: Dabigatran (prodrug).
  • Factor Xa inhibitors: Rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban.
  • Advantages over warfarin: no routine monitoring, fewer drug interactions, rapid onset. Disadvantages: cost, lack of reversal agents (except dabigatran has idarucizumab; andexanet alfa for Xa inhibitors).
22. Thrombolytics (Fibrinolytics)

Thrombolytics activate plasminogen to plasmin, which degrades fibrin clots. Used in STEMI, acute ischemic stroke (selected), massive PE.

  • Streptokinase: Antigenic, may cause allergic reactions; rarely used now.
  • Alteplase (tPA): Tissue plasminogen activator; fibrin‑selective.
  • Tenecteplase: Modified tPA, longer half‑life, single bolus.

Contraindications: Active bleeding, recent surgery, history of intracranial hemorrhage, severe hypertension.
