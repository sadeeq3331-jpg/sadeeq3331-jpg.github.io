1. Introduction to Microbiology

Microbiology is the study of microorganisms, including bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites. These organisms can be beneficial (normal flora) or pathogenic (causing disease). Infectious diseases remain a leading cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide.

Classification of Microorganisms
  • Bacteria: Prokaryotic, unicellular, peptidoglycan cell wall, reproduce asexually.
  • Viruses: Acellular, require host cell machinery to replicate, composed of nucleic acid (DNA or RNA) and protein coat.
  • Fungi: Eukaryotic, chitin cell wall, can be yeasts (unicellular) or molds (filamentous).
  • Parasites: Eukaryotic protozoa (unicellular) and helminths (multicellular worms).

Clinical pearl: Koch's postulates established criteria for proving a microbe causes a disease. Molecular versions (e.g., PCR) now supplement them.

2. Bacterial Structure & Physiology

Bacteria have characteristic structures that are targets for antibiotics and diagnostic tests.

Cell Wall
  • Gram‑positive: Thick peptidoglycan layer, retains crystal violet stain (purple). Contains teichoic and lipoteichoic acids. Examples: Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, Bacillus, Clostridium.
  • Gram‑negative: Thin peptidoglycan, outer membrane with lipopolysaccharide (LPS) and porins. Does not retain crystal violet (counterstain pink). LPS (endotoxin) triggers sepsis.
Capsule
  • Polysaccharide layer, antiphagocytic. Many pathogenic bacteria have capsules (e.g., Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Neisseria meningitidis).
Flagella
  • Long, filamentous structures for motility. Antigenic (H antigen).
Pili (Fimbriae)
  • Hair‑like structures for adherence to surfaces and other bacteria (sex pili for conjugation).
Spores
  • Dormant structures formed by Bacillus and Clostridium; resistant to heat, chemicals, radiation.

Memory aid: “Gram‑positive = thick purple wall; Gram‑negative = thin pink wall with outer LPS.”

3. Bacterial Genetics & Growth

Bacteria reproduce asexually by binary fission. They can exchange genetic material through transformation, transduction, and conjugation.

  • Transformation: Uptake of free DNA from environment (e.g., Streptococcus pneumoniae).
  • Transduction: Transfer of DNA via bacteriophage.
  • Conjugation: Direct transfer via pilus, often involving plasmids (e.g., antibiotic resistance genes).
Bacterial Growth Curve
  • Lag phase → log (exponential) phase → stationary phase → death phase.
4. Virology

Viruses consist of a nucleic acid core (DNA or RNA) surrounded by a protein capsid; some have an envelope derived from host cell membrane.

Classification
  • DNA viruses: Parvoviridae (ssDNA), Papillomaviridae, Adenoviridae, Herpesviridae, Poxviridae. Usually replicate in nucleus.
  • RNA viruses: Picornaviridae, Caliciviridae, Flaviviridae, Togaviridae, Retroviridae, Orthomyxoviridae, Paramyxoviridae, Rhabdoviridae, Filoviridae. Many replicate in cytoplasm.
  • Baltimore classification: Based on genome type and replication strategy.
Viral Replication
  • Attachment → penetration → uncoating → synthesis of viral components → assembly → release (lysis or budding).

Clinical pearl: Retroviruses (HIV) use reverse transcriptase to make DNA from RNA. Antiviral drugs target viral enzymes.

5. Mycology

Fungi are eukaryotes with chitin cell walls. They exist as yeasts (unicellular, reproduce by budding) or molds (filamentous, hyphae). Dimorphic fungi can switch between forms depending on temperature.

6. Parasitology

Parasites are eukaryotic organisms that live on or within a host. Major groups: protozoa (unicellular) and helminths (worms).

Protozoa
  • Intestinal: Entamoeba histolytica, Giardia lamblia, Cryptosporidium.
  • Urogenital: Trichomonas vaginalis.
  • Blood/tissue: Plasmodium (malaria), Toxoplasma gondii, Trypanosoma, Leishmania.
Helminths
  • Nematodes (roundworms): Ascaris, hookworms, Enterobius (pinworm), Strongyloides, filarial worms (Wuchereria bancrofti).
  • Cestodes (tapeworms): Taenia solium (pork), Taenia saginata (beef), Echinococcus (hydatid cyst).
  • Trematodes (flukes): Schistosoma, Clonorchis sinensis, Paragonimus westermani.
7. Host‑Pathogen Interactions

Pathogens use virulence factors to establish infection and evade host defenses.

  • Adherence: Pili, adhesins, biofilms.
  • Invasion: Invasins, phagosome escape (e.g., Listeria).
  • Evasion: Capsule (antiphagocytic), antigenic variation (e.g., Neisseria, influenza), IgA protease, interference with complement.
  • Toxins: Exotoxins (secreted proteins, highly potent) and endotoxin (LPS of Gram‑negative).

Clinical pearl: Exotoxins can be neutralized by antitoxins; endotoxin triggers systemic inflammatory response (sepsis).

8. Innate Immunity

Innate immunity is the first line of defense, non‑specific, and includes physical barriers, phagocytes, complement, and inflammation.

  • Physical barriers: Skin, mucous membranes, cilia, secretions.
  • Cells: Neutrophils (phagocytosis), macrophages (phagocytosis, antigen presentation), dendritic cells (APCs), NK cells (kill virus‑infected cells).
  • Complement: Classical (antibody‑activated), lectin (mannose‑binding lectin), alternative (spontaneous). MAC lyses bacteria.
  • PRRs and PAMPs: Toll‑like receptors recognize pathogen‑associated molecular patterns.
9. Adaptive Immunity

Adaptive immunity is specific, develops after exposure, and has memory. It involves B cells (humoral) and T cells (cellular).

  • B cells: Produce antibodies (IgG, IgA, IgM, IgE, IgD). Neutralize pathogens, opsonize, activate complement.
  • T cells: Helper (CD4+) recognize MHC II, help B cells and macrophages; cytotoxic (CD8+) kill infected cells via perforin/granzyme.
  • MHC: MHC I presents endogenous antigen to CD8+; MHC II presents exogenous to CD4+.
10. Normal Flora & Pathogenesis

The human body is colonized by a diverse microbial community (normal flora) that protects against pathogens, aids digestion, and trains the immune system.

  • Skin: Staphylococcus epidermidis, Propionibacterium acnes.
  • Oropharynx: Viridans streptococci, Neisseria spp., anaerobes.
  • GI tract: Bacteroides, E. coli, Lactobacillus, Clostridium.
  • Urogenital: Lactobacillus (vagina), skin flora (external urethra).

Clinical pearl: Disruption of normal flora by antibiotics can lead to overgrowth of pathogens (e.g., C. difficile colitis).

11. Epidemiology & Infection Control

Epidemiology studies the distribution and determinants of diseases. Infection control measures prevent transmission.

  • Chain of infection: Infectious agent → reservoir → portal of exit → mode of transmission → portal of entry → susceptible host.
  • Transmission routes: Contact (direct, indirect, droplet), airborne, vector‑borne, common vehicle.
  • Isolation precautions: Contact, droplet, airborne.
