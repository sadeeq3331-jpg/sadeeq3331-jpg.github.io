1. Neuronal Injury & Death
What Happens When Neurons Are Injured?

Neurons are highly sensitive to injury. They respond in characteristic ways depending on the type, severity, and duration of insult .

Acute Neuronal Injury (Red Neuron)

After acute hypoxia/ischemia, neurons undergo eosinophilic change (red neuron). The cytoplasm shrinks, nucleus becomes pyknotic, and the neuron stains bright red with eosin. This is irreversible and seen within 6–12 hours of severe ischemia .

Chronic Neuronal Injury
  • Central chromatolysis: Occurs after axonal injury. The cell body swells, Nissl substance (rough ER) disperses, and nucleus moves to periphery. It reflects increased protein synthesis for regeneration .
  • Neurofibrillary tangles: Aggregates of hyperphosphorylated tau protein in Alzheimer’s disease.
  • Lewy bodies: Eosinophilic cytoplasmic inclusions of α‑synuclein in Parkinson’s disease.
  • Pick bodies: Round, tau‑positive inclusions in Pick’s disease (frontotemporal dementia).
Neuronal Death Mechanisms
  • Necrosis: Passive, ATP‑independent; cell swells, membrane ruptures, inflammation follows (e.g., infarction).
  • Apoptosis: Programmed cell death; cell shrinks, chromatin condenses, caspases activated; no inflammation (e.g., neurodegenerative diseases).
  • Autophagy: Self‑digestion of organelles; can be protective or pathological.

Ischemic stroke causes necrosis in the core, apoptosis in the penumbra. Therapeutic hypothermia reduces apoptosis.

2. Glial Reactions (Astrocytes, Microglia)
Astrocytic Reactions
  • Astrogliosis (reactive gliosis): Astrocytes proliferate and hypertrophy, extending processes and increasing GFAP expression. This occurs in response to any CNS insult (trauma, ischemia, infection) .
  • Rosenthal fibers: Thick, eosinophilic inclusions in astrocytes, seen in chronic gliosis (e.g., pilocytic astrocytoma).
  • Corpora amylacea: Polyglucosan bodies in astrocytic processes; age‑related, incidental.
Microglial Reactions
  • Microglial nodules: Clusters of activated microglia; seen in viral encephalitis.
  • Neuronophagia: Microglia engulfing dead neurons.
  • Rod cells: Elongated microglia in general paresis (neurosyphilis).
3. Cerebral Edema & Herniation
Types of Edema
  • Vasogenic edema: BBB breakdown → fluid extravasates into extracellular space (white matter). Causes: tumor, abscess, trauma.
  • Cytotoxic edema: Cellular swelling due to Na⁺/K⁺ pump failure (ischemia). Gray and white matter.
  • Interstitial edema: Transependymal flow in hydrocephalus.
Herniation Syndromes
  • Subfalcine (cingulate): Cingulate gyrus under falx → may compress ACA.
  • Transtentorial (uncal): Uncus herniates over tentorium → compresses CN III (ipsilateral dilated pupil) and contralateral hemiparesis (Kernohan’s phenomenon).
  • Cerebellar tonsillar: Tonsils through foramen magnum → compresses medulla → respiratory arrest.

Rapid herniation is a neurosurgical emergency; mannitol, hyperventilation, and surgical decompression are used.

4. Cerebral Ischemia & Infarction

Ischemia results from reduced blood flow. Global ischemia (cardiac arrest) vs. focal ischemia (arterial occlusion).

Global Ischemia
  • Selective vulnerability: Certain neurons (hippocampal CA1, Purkinje cells, cortical layers 3,5,6) are most susceptible.
  • Morphology: Red neurons in affected areas.
  • Outcome: If brief, may be reversible; prolonged → infarction.
Focal Ischemia (Infarction)
  • Thrombotic: Atherosclerosis, small vessel disease (lacunar infarcts).
  • Embolic: From heart (AF, valve vegetations) or arteries → often wedge‑shaped, hemorrhagic transformation.
  • Watershed infarcts: Border zones between major arteries; after hypotension.

Sudden neurologic deficit; tPA within 3‑4.5h can salvage penumbra.

5. Intracranial Hemorrhage
  • Intracerebral hemorrhage: Hypertensive (basal ganglia, thalamus, pons, cerebellum), amyloid angiopathy (lobar), vascular malformations.
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage: Ruptured saccular aneurysm (circle of Willis) → thunderclap headache.
6. Vascular Malformations
  • Arteriovenous malformation (AVM): Abnormal connection arteries→veins; high bleed risk.
  • Cavernous malformation: Sinusoidal spaces; seizures, hemorrhage.
7. Head Injury (Contusions, DAI)
  • Contusion: Coup (at impact) and contrecoup (opposite). Common in frontal/temporal poles.
  • Diffuse axonal injury (DAI): Shearing forces → axonal retraction balls, petechial hemorrhages in corpus callosum, brainstem; causes prolonged coma.
8. Spinal Cord Trauma
Central cord syndrome, anterior cord syndrome, etc. Secondary injury mechanisms: ischemia, excitotoxicity, inflammation.
9. Meningitis (Bacterial, Viral, Fungal)

Inflammation of leptomeninges.

  • Bacterial: Exudate in subarachnoid space; neutrophils; organisms: N. meningitidis, S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae.
  • Viral: Lymphocytic infiltrate; usually milder (enteroviruses).
  • Tuberculous: Basilar exudates, cranial nerve palsies; caseous granulomas.
  • Fungal: Cryptococcus (immunocompromised); India ink positive.
10. Encephalitis (HSV, Arboviruses)

HSV encephalitis: Temporal lobe involvement, hemorrhagic necrosis, Cowdry A inclusions. Treated with acyclovir.

11. Brain Abscess
Encapsulated collection of pus; often from contiguous spread (sinusitis) or hematogenous. Ring‑enhancing lesion.
12. Tuberculosis & Tuberculoma
Caseating granulomas; basilar meningitis common.
13. Prion Diseases (CJD, vCJD)

Misfolded prion protein (PrPSc) causes spongiform change, neuronal loss, gliosis, no inflammation. Sporadic (CJD), genetic (FFI), acquired (kuru, vCJD).

14. Multiple Sclerosis

Autoimmune demyelination with relative axonal sparing. Perivenular inflammatory demyelination (plaques). Oligoclonal bands in CSF. Relapsing‑remitting most common.

15. Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy
JC virus (polyomavirus) in immunocompromised; lytic infection of oligodendrocytes → multifocal demyelination.
16. Adrenoleukodystrophy
X‑linked; defective peroxisomal β‑oxidation → accumulation of very‑long‑chain fatty acids → inflammatory demyelination and adrenal insufficiency.
17. Alzheimer’s Disease

Pathology: Cortical atrophy, especially hippocampus; senile plaques (Aβ amyloid), neurofibrillary tangles (hyperphosphorylated tau).

Genetics: Early‑onset (APP, PSEN1, PSEN2); late‑onset (APOE ε4 risk).

Mechanism: Aβ accumulation triggers tau hyperphosphorylation, synaptic loss, neuronal death.

18. Parkinson’s Disease

Loss of dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra pars compacta; Lewy bodies (α‑synuclein). Motor features: bradykinesia, resting tremor, rigidity.

19. Huntington’s Disease

Autosomal dominant CAG repeat (chromosome 4); huntingtin protein. Atrophy of caudate and putamen; chorea, dementia.

20. Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis
Loss of upper and lower motor neurons; corticospinal tract degeneration; Bunina bodies. Progressive weakness, fasciculations.
21. Frontotemporal Lobar Degeneration
Pick bodies (tau), TDP‑43 inclusions, FUS pathology. Behavioral and language variants.
22. Gliomas (Astrocytoma, GBM)
  • Diffuse astrocytoma (WHO grade 2): IDH mutation, often.
  • Glioblastoma (GBM, grade 4): Pseudopalisading necrosis, microvascular proliferation; IDH‑wildtype most common.
  • Oligodendroglioma: “Fried‑egg” cells, 1p/19q codeletion; better prognosis.
23. Meningioma
Benign, from arachnoid cap cells; psammoma bodies; compresses brain, invasion rare.
24. Medulloblastoma
Cerebellar primitive neuroectodermal tumor; Homer‑Wright rosettes; highly malignant.
25. Schwannoma & Neurofibroma
Schwannoma (CN VIII) – Antoni A/B; neurofibroma – NF1, can undergo malignant transformation.
26. Metastatic Tumors
Most common intracranial tumors in adults; lung, breast, melanoma, renal. Often multiple, at gray‑white junction.
27. Neural Tube Defects
Anencephaly, spina bifida occulta, meningocele, myelomeningocele. Folate deficiency is risk factor.
28. Phakomatoses (NF1, NF2, TSC)
  • NF1: Neurofibromas, café‑au‑lait spots, Lisch nodules, optic glioma.
  • NF2: Bilateral acoustic schwannomas, meningiomas.
  • Tuberous sclerosis: Cortical tubers, subependymal nodules (giant cell astrocytoma), cardiac rhabdomyoma.
29. Perinatal Brain Injury
Hypoxic‑ischemic encephalopathy, periventricular leukomalacia (prematurity), germinal matrix hemorrhage.
30. Peripheral Neuropathies
  • Axonal: “Dying back” – metabolic (diabetes), toxic (alcohol).
  • Demyelinating: Segmental – Guillain‑Barré, CIDP, CMT.
  • Mononeuropathy: Carpal tunnel (median), peroneal palsy.
31. Neuromuscular Junction (MG, LEMS)

Myasthenia gravis: Antibodies against ACh receptors; fatigable weakness; anti‑AChR Ab.

Lambert‑Eaton: Antibodies against presynaptic voltage‑gated Ca²⁺ channels; associated with SCLC; strength improves with repetition.

32. Myopathies (Duchenne, Becker)

Duchenne: X‑linked; dystrophin absent; progressive weakness, pseudohypertrophy.

Becker: Reduced dystrophin; milder course.

33. Epilepsy (Pathology & Mechanisms)

Mesial temporal sclerosis: Hippocampal neuronal loss, gliosis → temporal lobe epilepsy.

Cortical dysplasia: Malformed cortex → refractory epilepsy.

34. Hydrocephalus
  • Communicating: Impaired CSF absorption (e.g., SAH, meningitis).
  • Non‑communicating: Obstruction within ventricles (e.g., aqueductal stenosis, tumor).
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus: Triad: gait apraxia, incontinence, dementia; enlarged ventricles, normal opening pressure.
