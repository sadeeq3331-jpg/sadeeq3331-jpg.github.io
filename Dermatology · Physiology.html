1. Epidermis
The epidermis is the outermost layer of the skin, derived from ectoderm. It is a stratified squamous keratinized epithelium composed mainly of keratinocytes.
Layers (from deep to superficial)
  • Stratum basale (germinativum): Single layer of columnar cells attached to basement membrane; site of mitotically active keratinocytes; contains melanocytes and Merkel cells.
  • Stratum spinosum: Several layers of polyhedral cells connected by desmosomes; contains Langerhans cells.
  • Stratum granulosum: Flattened cells with keratohyalin granules (contain profilaggrin) and lamellar bodies (lipids).
  • Stratum lucidum: Clear layer present only in thick skin (palms, soles); composed of eleidin.
  • Stratum corneum: 15‑30 layers of anucleate, flattened corneocytes filled with keratin; surrounded by lipid matrix; main barrier.
Keratinization: As keratinocytes move upward, they undergo terminal differentiation, losing nuclei and organelles, and accumulating keratin filaments cross‑linked by filaggrin. The cornified envelope (involucrin, loricrin) forms beneath the plasma membrane.

Psoriasis: Hyperproliferation and abnormal differentiation leads to thickened epidermis, parakeratosis (retained nuclei in stratum corneum).

2. Dermis
The dermis is derived from mesoderm and provides structural support, nutrition, and sensation.
Layers
  • Papillary dermis: Thin, loose connective tissue with collagen type III, elastic fibers; contains capillary loops and Meissner corpuscles.
  • Reticular dermis: Dense irregular connective tissue with thick collagen type I bundles and coarse elastic fibers; contains blood vessels, nerves, appendages.
Extracellular matrix components: Collagen (type I, III), elastin, fibrillin, proteoglycans (decorin, versican), glycoproteins (fibronectin, laminin).

Ehlers‑Danlos syndrome: Defects in collagen synthesis → hyperextensible skin, joint hypermobility.

Marfan syndrome: Fibrillin defect → skin hyperextensibility, striae.

3. Hypodermis (Subcutaneous Tissue)
Composed mainly of adipocytes arranged in lobules separated by fibrous septa. Functions: insulation, energy storage, cushioning, and anchoring skin to underlying structures. Contains larger blood vessels and nerves.
4. Hair Follicles
Hair follicles are complex structures derived from epidermis, extending into dermis/hypodermis.
  • Hair shaft: Medulla, cortex, cuticle.
  • Inner root sheath: Surrounds shaft, keratinizes.
  • Outer root sheath: Continuation of epidermis.
  • Bulb: Matrix cells (proliferative), melanocytes, dermal papilla (mesenchymal).
Hair cycle: Anagen (growth), catagen (regression), telogen (resting), exogen (shedding).

Alopecia areata: Autoimmune attack on hair follicles → patchy hair loss.

5. Nails
  • Nail plate: Hard keratin produced by nail matrix.
  • Nail bed: Epidermis beneath nail plate.
  • Lunula: Visible portion of matrix.
  • Hyponychium: Seals nail bed at fingertip.
  • Eponychium (cuticle): Proximal fold.

Nails grow ~0.1 mm/day (faster in summer, younger age).

6. Sebaceous & Sweat Glands
Sebaceous glands
  • Holocrine secretion (sebum) into hair follicle.
  • Sebum: triglycerides, wax esters, squalene, cholesterol.
  • Androgen‑sensitive; largest on face, chest, back.
Eccrine sweat glands
  • Distributed over body; simple coiled tubular.
  • Secrete hypotonic fluid (water, electrolytes) for thermoregulation.
  • Cholinergic sympathetic innervation.
Apocrine sweat glands
  • Axillae, anogenital; open into hair follicle.
  • Secrete viscous, odorless fluid; bacteria produce odor.
  • Become active at puberty (androgen‑sensitive).
7. Barrier Function
The stratum corneum forms the primary barrier (brick and mortar model). Corneocytes (bricks) are embedded in lipid matrix (mortar) of ceramides, cholesterol, free fatty acids. Tight junctions in stratum granulosum contribute.
Transepidermal water loss (TEWL): Measure of barrier integrity; increased in eczema, burns.

Atopic dermatitis: Filaggrin mutations impair barrier, leading to TEWL, allergen penetration, inflammation.

8. Skin Immunology
  • Keratinocytes: Produce antimicrobial peptides (defensins, cathelicidins), cytokines (IL‑1, IL‑6, TNF‑α).
  • Langerhans cells: Dendritic cells in epidermis; capture antigen, migrate to lymph nodes, present to T cells.
  • Dermal dendritic cells, macrophages, mast cells, T cells (especially memory T cells).

Skin‑associated lymphoid tissue (SALT) mediates cutaneous immune responses.

9. Thermoregulation
  • Eccrine sweat evaporation cools body.
  • Arteriovenous anastomoses in acral skin regulate heat loss via sympathetic tone.
  • Piloerection (arrector pili muscles) minimal in humans.
10. Sensation
  • Free nerve endings: Pain, temperature, itch (C‑fibers).
  • Meissner corpuscles: Light touch (glabrous skin).
  • Pacinian corpuscles: Vibration, pressure (deep dermis).
  • Merkel cells: Light touch, texture (slow adapting).
11. Vitamin D Synthesis
UVB (290‑315 nm) converts 7‑dehydrocholesterol in keratinocytes to cholecalciferol (vitamin D3). Then hydroxylated in liver (25‑OH) and kidney (1,25‑OH) to active form. Essential for calcium homeostasis.
12. Melanocytes & Melanin
  • Melanocytes derived from neural crest; reside in stratum basale and hair bulbs.
  • Melanosomes synthesize melanin (eumelanin brown‑black, pheomelanin yellow‑red).
  • Transferred to keratinocytes via dendrites (each melanocyte supplies ~36 keratinocytes).
  • Melanin protects against UV damage.
13. Pigmentation Disorders
  • Vitiligo: Autoimmune destruction of melanocytes → depigmented patches.
  • Albinism: Genetic defect in tyrosinase or melanin synthesis → hypopigmentation, vision problems.
  • Freckles (ephelides): Increased melanin production, not melanocyte number.
  • Melasma: Hyperpigmentation (hormonal, UV).
14. Wound Healing
Phases: hemostasis, inflammation, proliferation (granulation tissue, re‑epithelialization, angiogenesis), remodeling (scar formation).
  • Healing by primary intention: Clean edges, minimal scarring.
  • Healing by secondary intention: Open wound, granulation tissue fills defect.
  • Healing by tertiary intention: Delayed closure.

Keloid: Excessive scar beyond wound boundaries; common in darker skin.
