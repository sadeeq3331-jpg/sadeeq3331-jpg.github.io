1. β₂‑Adrenergic Agonists
Mechanism of Action

β₂‑agonists stimulate β₂‑adrenergic receptors located primarily on airway smooth muscle cells. These receptors are Gs‑coupled, leading to activation of adenylyl cyclase, which increases intracellular cyclic AMP (cAMP). Elevated cAMP activates protein kinase A (PKA), which phosphorylates multiple targets, resulting in:

  • Smooth muscle relaxation – PKA inhibits myosin light‑chain kinase and promotes K+ channel opening, causing hyperpolarization and relaxation.
  • Inhibition of mast cell mediator release (e.g., histamine, leukotrienes).
  • Increased mucociliary clearance – enhanced ciliary beating.
  • Decreased vascular permeability and inhibition of cholinergic neurotransmission.
Short‑Acting β₂‑Agonists (SABA)
  • Examples: Albuterol (salbutamol), levalbuterol, terbutaline, pirbuterol.
  • Onset: Rapid (within 5 minutes).
  • Duration: 4‑6 hours.
  • Clinical uses: Acute relief of bronchospasm (asthma, COPD), prevention of exercise‑induced bronchospasm (taken 15‑20 minutes before exercise).
  • Pharmacokinetics: Inhaled (minimal systemic absorption), oral (available but more side effects), IV (rare).
  • Adverse effects: Tachycardia (β₁ cross‑stimulation at high doses), tremor (skeletal muscle β₂), hypokalemia (due to intracellular shift), nervousness, headache.
Long‑Acting β₂‑Agonists (LABA)
  • Examples: Salmeterol, formoterol, indacaterol, olodaterol, vilanterol.
  • Onset: Salmeterol is slow (30‑60 min); formoterol and indacaterol are rapid (5‑15 min).
  • Duration: >12 hours (some once‑daily).
  • Clinical uses: Maintenance therapy for asthma (always combined with inhaled corticosteroid) and COPD (can be used alone or with LAMA/ICS).
  • Important warning: LABA monotherapy in asthma increases the risk of severe exacerbations and asthma‑related death – must be used with an ICS.
  • Adverse effects: Similar to SABA; long‑term use may lead to receptor downregulation (tolerance).

Memory aid: “SABA for rescue, LABA for control – but only with ICS.”

2. Anticholinergics
Mechanism of Action

Anticholinergics block muscarinic receptors (especially M3 subtype) on bronchial smooth muscle. Acetylcholine released from parasympathetic nerves normally causes bronchoconstriction and mucus secretion. By competitively antagonizing these receptors, anticholinergics produce bronchodilation. They are functionally competitive antagonists – they reduce the effect of acetylcholine but do not block the action of other bronchodilators.

Short‑Acting (SAMA)
  • Ipratropium bromide: Quaternary ammonium compound, poorly absorbed, duration 4‑6 hours.
  • Onset: 15‑30 minutes.
  • Uses: COPD maintenance, acute asthma (added to SABA in emergency department), rhinorrhea.
  • Side effects: Dry mouth, cough, bitter taste; rarely urinary retention or worsening of narrow‑angle glaucoma (if nebulized mist contacts eyes).
Long‑Acting (LAMA)
  • Examples: Tiotropium, umeclidinium, glycopyrronium, aclidinium.
  • Duration: 24 hours (once‑daily) for most; aclidinium is twice‑daily.
  • Uses: First‑line maintenance for COPD; also add‑on therapy for asthma not controlled with ICS‑LABA.
  • Side effects: Similar to ipratropium; less systemic because of minimal absorption.

Clinical pearl: In COPD, LAMAs reduce exacerbations and improve lung function; they are often combined with LABAs or ICS in fixed‑dose inhalers.

3. Methylxanthines
Mechanism of Action

Methylxanthines (e.g., theophylline, aminophylline) have multiple proposed mechanisms:

  • Non‑selective phosphodiesterase (PDE) inhibition: Increases cAMP and cGMP in airway smooth muscle and inflammatory cells.
  • Adenosine receptor antagonism: Prevents adenosine‑induced bronchoconstriction and mast cell activation.
  • Increased histone deacetylase (HDAC) activity: Restores corticosteroid sensitivity in smokers.
  • Enhanced diaphragmatic contractility: May improve respiratory muscle function.
Pharmacokinetics
  • Narrow therapeutic index: 10‑20 μg/mL (55‑110 μmol/L). Toxicity common above 20 μg/mL.
  • Metabolized by CYP1A2 and CYP3A4; many drug interactions.
  • Factors increasing levels: Liver disease, heart failure, cimetidine, erythromycin, fluconazole, ciprofloxacin.
  • Factors decreasing levels: Smoking, rifampin, phenytoin, carbamazepine.
Clinical Uses
  • Add‑on therapy for severe asthma or COPD (third‑line).
  • Neonatal apnea (caffeine is preferred).
Adverse Effects
  • Mild: nausea, vomiting, insomnia, nervousness.
  • Severe (toxicity): tachycardia, arrhythmias, seizures, hypokalemia, hyperglycemia.

Memory aid: “Theophylline toxicity – from caffeine‑like symptoms to seizures.”

4. Corticosteroids
Mechanism of Action

Corticosteroids diffuse across the cell membrane and bind to cytoplasmic glucocorticoid receptors. The complex translocates to the nucleus and modulates gene transcription:

  • Transrepression: Decreased transcription of pro‑inflammatory genes (cytokines, chemokines, adhesion molecules, inflammatory enzymes).
  • Transactivation: Increased transcription of anti‑inflammatory proteins (lipocortin‑1, IL‑10, IκB‑α).
  • Non‑genomic effects: Rapid (minutes) via membrane‑bound receptors.

In airways, they reduce eosinophil, mast cell, and lymphocyte numbers; decrease mucus secretion; and restore β₂‑receptor sensitivity.

Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS)
  • Examples: Fluticasone, budesonide, beclomethasone, mometasone, ciclesonide.
  • Pharmacokinetics: High first‑pass metabolism to minimize systemic effects; delivered via inhaler or nebulizer.
  • Uses: First‑line controller for persistent asthma; in COPD for patients with frequent exacerbations.
  • Adverse effects (local): Oropharyngeal candidiasis, dysphonia (hoarseness) – minimized by using a spacer and rinsing mouth after inhalation.
  • Adverse effects (systemic): Minimal at low doses; high doses may cause adrenal suppression, osteoporosis, growth suppression in children.
Systemic Corticosteroids
  • Examples: Prednisone, prednisolone, methylprednisolone (oral or IV).
  • Uses: Acute severe asthma exacerbations, COPD exacerbations, interstitial lung diseases.
  • Short‑term side effects: Hyperglycemia, mood changes, insomnia, fluid retention.
  • Long‑term side effects: Osteoporosis, adrenal suppression, cataracts, easy bruising, immunosuppression.

Important: Always use the lowest effective ICS dose and reassess regularly. In acute exacerbations, early systemic corticosteroids reduce hospitalizations and relapses.

5. Leukotriene Modifiers
Leukotriene Synthesis and Action

Leukotrienes (LT) are inflammatory mediators derived from arachidonic acid via the 5‑lipoxygenase (5‑LO) pathway. LTC4, LTD4, and LTE4 (cysteinyl leukotrienes) cause bronchoconstriction, increased vascular permeability, and eosinophil recruitment. They are key mediators in asthma, especially allergic and aspirin‑sensitive forms.

5‑Lipoxygenase Inhibitor
  • Zileuton: Inhibits leukotriene synthesis.
  • Uses: Mild persistent asthma, aspirin‑exacerbated respiratory disease.
  • Side effects: Hepatotoxicity (LFT monitoring required), drug interactions (inhibits CYP1A2).
CysLT₁ Receptor Antagonists
  • Montelukast, zafirlukast, pranlukast: Block LTD4 receptors.
  • Uses: Exercise‑induced bronchospasm, allergic rhinitis, aspirin‑sensitive asthma, mild persistent asthma.
  • Side effects: Generally well tolerated; rare neuropsychiatric events (agitation, depression) with montelukast (FDA black box warning).

Memory aid: “Zileuton – watch the liver; montelukast – watch the mood.”

6. Mast Cell Stabilizers

Cromolyn sodium and nedocromil sodium are believed to inhibit degranulation of mast cells by stabilizing cell membranes, possibly by blocking chloride channels. They prevent the release of histamine and other mediators.

  • Uses: Prophylactic treatment of asthma (not for acute attacks), exercise‑induced bronchospasm, allergic conjunctivitis, and rhinitis.
  • Onset: Several weeks needed for maximal effect.
  • Safety: Extremely safe – minimal side effects (occasional cough, unpleasant taste with nedocromil).

They are rarely used now in asthma due to availability of more effective controllers, but remain an option for children and mild cases.

7. Biologics

Biologic agents target specific inflammatory pathways in severe asthma not controlled with conventional therapy.

Anti‑IgE (Omalizumab)
  • Mechanism: Humanized monoclonal antibody that binds free IgE, preventing its interaction with high‑affinity receptors on mast cells and basophils.
  • Uses: Moderate‑to‑severe allergic asthma with elevated IgE levels; chronic idiopathic urticaria.
  • Administration: Subcutaneous every 2‑4 weeks.
  • Side effects: Injection site reactions, anaphylaxis (rare), increased risk of parasitic infections.
Anti‑IL5 (Mepolizumab, Reslizumab, Benralizumab)
  • Mechanism: Block IL‑5, a key cytokine for eosinophil maturation, activation, and survival. Benralizumab binds the IL‑5 receptor α, inducing antibody‑dependent cell‑mediated cytotoxicity of eosinophils.
  • Uses: Severe eosinophilic asthma (blood eosinophils >150‑300 cells/μL).
  • Administration: Subcutaneous (mepolizumab, benralizumab) or IV (reslizumab).
  • Side effects: Headache, injection site reactions, rare hypersensitivity.
Anti‑IL4/IL13 (Dupilumab)
  • Mechanism: Blocks the IL‑4 receptor α, inhibiting both IL‑4 and IL‑13 signaling.
  • Uses: Moderate‑to‑severe asthma (eosinophilic or oral corticosteroid‑dependent), atopic dermatitis, nasal polyps.
  • Side effects: Injection site reactions, eosinophilia, conjunctivitis.

Note: Biologics are reserved for severe asthma; they are expensive and require specialist supervision.

8. Antitussives

Antitussives suppress cough by acting on the cough center in the medulla or on peripheral cough receptors.

  • Dextromethorphan: Non‑opioid, acts on sigma receptors and NMDA receptors; OTC. May cause dizziness, drowsiness.
  • Codeine: Opioid, acts on μ‑opioid receptors in cough center; controlled substance. Side effects: sedation, constipation, risk of dependence.
  • Benzonatate: Local anesthetic derivative that anesthetizes stretch receptors in the respiratory tract; non‑narcotic. Side effects: sedation, nausea, may cause systemic toxicity if chewed.

Warning: Antitussives are contraindicated in productive cough (retained secretions). Codeine should not be used in children (risk of respiratory depression).

9. Expectorants & Mucolytics

Expectorants increase the volume and reduce the viscosity of respiratory tract secretions, making cough more productive.

  • Guaifenesin: Increases respiratory tract fluid, decreasing mucus viscosity. Often used OTC. Minimal side effects.

Mucolytics chemically break down mucus polymers.

  • Acetylcysteine (N‑acetylcysteine): Breaks disulfide bonds in mucoproteins, reducing viscosity. Used in COPD, cystic fibrosis, and as an antidote for acetaminophen overdose. Nebulized or oral. May cause bronchospasm (use with bronchodilator).
  • Dornase alfa (rhDNase): Recombinant human DNase I; cleaves DNA released from degenerating neutrophils, reducing sputum viscosity in cystic fibrosis. Administered via nebulizer. Side effects: pharyngitis, voice changes, rash.
  • Hypertonic saline (7%): Osmotic mucolytic; used in cystic fibrosis to improve mucociliary clearance.

Memory aid: “NAC – for mucus and paracetamol overdose; dornase – for CF.”

10. Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension Drugs

Pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) is a progressive disease characterized by vasoconstriction and vascular remodeling. Drug therapy targets three main pathways: prostacyclin, endothelin, and nitric oxide.

Prostacyclin Pathway
  • Prostacyclin (PGI₂) analogues: Epoprostenol (continuous IV, half‑life 3‑5 min), treprostinil (IV/SC/inhaled/oral), iloprost (inhaled, IV).
  • Mechanism: Activate IP receptors → ↑ cAMP → vasodilation, anti‑proliferation, anti‑platelet.
  • Selexipag: Oral prodrug of a selective IP receptor agonist.
Endothelin Pathway
  • Endothelin receptor antagonists (ERA): Bosentan (dual ETA/ETB antagonist), ambrisentan (selective ETA), macitentan (dual).
  • Mechanism: Block vasoconstrictive and mitogenic effects of endothelin‑1.
  • Side effects: Hepatotoxicity (bosentan – monthly LFTs), peripheral edema, teratogenicity (pregnancy category X).
Nitric Oxide Pathway
  • Phosphodiesterase‑5 (PDE‑5) inhibitors: Sildenafil, tadalafil – increase cGMP by preventing its breakdown, potentiating NO‑mediated vasodilation.
  • Guanylate cyclase stimulator: Riociguat – stimulates soluble guanylate cyclase directly, increasing cGMP independent of NO.
  • Side effects: Headache, flushing, dyspepsia, hypotension; contraindicated with nitrates.

Combination therapy: Often used to target multiple pathways. All patients should be managed in specialized centers.

11. Smoking Cessation Aids

Smoking cessation reduces the risk of respiratory and cardiovascular diseases. Pharmacotherapy doubles quit rates.

  • Nicotine replacement therapy (NRT): Patch, gum, lozenge, inhaler, nasal spray. Provides nicotine without carcinogens. Side effects: skin irritation (patch), mouth irritation (gum).
  • Varenicline: Partial agonist at α₄β₂ nicotinic receptors – reduces cravings and withdrawal. Common side effects: nausea, vivid dreams, headache. FDA black box for neuropsychiatric events (but evidence suggests risk is low).
  • Bupropion (Zyban): Atypical antidepressant; inhibits dopamine and norepinephrine reuptake. Also reduces cravings. Contraindicated in seizure disorders, eating disorders. Side effects: insomnia, dry mouth, lower seizure threshold.
  • Combination therapy: NRT + varenicline or bupropion may be more effective.

Note: Electronic cigarettes are not FDA‑approved as cessation aids; they may help some but long‑term safety is unknown.

12. Surfactant Preparations

Pulmonary surfactant is a mixture of phospholipids and proteins that reduces surface tension, preventing alveolar collapse. Deficiency in preterm infants leads to neonatal respiratory distress syndrome (RDS).

Types
  • Natural surfactants: Beractant (Survanta), poractant alfa (Curosurf), calfactant (Infasurf) – derived from bovine or porcine lungs.
  • Synthetic surfactants: Lucinactant (Surfaxin) – contains synthetic phospholipids and a peptide analogue of surfactant protein B.
Mechanism

Instilled intratracheally, they spread over the alveolar surface, reduce surface tension, increase lung compliance, and improve oxygenation.

Uses
  • Prophylaxis and rescue therapy for neonatal RDS in premature infants.
  • Occasionally used in meconium aspiration syndrome and ARDS (off‑label).

Administration: Given via endotracheal tube shortly after birth. May transiently cause bradycardia, oxygen desaturation, or reflux into the tube.
