1. Introduction to Renal Pathology

Renal pathology encompasses diseases affecting the glomeruli, tubules, interstitium, blood vessels, and collecting system. Major clinical presentations include:

  • Nephrotic syndrome: Heavy proteinuria (>3.5 g/day), hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia.
  • Nephritic syndrome: Hematuria, RBC casts, hypertension, mild proteinuria, oliguria.
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI) and chronic kidney disease (CKD).

2. Nephrotic Syndrome

Nephrotic syndrome is caused by increased permeability of the glomerular filtration barrier to proteins. Common causes:

Minimal Change Disease (MCD)
  • Epidemiology: Most common cause in children (80%).
  • Pathology: Light microscopy normal; electron microscopy shows diffuse effacement of podocyte foot processes. Immunofluorescence negative.
  • Clinical: Sudden onset nephrotic syndrome; selective proteinuria (mostly albumin).
  • Treatment/Prognosis: Excellent response to corticosteroids.
Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)
  • Epidemiology: Common cause in adults, especially African Americans.
  • Pathology: Segmental sclerosis affecting some glomeruli. Variants: collapsing, tip, cellular.
  • Pathogenesis: Podocyte injury; some cases due to circulating permeability factor.
  • Clinical: Nephrotic syndrome, often resistant to steroids; progresses to ESRD.
Membranous Nephropathy (MN)
  • Etiology: Autoimmune; antibodies against phospholipase A2 receptor (PLA2R) on podocytes. Secondary causes: infections, drugs, malignancies.
  • Pathology: Diffuse thickening of GBM (spike and dome appearance on silver stain). Immunofluorescence: granular IgG and C3 along capillary loops.
  • Clinical: Nephrotic syndrome; can progress slowly.
Membranoproliferative Glomerulonephritis (MPGN)
  • Now classified by immunofluorescence: immune‑complex mediated (IgG + C3) vs complement mediated (C3 glomerulopathy).
  • Pathology: Mesangial proliferation, endocapillary proliferation, GBM duplication (tram‑track).
  • Associated with: HCV, autoimmune diseases, dysproteinemias.

Clinical pearl: MCD → podocyte foot process effacement; FSGS → segmental sclerosis; MN → subepithelial deposits with spikes.

3. Nephritic Syndrome

Nephritic syndrome results from glomerular inflammation. Hallmarks: hematuria, RBC casts, hypertension, oliguria.

Post‑streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN)
  • Etiology: Immune complex deposition following group A strep infection (pharyngitis or skin). Latency 1‑3 weeks.
  • Pathology: Diffuse proliferative GN with neutrophil infiltration, subepithelial humps (electron‑dense deposits). Immunofluorescence: granular IgG and C3 (starry sky).
  • Clinical: Acute nephritic syndrome; often resolves in children, may progress in adults.
Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)
  • Characterized by crescents in Bowman's space (crescentic GN).
  • Type I (Anti‑GBM disease): Linear IgG along GBM (Goodpasture syndrome, often with lung hemorrhage).
  • Type II (Immune complex): Granular deposits; e.g., post‑infectious, lupus, IgA nephropathy.
  • Type III (Pauci‑immune): Few or no deposits; ANCA‑associated (granulomatosis with polyangiitis, microscopic polyangiitis).

Memory aid: RPGN types: I = linear (Anti‑GBM), II = lumpy (immune complex), III = ANCA (pauci‑immune).

4. Specific Glomerulonephritides
IgA Nephropathy (Berger Disease)
  • Most common GN worldwide. Characterized by mesangial IgA deposits.
  • Pathology: Mesangial proliferation, IgA ± IgG, C3 on IF. May show crescents.
  • Clinical: Hematuria (often following mucosal infections), can progress to CKD.
Lupus Nephritis (WHO/ISN Classes)
  • Class I: Minimal mesangial; Class II: Mesangial proliferative; Class III: Focal; Class IV: Diffuse; Class V: Membranous; Class VI: Advanced sclerosing.
  • Full house immunofluorescence (IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, C1q).
Alport Syndrome
  • X‑linked or AR defect in type IV collagen (α3, α4, α5 chains).
  • Pathology: Thin GBM early, later splitting and lamellation.
  • Extrarenal: sensorineural hearing loss, ocular abnormalities.
Thin Basement Membrane Disease
  • Benign familial hematuria; diffuse thinning of GBM (no progression).
5. Tubulointerstitial Diseases
Acute Tubular Injury (ATI) / Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)
  • Ischemic: Tubular necrosis, patchy loss of brush border, mitotic figures.
  • Toxic: Direct injury by drugs (aminoglycosides), heavy metals, myoglobin, contrast.
Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN)
  • Causes: Drugs (NSAIDs, penicillins, rifampin), infections, autoimmune.
  • Pathology: Interstitial edema, lymphocytic infiltrate, eosinophils (drug‑induced). Tubulitis.
Chronic Tubulointerstitial Nephritis
  • Results from prolonged injury; interstitial fibrosis, tubular atrophy.
  • Causes: Analgesic nephropathy, lead toxicity, reflux nephropathy.

Clinical pearl: In AIN, patients present with fever, rash, eosinophilia, and eosinophiluria (if drug‑induced).

6. Vascular Diseases of the Kidney
Benign Nephrosclerosis
  • Associated with chronic hypertension. Arteriosclerosis (fibrointimal thickening) of small arteries, hyaline arteriolosclerosis.
Malignant Hypertension
  • Hyperplastic arteriolosclerosis (onion‑skin), fibrinoid necrosis of arterioles. May cause petechial hemorrhages on kidney surface (flea‑bitten appearance).
Renal Artery Stenosis
  • Atherosclerotic (older men) or fibromuscular dysplasia (young women).
  • Ischemic nephropathy; can lead to flash pulmonary edema.
Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TMA)
  • Includes thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP), hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS).
  • Pathology: Fibrin thrombi in glomeruli and arterioles; endothelial injury, thickening of vessel walls.
7. Cystic Kidney Diseases
Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD)
  • Genetics: PKD1 (85%) or PKD2 mutation.
  • Pathology: Numerous bilateral cysts of varying size; cysts may also occur in liver, pancreas.
  • Clinical: Hypertension, hematuria, flank pain, progressive CKD. Extrarenal: berry aneurysms, mitral valve prolapse.
Autosomal Recessive Polycystic Kidney Disease (ARPKD)
  • Presents in infancy; enlarged kidneys with small fusiform cysts (ectatic collecting ducts). Associated with congenital hepatic fibrosis.
Medullary Sponge Kidney
  • Dilated collecting ducts in medulla; predisposes to nephrolithiasis.
Simple Cysts
  • Common in elderly; benign, incidental.
8. Renal Tumors
Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC)
  • Clear cell (75%): VHL mutation, 3p loss; cells with clear cytoplasm, alveolar pattern.
  • Papillary (10‑15%): Chromophilic cells forming papillae; associated with MET mutations.
  • Chromophobe (5%): Large pale cells, perinuclear halos; better prognosis.
  • Collecting duct (rare): aggressive.
Oncocytoma
  • Benign tumor of oncocytes (eosinophilic cells packed with mitochondria).
Angiomyolipoma
  • Benign tumor composed of blood vessels, smooth muscle, adipose tissue; associated with tuberous sclerosis.
Wilms Tumor (Nephroblastoma)
  • Most common renal malignancy in children; triphasic (blastema, stroma, epithelium). Associated with WT1 mutations, WAGR, Denys‑Drash, Beckwith‑Wiedemann.
Urothelial Carcinoma of Renal Pelvis
  • Transitional cell carcinoma; risk factors: smoking, aristolochic acid (Chinese herb), phenacetin.
9. Urinary Tract Obstruction & Reflux
Obstructive Uropathy
  • Can be congenital (UPJ obstruction) or acquired (stones, tumors, BPH).
  • Hydronephrosis: dilation of renal pelvis and calyces; if prolonged, leads to hydronephrotic atrophy.
Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR)
  • Incompetent ureterovesical valve; leads to reflux of urine, recurrent pyelonephritis, and reflux nephropathy.
  • Reflux nephropathy: coarse cortical scarring, often at poles.
10. Renal Transplant Pathology
Hyperacute Rejection
  • Occurs minutes to hours; preformed antibodies (ABO or HLA). Thrombosis and infarction.
Acute Rejection
  • Cellular (T‑cell mediated): Interstitial infiltrate, tubulitis.
  • Humoral (antibody‑mediated): Glomerulitis, peritubular capillaritis, C4d deposition in peritubular capillaries.
Chronic Rejection / Transplant Glomerulopathy
  • Double contours of GBM, interstitial fibrosis, tubular atrophy.
Other Transplant Pathology
  • BK virus nephropathy (viral inclusions in tubular cells).
  • Calcineurin inhibitor toxicity (isometric vacuolization, hyaline arteriolosclerosis).
