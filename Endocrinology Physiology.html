1. Overview of the Endocrine System

The endocrine system consists of glands that secrete hormones into the bloodstream to regulate distant target organs. Hormones are chemical messengers that influence metabolism, growth, reproduction, and homeostasis.

Classification of Hormones
  • Peptide hormones: Water‑soluble, act via surface receptors (e.g., insulin, GH, TRH).
  • Steroid hormones: Lipid‑soluble, derived from cholesterol, act via intracellular receptors (e.g., cortisol, aldosterone, sex steroids).
  • Amine hormones: Derived from tyrosine (thyroid hormones, catecholamines).
Feedback Loops

Most endocrine axes are regulated by negative feedback. For example, thyroid hormone inhibits TRH and TSH secretion. Positive feedback is rare (e.g., LH surge before ovulation).

Pearl: Disorders can arise from hormone excess (e.g., Cushing's) or deficiency (e.g., Addison's).

Anterior Pituitary Hormones
  • GH (growth hormone): Promotes growth via IGF‑1; increases protein synthesis, lipolysis, and blood glucose.
  • TSH (thyroid‑stimulating hormone): Stimulates thyroid hormone synthesis.
  • ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone): Stimulates adrenal cortisol release.
  • FSH & LH: Regulate gonadal function.
  • Prolactin: Stimulates milk production; inhibited by dopamine.
Posterior Pituitary Hormones
  • ADH (vasopressin): Increases water reabsorption in collecting ducts.
  • Oxytocin: Stimulates uterine contractions and milk ejection.

Disorders: Hypopituitarism (Sheehan's), acromegaly, prolactinoma, diabetes insipidus.

Memory aid: “GOAT FLAP” for anterior pituitary: GH, Oxytocin (actually posterior), ACTH, TSH, FSH, LH, Prolactin (but oxytocin is posterior). Better: “FLAT PiG” – FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH, Prolactin, GH.

3. Thyroid Gland

Thyroid produces T₃ (triiodothyronine) and T₄ (thyroxine). T₃ is the active form; T₄ is a prohormone. Synthesis requires iodine and thyroglobulin.

Synthesis Steps (Iodine trapping, oxidation, iodination, coupling)
  • Iodide trapping: NIS (Na/I symporter) concentrates iodide in follicular cells.
  • Oxidation: Thyroid peroxidase (TPO) oxidizes I⁻ to I⁰.
  • Iodination: Tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin become MIT and DIT.
  • Coupling: MIT + DIT → T₃; DIT + DIT → T₄.
Regulation

Hypothalamus releases TRH → anterior pituitary releases TSH → TSH stimulates thyroid hormone synthesis and release. Thyroid hormones exert negative feedback on TRH and TSH.

Physiologic Effects
  • Increase basal metabolic rate, calorigenesis, heart rate, sensitivity to catecholamines.
  • Promote growth and CNS development (critical in infancy).

Disorders: Hyperthyroidism (Graves’ disease), hypothyroidism (Hashimoto’s, iodine deficiency), goiter.

4. Parathyroid & Calcium Homeostasis

Calcium homeostasis is regulated by parathyroid hormone (PTH), vitamin D, and calcitonin.

HormoneSourceEffect on Ca²⁺
PTHParathyroid chief cells↑ serum Ca²⁺ (bone resorption, kidney reabsorption, ↑ calcitriol)
Calcitriol (1,25(OH)₂D)Kidney (activated)↑ intestinal Ca²⁺ and PO₄³⁻ absorption
CalcitoninParafollicular cells (C cells) of thyroid↓ serum Ca²⁺ (inhibits bone resorption)
PTH Actions
  • Bone: Stimulates osteoclast activity (indirectly via RANKL on osteoblasts) → Ca²⁺ release.
  • Kidney: Increases Ca²⁺ reabsorption in DCT, inhibits PO₄³⁻ reabsorption (phosphaturia), and stimulates 1α‑hydroxylase → calcitriol.

Disorders: Hyperparathyroidism (↑PTH → hypercalcemia), hypoparathyroidism (↓PTH → hypocalcemia, tetany). Vitamin D deficiency → rickets/osteomalacia.

5. Adrenal Gland
Adrenal Cortex (Zones)
  • Zona glomerulosa: Mineralocorticoids (aldosterone) – regulated by angiotensin II and K⁺; ↑ Na⁺ reabsorption, K⁺ excretion.
  • Zona fasciculata: Glucocorticoids (cortisol) – regulated by ACTH; ↑ blood glucose, anti‑inflammatory, catabolic.
  • Zona reticularis: Androgens (DHEA) – weak androgens, converted peripherally.
Adrenal Medulla

Secretes catecholamines (epinephrine 80%, norepinephrine 20%) in response to sympathetic stimulation (fight‑or‑flight).

Disorders: Cushing's syndrome (cortisol excess), Addison's disease (cortisol/aldosterone deficiency), Conn's syndrome (aldosteronism), pheochromocytoma (catecholamine excess).

6. Endocrine Pancreas & Diabetes

The islets of Langerhans contain α‑cells (glucagon), β‑cells (insulin), δ‑cells (somatostatin), and PP‑cells (pancreatic polypeptide).

HormoneEffects
Insulin↑ glucose uptake (muscle, fat), ↑ glycogenesis, ↑ lipogenesis, ↑ protein synthesis, ↓ gluconeogenesis, ↓ lipolysis, ↓ ketogenesis.
Glucagon↑ glycogenolysis, ↑ gluconeogenesis, ↑ lipolysis, ↑ ketogenesis – opposite of insulin.
SomatostatinParacrine inhibitor of insulin and glucagon release.
Regulation of Insulin Release
  • Glucose enters β‑cells via GLUT2 → metabolized → ↑ATP/ADP ratio → closes KATP channels → depolarization → opens voltage‑gated Ca²⁺ channels → insulin exocytosis.
  • Also stimulated by incretins (GIP, GLP‑1).

Diabetes mellitus: Type 1 (autoimmune β‑cell destruction), Type 2 (insulin resistance + relative deficiency). Complications: microvascular (retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy) and macrovascular.

7. Male Reproductive Hormones

Hypothalamic GnRH stimulates pituitary LH and FSH.

  • LH acts on Leydig cells → testosterone.
  • FSH acts on Sertoli cells → spermatogenesis.
  • Testosterone promotes male secondary sex characteristics, spermatogenesis, anabolic effects. Negative feedback on LH.
  • Inhibin from Sertoli cells inhibits FSH.

Disorders: Hypogonadism, Klinefelter syndrome (XXY), 5α‑reductase deficiency (impaired DHT synthesis).

8. Female Reproductive Hormones
Menstrual Cycle Phases
  • Follicular phase: FSH stimulates follicle growth → estradiol from granulosa cells → endometrial proliferation. Estradiol positive feedback triggers LH surge.
  • Ovulation: LH surge triggers oocyte release.
  • Luteal phase: Corpus luteum secretes progesterone and estradiol → endometrial secretory phase. If no pregnancy, corpus luteum degenerates, hormone levels drop, menstruation.
Hormones
  • Estrogen: Proliferative effects, feedback on GnRH/LH/FSH (negative except mid‑cycle positive).
  • Progesterone: Secretory endometrium, thermogenic, maintains pregnancy.
  • hCG: From placenta, maintains corpus luteum in early pregnancy.

Disorders: PCOS, amenorrhea, menopause, infertility.

9. Pineal Gland & Circadian Rhythms

The pineal gland secretes melatonin, which regulates circadian rhythms. Synthesis from serotonin is stimulated by darkness and inhibited by light (via retinohypothalamic tract and suprachiasmatic nucleus). Melatonin induces sleep and influences seasonal breeding in some animals.

10. Local Hormones (Eicosanoids, etc.)

Eicosanoids: Derived from arachidonic acid via COX (prostaglandins, thromboxane, prostacyclin) or LOX (leukotrienes). Act locally as paracrine/autocrine signals.

  • Prostaglandins: Diverse effects: inflammation, pain, fever, cytoprotection (PGE₂), platelet aggregation (TXA₂), vasodilation (PGI₂).
  • Leukotrienes: Bronchoconstriction, chemotaxis.

Other local mediators: Histamine, serotonin, bradykinin, platelet‑activating factor.
