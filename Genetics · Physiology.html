1. DNA Structure & Replication
Double helix (Watson & Crick): Two antiparallel strands (5'→3' and 3'→5') held by hydrogen bonds between complementary bases: A‑T (2 bonds), G‑C (3 bonds). Sugar‑phosphate backbone.
  • Nucleotides: phosphate, deoxyribose, nitrogenous base (purines: adenine, guanine; pyrimidines: cytosine, thymine).
  • Genome organization: Nuclear DNA (3.2 billion bp) organized into chromosomes (46, 23 pairs). Mitochondrial DNA (16.5 kb, circular).
DNA Replication
  • Semiconservative: Each daughter duplex contains one parental and one new strand.
  • Origin of replication: Replication forks bidirectional.
  • Enzymes:
    • Helicase – unwinds DNA.
    • DNA polymerase α/δ/ε – synthesize new strands; require primer (RNA).
    • Primase – RNA primer synthesis.
    • Ligase – joins Okazaki fragments.
    • Topoisomerase – relieves supercoiling.
Leading strand continuous, lagging strand discontinuous (Okazaki fragments).
2. Gene Expression (Transcription & Translation)
Transcription
  • DNA → RNA; catalyzed by RNA polymerase.
  • Promoter region (e.g., TATA box) binds transcription factors.
  • Post‑transcriptional processing: 5' cap, 3' poly‑A tail, splicing (removal of introns, exons joined).
  • Alternative splicing yields multiple protein variants.
Translation
  • mRNA → protein on ribosomes (rRNA + proteins).
  • tRNAs carry amino acids; anticodon pairs with codon.
  • Start codon: AUG (methionine). Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA.
  • Ribosome moves along mRNA; elongation factors, release factors.

DNA Repair Mechanisms
  • Direct reversal: Photolyase (UV damage).
  • Base excision repair (BER): Removes damaged base (e.g., deamination).
  • Nucleotide excision repair (NER): Removes bulky lesions (e.g., pyrimidine dimers) – defective in xeroderma pigmentosum.
  • Mismatch repair (MMR): Corrects replication errors – defective in HNPCC.
  • Double‑strand break repair: Homologous recombination (error‑free) or non‑homologous end joining (error‑prone).
4. Autosomal Inheritance
Autosomal Dominant
  • Affected individuals have one mutant allele. Both sexes equally affected. Multiple generations affected (vertical transmission). 50% risk to offspring.
  • Examples: Huntington disease, neurofibromatosis, Marfan syndrome, familial hypercholesterolemia.
Autosomal Recessive
  • Two mutant alleles required. Usually skips generations; parents are carriers (25% risk). Higher incidence in consanguinity.
  • Examples: Cystic fibrosis, sickle cell disease, Tay‑Sachs, phenylketonuria.
5. X‑Linked Inheritance
X‑Linked Recessive
  • Males affected (hemizygous), females carriers (usually unaffected). No male‑to‑male transmission. Sons of carrier females have 50% chance affected.
  • Examples: Duchenne muscular dystrophy, hemophilia A, G6PD deficiency.
X‑Linked Dominant
  • Affected males transmit to all daughters (no sons); affected females transmit to 50% offspring. Often lethal in males.
  • Example: Incontinentia pigmenti, Rett syndrome (mostly sporadic).
6. Mitochondrial Inheritance
  • mtDNA inherited maternally (egg contributes mitochondria).
  • Disorders involve high‑energy tissues (muscle, brain). Heteroplasmy (mix of mutant and wild‑type mtDNA) affects phenotype.
  • Examples: MELAS, Leber hereditary optic neuropathy.
7. Hardy‑Weinberg Principle
For a gene with two alleles (p + q = 1): genotype frequencies p², 2pq, q². Assumptions: large population, random mating, no mutation, no selection, no migration.

Used to calculate carrier frequency (2pq) for autosomal recessive disorders. Example: CF incidence 1/2500 → q² = 0.0004 → q = 0.02 → p = 0.98 → carrier frequency 2pq ≈ 0.039 (1 in 26).

8. Genetic Selection & Drift
  • Selection: Differential survival/reproduction. Sickle cell trait (heterozygote advantage) in malaria regions.
  • Genetic drift: Random fluctuations in allele frequency (founder effect, bottleneck). Examples: Old Order Amish (Ellis‑van Creveld).
  • Gene flow: Migration between populations.
9. Chromosome Structure
  • Chromosomes: centromere (metacentric, submetacentric, acrocentric), telomeres, arms (p short, q long).
  • Karyotype: 22 pairs autosomes + 1 pair sex chromosomes.
  • Banding: G‑banding identifies structural abnormalities.
10. Meiosis & Nondisjunction
  • Meiosis I: Homologous chromosomes separate.
  • Meiosis II: Sister chromatids separate.
  • Nondisjunction: Failure to separate → aneuploidy.
    • Meiosis I: homologous chromosomes fail to separate → abnormal gametes (e.g., Down syndrome).
    • Meiosis II: sister chromatids fail to separate.

11. Polymorphisms & SNPs
  • Single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP): Variation at single base with population frequency >1%.
  • Copy number variation (CNV): Deletions/duplications larger than 1 kb.
  • VNTR/STR (microsatellites): Tandem repeats used in forensics.
12. Epigenetics
  • DNA methylation: Usually CpG islands; silences gene expression. Imprinting (e.g., Igf2, H19).
  • Histone modification: Acetylation (active), methylation (active or repressive).
  • X‑inactivation: Random in females; Barr body.
  • Imprinting disorders: Prader‑Willi (paternal deletion), Angelman (maternal deletion).
