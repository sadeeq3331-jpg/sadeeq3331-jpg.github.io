1. Cell Injury & Death in Emergencies
In emergency medicine, rapid recognition of cell injury patterns is crucial. Common causes: ischemia, trauma, toxins, infection.
  • Reversible injury: Cellular swelling, fatty change, mitochondrial changes. If insult removed, cell recovers.
  • Irreversible injury: Membrane damage, nuclear changes (pyknosis, karyorrhexis, karyolysis).

2. Acute Inflammation & SIRS
Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS) is a common pathway in critical illness. Criteria: temp >38°C or <36°C, HR >90, RR >20 or PaCO₂ <32, WBC >12k or <4k or >10% bands.
  • Sepsis: SIRS + infection.
  • Septic shock: Sepsis + hypotension despite fluid resuscitation.
  • Pathology: Endothelial activation, microvascular thrombosis, capillary leak, multi‑organ dysfunction.
3. Trauma Pathology
  • Blunt trauma: Acceleration‑deceleration, crushing, shearing. Causes contusions, lacerations, fractures.
  • Penetrating trauma: Stab wounds, gunshot wounds. Cavitation effect.
  • Specific organ injuries: Splenic rupture (most common in blunt abdominal trauma), liver laceration, pulmonary contusion.
  • Fat embolism syndrome: Long bone fractures → fat globules enter circulation → pulmonary and neurologic symptoms, petechiae.

5. Fat Embolism Syndrome
  • Cause: Long bone fractures, orthopedic surgery.
  • Pathology: Fat globules occlude pulmonary and cerebral microvasculature → endothelial damage, release of free fatty acids.
  • Clinical triad: Hypoxemia, neurologic dysfunction, petechial rash.
  • Onset: 24‑72 hours after injury.
6. Compartment Syndrome
  • Definition: Increased pressure within a closed fascial compartment → decreased perfusion → ischemia.
  • Causes: Fractures, crush injury, tight casts, burns.
  • Pathology: Ischemia of muscles and nerves; irreversible damage after 4‑6 hours.
  • Clinical: Pain out of proportion, paresthesia, pallor, paralysis, pulselessness (late).
  • Treatment: Fasciotomy.
7. Crush Injury & Rhabdomyolysis
  • Mechanism: Prolonged compression of muscles → ischemia, necrosis, release of intracellular contents (myoglobin, K⁺, phosphate).
  • Rhabdomyolysis: Myoglobinuria → acute kidney injury (pigment casts, tubular necrosis).
  • Complications: Hyperkalemia, hypocalcemia, metabolic acidosis.
8. Myocardial Infarction
  • Pathogenesis: Plaque rupture → thrombosis → coronary occlusion → ischemia.
  • Timeline:
    • 0‑12h: Coagulative necrosis, wavy fibers.
    • 12‑24h: Neutrophilic infiltrate.
    • 1‑3 days: Myocyte necrosis, inflammation peak.
    • 4‑7 days: Macrophages, granulation tissue.
    • 1‑2 weeks: Fibrosis begins.
  • Complications: Arrhythmia, heart failure, ventricular rupture (3‑7 days), pericarditis (Dressler).
9. Myocarditis
  • Causes: Viral (coxsackie, influenza), bacterial, autoimmune.
  • Pathology: Lymphocytic infiltration, myocyte necrosis. May progress to dilated cardiomyopathy.
10. Aortic Dissection
  • Pathology: Intimal tear → blood enters media → false lumen. Hypertension is main risk.
  • Types: Stanford A (ascending) – surgical; Stanford B (descending) – medical.
  • Complications: Rupture, tamponade, aortic regurgitation, myocardial infarction, stroke.
11. Cardiac Tamponade
  • Pathophysiology: Fluid in pericardial space → increased pressure → impaired diastolic filling → decreased cardiac output.
  • Causes: Trauma, post‑MI, uremia, malignancy.
  • Signs: Beck's triad (hypotension, muffled heart sounds, JVD), pulsus paradoxus.
12. Pulmonary Embolism Pathology
  • Source: Deep vein thrombosis (95%).
  • Pathology: Thrombus lodges in pulmonary arteries → infarction (hemorrhagic, wedge‑shaped) if collateral circulation inadequate.
  • Microscopy: Fresh thrombus, later organization.
13. Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)
  • Definition: Acute onset, bilateral infiltrates, PaO₂/FiO₂ ≤300, no evidence of cardiogenic edema.
  • Pathology: Diffuse alveolar damage (DAD):
    • Exudative phase (0‑7 days): edema, hyaline membranes.
    • Proliferative phase (7‑21 days): type II pneumocyte hyperplasia, fibroblast proliferation.
    • Fibrotic phase (>21 days): fibrosis.
14. Pneumonia
  • Lobar pneumonia: Entire lobe – stages: congestion, red hepatization (RBCs, fibrin), gray hepatization (neutrophils), resolution.
  • Bronchopneumonia: Patchy, peribronchiolar inflammation.
  • Interstitial pneumonia: Viral, mycoplasma – inflammation in alveolar septa.
15. Tension Pneumothorax
  • Pathophysiology: Air enters pleural space but cannot escape → increased intrapleural pressure → mediastinal shift, impaired venous return, hypotension.
  • Signs: Hypotension, tracheal deviation, absent breath sounds, distended neck veins.
  • Treatment: Needle decompression, chest tube.
16. Hemothorax
  • Cause: Trauma (intercostal or internal mammary vessels, lung laceration).
  • Pathology: Blood in pleural space → hypovolemia, lung collapse.
17. Ischemic Stroke
  • Pathology: Infarction of brain tissue. Early (6‑12h): red neurons (eosinophilic cytoplasm, pyknotic nuclei).
  • Later: Neutrophils (day 1‑3), macrophages (day 3‑7), gliosis (weeks).
  • Embolic stroke: Source from heart or carotid.
  • Lacunar stroke: Hypertensive small vessel disease.
18. Intracranial Hemorrhage
  • Epidural: Arterial (middle meningeal) – lens‑shaped, often after trauma with skull fracture.
  • Subdural: Venous (bridging veins) – crescent‑shaped, often in elderly, anticoagulated.
  • Subarachnoid: Berry aneurysm rupture → blood in CSF.
  • Intracerebral: Hypertension, amyloid angiopathy.
19. Meningitis
  • Pathology: Inflammation of pia‑arachnoid. Exudate in subarachnoid space.
  • Bacterial: Neutrophils; complications: hydrocephalus, cranial nerve palsies.
  • Viral: Lymphocytes; usually milder.
20. Encephalitis
  • Pathology: Inflammation of brain parenchyma, perivascular cuffing, microglial nodules, neuronophagia.
  • Causes: HSV‑1 (temporal lobe), arboviruses, CMV.
21. Diabetic Ketoacidosis & HHS
  • DKA: Insulin deficiency → lipolysis, ketogenesis → metabolic acidosis, hyperglycemia, ketonuria.
  • HHS: Severe hyperglycemia, hyperosmolarity, minimal ketosis. Typically type 2 diabetics.
22. Electrolyte Disorders
  • Hyperkalemia: ECG changes: peaked T, wide QRS, sine wave. Pathology: no specific histology.
  • Hypokalemia: U waves; myocyte necrosis (in severe).
  • Hyponatremia: Cerebral edema; risk of osmotic demyelination if corrected too fast.
23. Overdose Pathology
  • Acetaminophen: Centrilobular necrosis.
  • Opioids: Pulmonary edema, brain hypoxic injury.
  • Tricyclics: Cardiac necrosis.
  • Salicylates: Pulmonary edema, petechial hemorrhages.
24. Hyperthermia & Heat Stroke
  • Heat stroke: Core temp >40°C, neurologic dysfunction. Pathology: multi‑organ failure, rhabdomyolysis, DIC.
  • Malignant hyperthermia: RYR1 mutation, triggered by succinylcholine/volatile anesthetics → massive muscle contraction, hyperthermia, acidosis.
25. Hypothermia
  • Definition: Core temp <35°C. Pathophysiology: decreased metabolic rate, arrhythmias (J wave), coagulopathy.
  • Post‑mortem: Wischnewsky spots (gastric erosions).
26. Drowning
  • Freshwater: Hypotonic → alveolar damage, dilution of surfactant, atelectasis.
  • Saltwater: Hypertonic → pulmonary edema, fluid shift into alveoli.
  • Both: Hypoxia, cerebral edema.
27. Electrical & Lightning Injuries
  • Electrical: Current path determines injury. Myocardial necrosis, arrhythmias, deep tissue burns, myoglobinuria.
  • Lightning: Massive current, flashover phenomenon, cardiac arrest, neurologic sequelae, Lichtenberg figures.
