1. Pituitary & Hypothalamus Pathology
Pituitary Adenomas

Definition: Benign tumors of the anterior pituitary. Classified by size (microadenoma <1 cm, macroadenoma >1 cm) and hormone secretion.

  • Prolactinoma: Most common; causes galactorrhea, hypogonadism (amenorrhea, infertility, decreased libido).
  • Somatotroph adenoma (GH excess): In children → gigantism; in adults → acromegaly (coarse features, prognathism, organomegaly, glucose intolerance).
  • Corticotroph adenoma (ACTH excess): Cushing disease (central obesity, moon facies, purple striae, hypertension).
  • Non‑functioning adenoma: Present with mass effects (bitemporal hemianopia, headache, hypopituitarism).

Pearl: Prolactinomas are treated with dopamine agonists (cabergoline). GH‑secreting tumors may require surgery and/or somatostatin analogs.

Hypopituitarism
  • Causes: Pituitary tumors (compression), pituitary apoplexy (hemorrhage into adenoma), Sheehan syndrome (postpartum necrosis), empty sella syndrome, infiltrative diseases (sarcoidosis, hemochromatosis), radiation.
  • Clinical features: Loss of target gland hormones (secondary hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency, hypogonadism).
Posterior Pituitary Disorders
  • Diabetes insipidus (DI): Deficiency of ADH (central) or renal resistance (nephrogenic). Presents with polyuria, polydipsia, dilute urine.
  • SIADH (syndrome of inappropriate ADH): Excess ADH → water retention, hyponatremia, concentrated urine. Causes: CNS disorders, lung cancer (small cell), drugs.
2. Thyroid Pathology
Hyperthyroidism
  • Graves disease: Autoimmune, TSH receptor antibodies (TSI) stimulate thyroid. Pathology: diffuse hyperplasia, lymphocytic infiltration. Extrathyroidal manifestations: ophthalmopathy, pretibial myxedema.
  • Toxic multinodular goiter (Plummer disease): Hyperfunctioning nodules, usually in elderly.
  • Toxic adenoma: Solitary hyperfunctioning nodule.
Hypothyroidism
  • Hashimoto thyroiditis: Autoimmune destruction, most common cause in iodine‑sufficient areas. Pathology: lymphocytic infiltration, germinal centers, Hurthle cell change, fibrosis. Associated with other autoimmune diseases.
  • Primary myxedema: Atrophic form.
  • Secondary hypothyroidism: Pituitary failure (TSH deficiency).
  • Cretinism: Congenital hypothyroidism → intellectual disability, short stature.
Thyroiditis
  • Subacute (De Quervain) thyroiditis: Post‑viral, painful, granulomatous inflammation with giant cells. Transient hyperthyroid phase.
  • Silent (painless) thyroiditis: Lymphocytic, often postpartum; transient hyperthyroid then hypothyroid.
  • Riedel thyroiditis: Fibrous invasion of thyroid and surrounding tissues; rare.
Goiter

Diffuse or nodular enlargement of thyroid. Causes: iodine deficiency, goitrogens, dyshormonogenesis. Simple goiter: compensatory hyperplasia; multinodular goiter may become toxic.

3. Parathyroid & Calcium Disorders
Hyperparathyroidism
  • Primary: Adenoma (80‑85%), hyperplasia (10‑15%), carcinoma (<1%). Presents with hypercalcemia (stones, bones, groans, psychiatric overtones). Pathology: chief cell adenoma, water‑clear cell hyperplasia (rare).
  • Secondary: Due to chronic renal failure, vitamin D deficiency → compensatory hyperplasia of all four glands.
  • Tertiary: Autonomous hyperfunction after long‑term secondary hyperplasia (e.g., after renal transplant).
Hypoparathyroidism
  • Most common: Postsurgical (thyroidectomy). Autoimmune (APECED syndrome).
  • Pseudohypoparathyroidism: End‑organ resistance to PTH; associated with Albright hereditary osteodystrophy (short stature, brachydactyly).
4. Adrenal Pathology
Adrenal Cortex
  • Cushing syndrome: Excess cortisol. Causes: iatrogenic (steroids), pituitary adenoma (Cushing disease), adrenal adenoma/carcinoma, ectopic ACTH (e.g., small cell lung cancer). Pathology: adrenal hyperplasia or tumor. Clinical: central obesity, moon facies, easy bruising, striae, hypertension.
  • Conn syndrome (primary hyperaldosteronism): Aldosterone‑producing adenoma (APA) or bilateral adrenal hyperplasia. Hypertension, hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis.
  • Adrenocortical carcinoma: Rare, large, aggressive. May secrete androgens causing virilization, or mixed hormones.
  • Adrenal insufficiency:
    • Primary (Addison disease): Autoimmune destruction, TB, metastasis, HIV. Low cortisol, high ACTH, hyperpigmentation (due to ACTH stimulation of melanocytes).
    • Secondary: Pituitary failure → low ACTH, no hyperpigmentation.
  • Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH): Most common 21‑hydroxylase deficiency → cortisol deficiency, ACTH driven androgen excess → virilization, salt‑wasting crisis.
Adrenal Medulla
  • Pheochromocytoma: Tumor of chromaffin cells; secretes catecholamines. Rule of 10: 10% bilateral, 10% extra‑adrenal, 10% malignant, 10% familial (MEN 2, VHL, NF1). Presents with episodic hypertension, headache, palpitations.
  • Neuroblastoma: Pediatric malignancy from neural crest; adrenal most common site. May secrete catecholamines, urine VMA/HVA.
5. Endocrine Pancreas Pathology
Diabetes Mellitus
  • Type 1: Autoimmune destruction of β‑cells (insulitis). Genetic susceptibility (HLA DR3/DR4). Presents in youth with ketoacidosis. Pathology: reduced islet mass, lymphocytic infiltration.
  • Type 2: Insulin resistance + β‑cell dysfunction. Associated with obesity. Pathology: amyloid deposition in islets (amylin), β‑cell exhaustion.
  • Complications: Microvascular (retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy) and macrovascular (atherosclerosis).

6. Gonadal Pathology
Male
  • Hypogonadism: Primary (testicular failure) or secondary (hypogonadotropic). Klinefelter syndrome (XXY) – small firm testes, tall, infertility.
  • Testicular tumors: Germ cell tumors (seminoma, embryonal, yolk sac, teratoma, choriocarcinoma) and sex cord stromal (Leydig cell, Sertoli cell). Cryptorchidism risk factor. Tumor markers: AFP (yolk sac, embryonal), hCG (choriocarcinoma, syncytiotrophoblast).
Female
  • Hypogonadism: Turner syndrome (XO) – streak ovaries, webbed neck, coarctation.
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS): Hyperandrogenism, oligo‑anovulation, polycystic ovaries. Associated with insulin resistance.
  • Ovarian tumors: Surface epithelial (serous, mucinous, endometrioid), germ cell (dysgerminoma, teratoma), sex cord‑stromal (granulosa cell – produces estrogen, may cause precocious puberty).

8. Polyglandular Autoimmune Syndromes
  • PGA I (APS1): Autoimmune polyendocrinopathy‑candidiasis‑ectodermal dystrophy (APECED). AIRE gene. Mucocutaneous candidiasis, hypoparathyroidism, Addison disease.
  • PGA II (Schmidt syndrome): HLA‑DR3/DR4. Addison disease + autoimmune thyroid disease (Hashimoto or Graves) + type 1 diabetes.
9. Neuroendocrine Tumors (Carcinoid, etc.)

Carcinoid tumors: Arise from enterochromaffin cells; most common in appendix, ileum, lung. Secrete serotonin and other vasoactive substances. Carcinoid syndrome (flushing, diarrhea, wheezing, right‑sided heart valve lesions) occurs with liver metastases (bypass hepatic degradation).

Histology: Nest of uniform cells, granular chromatin. Silver stains positive. May be associated with MEN1.

10. Metabolic Bone Disorders
  • Osteoporosis: Decreased bone mass, microarchitectural deterioration. Primary (postmenopausal, senile) or secondary (steroids, hyperparathyroidism).
  • Osteomalacia / Rickets: Defective mineralization due to vitamin D deficiency or hypophosphatemia. Rickets in children: bowing, growth retardation. Osteomalacia in adults: bone pain, fractures.
  • Paget disease of bone: Disordered bone remodeling – excessive osteoclast activity followed by osteoblast activity. Mosaic bone. Complications: fractures, deformity, high‑output heart failure, osteosarcoma risk.
