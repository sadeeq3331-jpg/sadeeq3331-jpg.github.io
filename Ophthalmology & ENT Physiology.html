1. Anatomy of the Eye
Layers of the Eyeball
The eye has three concentric layers:
  • Fibrous tunic (outer): Sclera (opaque, protective) and cornea (transparent, refractive).
  • Vascular tunic (uvea): Iris, ciliary body, choroid.
  • Nervous tunic (inner): Retina.
Anterior Segment
Cornea: Avascular, 5 layers: epithelium, Bowman's layer, stroma, Descemet's membrane, endothelium. Endothelium maintains corneal dehydration via Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase pumps.
Anterior chamber: Between cornea and iris. Posterior chamber: Between iris and lens. Both filled with aqueous humor.
Iris: Two muscles: sphincter pupillae (parasympathetic, miosis) and dilator pupillae (sympathetic, mydriasis).
Ciliary body: Consists of ciliary processes (produce aqueous humor) and ciliary muscle (accommodation).
Lens: Biconvex, transparent, avascular. Suspended by zonules from ciliary body.
Posterior Segment
Vitreous humor: Gel filling the posterior cavity, attached to retina.
Retina: Composed of pigment epithelium and neural retina. Layers: photoreceptors (rods/cones) → bipolar cells → ganglion cells. Macula (central) contains fovea with highest cone density.
Optic disc: Where ganglion cell axons exit; no photoreceptors → blind spot.
2. Refraction & Accommodation
Refraction is the bending of light rays to focus on the retina. The cornea provides about 2/3 of refractive power (~43 diopters); the lens provides variable power (~15-30 D).
Accommodation
For near vision: Ciliary muscle contracts → zonules relax → lens becomes more convex (increased refractive power). Pupil constricts (miosis) to increase depth of focus. Convergence of eyes also occurs.
Presbyopia: Age‑related loss of lens elasticity, decreasing accommodative amplitude. Near point recedes; corrected with reading glasses.

3. Photoreceptors & Phototransduction
Two types: Rods (dim light, scotopic, monochrome) and Cones (bright light, photopic, color). Cones are concentrated in fovea; rods in periphery.
Phototransduction
In dark: high cGMP keeps Na⁺ channels open (depolarized, glutamate release). Light isomerizes 11‑cis retinal to all‑trans retinal, activating opsin → transducin → phosphodiesterase → cGMP hydrolysis → channels close → hyperpolarization → less glutamate release. This is opposite to most neurons.
Dark current: Inward Na⁺ current in dark. Light reduces it.
Light hyperpolarizes photoreceptors; dark depolarizes.
4. Visual Pathway
Retinal ganglion cell axons form optic nerve. At optic chiasm, fibers from nasal retina cross. Optic tract synapses in lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) of thalamus. Geniculocalcarine tract (optic radiation) projects to primary visual cortex (V1) in occipital lobe.
Lesion effects:
  • Optic nerve: unilateral blindness.
  • Optic chiasm: bitemporal hemianopia (pituitary tumor).
  • Optic tract: contralateral homonymous hemianopia.
  • Meyer's loop (temporal radiation): superior quadrantanopia.
  • Dorsal radiation (parietal): inferior quadrantanopia.
5. Aqueous Humor Dynamics
Aqueous humor is produced by ciliary processes (active secretion and ultrafiltration). It flows from posterior chamber through pupil into anterior chamber, then drains via trabecular meshwork into Schlemm's canal (conventional outflow) and uveoscleral pathway (unconventional). Intraocular pressure (IOP) normally 10-21 mmHg.
6. Pupillary Reflexes
Light reflex: Afferent: optic nerve; efferent: parasympathetic via oculomotor nerve to sphincter pupillae. Direct and consensual responses.
Near reflex (accommodation triad): Convergence, lens accommodation, pupillary constriction. Mediated by higher cortical pathways.
Marcus Gunn pupil (relative afferent pupillary defect): In optic nerve disease, light in affected eye causes less constriction, but consensual response normal.
7. Lacrimal System
Lacrimal gland (orbital and palpebral parts) produces tears. Parasympathetic innervation (CN VII via pterygopalatine ganglion) stimulates secretion. Tears drain via puncta → canaliculi → lacrimal sac → nasolacrimal duct → inferior meatus.
8. Anatomy of the Ear
External ear: Pinna, external auditory canal (cerumen glands). Tympanic membrane separates external and middle ear.
Middle ear: Air‑filled cavity with ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes). Stapes footplate fits into oval window. Tensor tympani (CN V) and stapedius (CN VII) muscles dampen sound. Eustachian tube connects to nasopharynx, equalizes pressure.
Inner ear: Bony labyrinth (perilymph) and membranous labyrinth (endolymph). Cochlea (hearing), vestibule (utricle/saccule), semicircular canals (balance).
9. Sound Transmission
Sound waves → tympanic membrane → ossicles amplify (lever action, area ratio) → stapes pushes oval window → pressure wave in perilymph of scala vestibuli → travels through helicotrema to scala tympani → round window bulges. Basilar membrane vibrates according to frequency (tonotopy).
Impedance matching: Middle ear overcomes air‑fluid impedance; without it, most sound energy would reflect.
10. Cochlear Mechanics & Hair Cells
Organ of Corti sits on basilar membrane. Inner hair cells (IHCs) are primary sensory cells; outer hair cells (OHCs) amplify vibrations via electromotility. Stereocilia deflection opens mechanotransduction channels → depolarization → neurotransmitter release.
Endolymph has high K⁺, low Na⁺ (like intracellular fluid). Perilymph has high Na⁺, low K⁺. The endocochlear potential (+80 mV) drives K⁺ into hair cells.
"Endolymph is K‑rich (potassium), perilymph is Na‑rich (sodium)."
11. Auditory Pathway
Spiral ganglion cells → cochlear nerve → cochlear nuclei (medulla) → superior olive → lateral lemniscus → inferior colliculus → medial geniculate body (thalamus) → primary auditory cortex (Heschl's gyrus). Bilateral representation.
12. Vestibular System
Semicircular canals: Detect angular acceleration. Crista ampullaris with hair cells embedded in cupula. Endolymph movement deflects cupula.
Otolith organs (utricle, saccule): Detect linear acceleration and gravity. Hair cells embedded in otolithic membrane with otoconia (calcium carbonate crystals). Utricle senses horizontal, saccule vertical.
13. Vestibular Reflexes
Vestibulo‑ocular reflex (VOR): Maintains gaze during head movement. Signals from semicircular canals → vestibular nuclei → oculomotor nuclei → compensatory eye movement.
Vestibulospinal reflex: Maintains posture.
14. Olfaction
Olfactory epithelium in superior nasal cavity contains bipolar neurons with cilia bearing odorant receptors. Each receptor binds specific odorants, activating G‑protein (Golf) → adenylate cyclase → cAMP → Na⁺/Ca²⁺ influx. Axons project through cribriform plate to olfactory bulb → olfactory tract → primary olfactory cortex (uncus, entorhinal) without thalamic relay.
15. Taste
Taste buds on tongue, palate, epiglottis. Five basic tastes: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami (glutamate). Receptor cells synapse with afferent fibers:
  • Anterior 2/3 (fungiform, foliate) → chorda tympani (CN VII).
  • Posterior 1/3 (circumvallate) → glossopharyngeal (CN IX).
  • Epiglottis → vagus (CN X).
