1. Introduction to the Immune System
The immune system protects the body from pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites) and eliminates damaged or malignant cells. It comprises two interconnected arms: innate immunity (rapid, non‑specific) and adaptive immunity (slow, highly specific, with memory).
  • Innate immunity: Physical barriers (skin, mucosa), chemical barriers (lysozyme, defensins), cellular components (macrophages, neutrophils, NK cells), and soluble proteins (complement).
  • Adaptive immunity: Lymphocytes (B and T cells) and their products (antibodies, cytokines). Characterized by specificity, diversity, memory, and self/non‑self discrimination.

3. Lymphoid Organs
  • Primary (central) lymphoid organs: Bone marrow (B cell development) and thymus (T cell development).
  • Secondary (peripheral) lymphoid organs: Lymph nodes, spleen, mucosa‑associated lymphoid tissue (MALT), tonsils, appendix. Sites where lymphocytes encounter antigens and are activated.
4. Innate Immunity
Innate immunity provides immediate defense against pathogens. It recognizes conserved microbial patterns via germline‑encoded receptors. Does not generate memory.
  • Barriers: Skin (keratin, low pH), mucous membranes, cilia, antimicrobial peptides (defensins, cathelicidins).
  • Cellular components: Phagocytes (macrophages, neutrophils), NK cells, dendritic cells.
  • Soluble mediators: Complement, acute‑phase proteins (C‑reactive protein, mannose‑binding lectin), cytokines.
5. Pattern Recognition Receptors (PRRs)
  • Toll‑like receptors (TLRs): Membrane‑bound (endosome or cell surface) that recognize PAMPs (e.g., LPS, flagellin, dsRNA).
  • NOD‑like receptors (NLRs): Cytosolic; recognize bacterial products; form inflammasomes (e.g., NLRP3).
  • RIG‑I‑like receptors (RLRs): Detect viral RNA in cytosol.
  • C‑type lectin receptors (CLRs): Recognize fungal carbohydrates.
6. Complement System

7. Phagocytosis
  • Uptake of large particles (>0.5 μm) by phagocytes.
  • Steps: Recognition (via receptors for Fc, complement, scavenger), ingestion, phagosome formation, fusion with lysosome, killing (reactive oxygen species, nitric oxide, enzymes).
8. B Lymphocytes & Antibodies
  • B cells develop in bone marrow, express B‑cell receptor (BCR = membrane IgM/IgD).
  • Upon antigen binding and T‑cell help, differentiate into plasma cells (secrete antibodies) and memory B cells.
  • Antibody structure: Two heavy chains, two light chains; variable region binds antigen, constant region mediates effector functions.

9. T Lymphocytes & Receptors
  • T cells develop in thymus; express T‑cell receptor (TCR) associated with CD3 complex.
  • CD4+ helper T cells: Recognize antigen on MHC class II; differentiate into Th1, Th2, Th17, Tfh, Treg.
  • CD8+ cytotoxic T cells: Recognize antigen on MHC class I; kill infected or malignant cells.

11. Antigen Processing & Presentation
  • MHC I pathway: Endogenous proteins degraded by proteasome → peptides transported by TAP into ER → loaded onto MHC I → Golgi → cell surface.
  • MHC II pathway: Exogenous antigens endocytosed → degraded in lysosomes → MHC II (with invariant chain) binds peptide in MIIC compartment → surface.
12. Humoral Immunity
  • Mediated by antibodies. Functions: neutralization, opsonization, complement activation, antibody‑dependent cell‑mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC).
13. Cell‑Mediated Immunity
  • CD8+ T cells: Kill via perforin/granzyme or Fas/FasL.
  • CD4+ T helper subsets:
    • Th1: IFN‑γ, macrophage activation (intracellular pathogens).
    • Th2: IL‑4, IL‑5, IgE, eosinophils (helminths, allergy).
    • Th17: IL‑17, neutrophil recruitment (extracellular bacteria, fungi).
    • Treg: Suppress immune responses (IL‑10, TGF‑β).
14. Cytokines
  • Interleukins (IL): IL‑2 (T cell growth), IL‑4 (B cell switching), IL‑6 (inflammation).
  • Interferons (IFN): Type I (IFN‑α/β) antiviral; type II (IFN‑γ) macrophage activation.
  • TNF‑α: Inflammation, fever.
  • Chemokines: Chemotaxis (e.g., IL‑8).
  • TGF‑β: Anti‑inflammatory, fibrosis.
15. Immunological Tolerance
  • Central tolerance: In thymus (T cells) and bone marrow (B cells) – negative selection of self‑reactive lymphocytes.
  • Peripheral tolerance: Anergy, deletion, regulatory T cells.
16. Immunological Memory
  • Secondary response is faster, stronger, and more specific due to memory B and T cells.
17. Vaccines & Immunization
  • Active immunization: exposure to antigen (live attenuated, inactivated, subunit, toxoid) induces protective immune response.
  • Passive immunization: transfer of antibodies (e.g., maternal, immunoglobulin therapy).

19. Autoimmunity
Breakdown of tolerance leading to immune responses against self. Examples: type 1 diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, multiple sclerosis. Risk factors: genetic (HLA associations), environmental, failure of regulatory mechanisms.
