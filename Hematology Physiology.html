1. Introduction & Blood Composition

Blood is a specialized connective tissue composed of cells and plasma. It carries oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and waste products; regulates pH and temperature; and provides immunity and hemostasis.

Components of Blood
  • Plasma: 55% of blood volume; water (92%), proteins (7%) – albumin, globulins, fibrinogen, and solutes.
  • Formed elements: Red blood cells (RBCs), white blood cells (WBCs), platelets.
Cell TypeNormal CountFunction
RBCs4.5‑5.5 million/μLO₂ transport
Platelets150‑400 thousand/μLHemostasis
WBCs4‑11 thousand/μLImmunity

Clinical pearl: Hematocrit (packed cell volume) is the percentage of RBCs in blood; normal ~45%.

2. Hematopoiesis & Stem Cells

Hematopoiesis is the process of blood cell formation, occurring in the bone marrow (and fetal liver/spleen). It begins with pluripotent hematopoietic stem cells (PHSCs).

Stem Cell Hierarchy
  • Pluripotent stem cell → committed progenitors (CFUs) → precursor cells (blasts) → mature cells.
  • Erythropoietin (EPO): Stimulates RBC production (from kidney).
  • Thrombopoietin (TPO): Stimulates platelet production (from liver).
  • Colony‑stimulating factors (CSFs): Granulocyte‑CSF, macrophage‑CSF, etc., stimulate WBC production.

Clinical pearl: Bone marrow biopsy is used to assess hematopoiesis. Aplastic anemia results from stem cell failure.

3. Erythropoiesis & Red Blood Cells

Erythropoiesis is the production of RBCs, taking about 7 days. It is regulated by erythropoietin (EPO).

Stages
  • Pronormoblast → basophilic normoblast → polychromatophilic normoblast → orthochromatic normoblast → reticulocyte → mature RBC.
  • Reticulocytes are released into blood and mature in 1‑2 days.
RBC structure: Biconcave disc, no nucleus, no organelles, rich in hemoglobin.

Clinical pearl: Reticulocyte count indicates bone marrow response to anemia. High in hemolysis; low in deficiency states.

4. Hemoglobin & Oxygen Transport

Hemoglobin (Hb) is a tetramer of globin chains and heme (iron‑protoporphyrin IX). It reversibly binds O₂.

Types of Hemoglobin
  • HbA (α₂β₂): 95‑98% in adults.
  • HbA₂ (α₂δ₂): 2‑3%.
  • HbF (α₂γ₂): Main in fetal life, higher O₂ affinity.
Oxygen Dissociation Curve
  • Right shift (↓affinity) by ↑PCO₂, ↑H⁺ (Bohr effect), ↑2,3‑BPG, ↑temperature – enhances O₂ unloading.
  • Left shift (↑affinity) by opposite factors.

Clinical pearl: Sickle cell disease results from HbS (β⁶Glu→Val) – polymerizes under low O₂. Thalassemia from globin chain imbalance.

5. Iron Metabolism

Iron is essential for heme synthesis. It is absorbed in duodenum, transported by transferrin, stored as ferritin and hemosiderin.

  • Absorption: Ferric (Fe³⁺) reduced to ferrous (Fe²⁺) by duodenal cytochrome b; taken up by DMT1.
  • Export: Ferroportin exports iron into plasma; oxidized to Fe³⁺ by hephaestin, bound to transferrin.
  • Regulation: Hepcidin (from liver) degrades ferroportin, decreasing iron absorption and release.

Clinical pearl: Iron deficiency causes microcytic, hypochromic anemia. Hemochromatosis results from iron overload.

6. Leukopoiesis & White Blood Cells

WBCs are classified into granulocytes (neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils) and agranulocytes (lymphocytes, monocytes).

Lymphocytes20‑40%B cells (antibodies), T cells (cellular), NK cells
Monocytes2‑8%Become macrophages, phagocytosis, antigen presentation
Eosinophils1‑4%Anti‑parasitic, allergy

Clinical pearl: Neutrophilia in infection; neutropenia increases infection risk. Lymphocytosis in viral infections.

7. Platelets & Megakaryopoiesis

Platelets (thrombocytes) are fragments of megakaryocytes, produced in bone marrow under thrombopoietin (TPO) stimulation.

  • Structure: α‑granules (adhesion proteins), dense granules (ADP, Ca²⁺, serotonin), lysosomes.
  • Function: Primary hemostasis: adhesion (vWF), aggregation, and secretion.

Clinical pearl: Thrombocytopenia (<50,000/μL) risks bleeding. Aspirin irreversibly inhibits cyclooxygenase, blocking thromboxane A₂, impairing aggregation.

8. Hemostasis & Coagulation

Hemostasis prevents blood loss after vascular injury. It involves vasoconstriction, platelet plug formation (primary hemostasis), and coagulation cascade (secondary hemostasis).

Coagulation Cascade
  • Intrinsic pathway: Factor XII, XI, IX, VIII – initiated by contact with collagen; measured by aPTT.
  • Extrinsic pathway: Factor VII + tissue factor – measured by PT.
  • Common pathway: Factors X, V, prothrombin, fibrinogen.
Natural Anticoagulants
  • Antithrombin (inhibits thrombin, Xa; heparin enhances).
  • Protein C and S (inactivate Va, VIIIa).
  • Tissue factor pathway inhibitor (TFPI).
Fibrinolysis
  • Plasminogen → plasmin (by tPA) → degrades fibrin → D‑dimers.

Clinical pearl: Hemophilia A (factor VIII deficiency) prolongs aPTT. Warfarin inhibits vitamin K‑dependent factors (II, VII, IX, X).

Rh system: Rh‑positive has D antigen. Anti‑D is not naturally present; sensitization occurs after exposure (transfusion or pregnancy).

Clinical pearl: Hemolytic disease of the newborn (HDN) occurs when Rh‑negative mother carries Rh‑positive baby; anti‑D crosses placenta.

10. Spleen & Mononuclear Phagocyte System

The spleen filters blood, removes old RBCs, recycles iron, and participates in immune responses.

  • Red pulp: Removes damaged RBCs, stores platelets, and recycles iron.
  • White pulp: Contains lymphoid follicles (B cells) and periarteriolar lymphoid sheaths (T cells).
  • Marginal zone: Filters blood‑borne antigens.

Clinical pearl: Hyposplenism (e.g., after splenectomy) increases risk of encapsulated bacteria (Streptococcus pneumoniae, H. influenzae, Neisseria meningitidis). Howell‑Jolly bodies (nuclear remnants) appear in RBCs.
