1. Topical Corticosteroids
Topical corticosteroids are the most frequently prescribed dermatologic drugs. They have anti‑inflammatory, antiproliferative, and immunosuppressive effects.
Mechanism of Action
  • Diffuse into cells, bind glucocorticoid receptors → complex translocates to nucleus → regulate gene transcription (trans‑repression of NF‑κB, AP‑1).
  • Inhibit cytokine production (IL‑1, IL‑2, IFN‑γ, TNF‑α).
  • Induce lipocortin, which inhibits phospholipase A2 → ↓ prostaglandins and leukotrienes.
  • Vasoconstriction (blanching effect).
Potency Classification (US system – Classes I‑VII)
ClassPotencyExamples
ISuper‑highClobetasol propionate 0.05%, Betamethasone dipropionate 0.05% (optimized vehicle)
II‑IIIHighMometasone furoate 0.1%, Triamcinolone acetonide 0.1%
IV‑VMediumHydrocortisone valerate 0.2%, Betamethasone valerate 0.1%
VI‑VIILowHydrocortisone 1‑2.5%, Dexamethasone 0.1%

Choice of potency: Use lowest potency that is effective. Low potency for face, intertriginous areas; high potency for palms, soles, thick plaques.

Adverse Effects
  • Local: Skin atrophy, striae, telangiectasias, hypopigmentation, acneiform eruptions, perioral dermatitis, rosacea exacerbation, tinea incognito.
  • Systemic (with high potency/large area/occlusion): HPA axis suppression, Cushing syndrome, hyperglycemia.
Vehicle Selection
  • Ointments: Greasy, more occlusive, best for dry, lichenified lesions.
  • Creams: Water‑washable, good for acute/subacute dermatoses.
  • Lotions/gels: For scalp, hairy areas.
  • Foams: Easy application for scalp.

Systemic Antibiotics in Dermatology
  • Tetracyclines (doxycycline, minocycline): First‑line for moderate‑severe acne, rosacea, perioral dermatitis. Also anti‑inflammatory effects (MMP inhibition). Side effects: photosensitivity (doxycycline), vertigo (minocycline), hyperpigmentation (minocycline).
  • Macrolides (azithromycin, erythromycin): Alternative in acne, also for Bartonella, Lyme disease.
  • Cephalexin: For staphylococcal and streptococcal skin infections (cellulitis, impetigo).
  • Dicloxacillin: Anti‑staphylococcal penicillin.
  • Sulfamethoxazole‑trimethoprim: MRSA skin infections, acne.

Systemic Antifungals
  • Terbinafine: For onychomycosis, tinea capitis (dermatophytes). Hepatotoxicity risk.
  • Itraconazole: Broad spectrum (dermatophytes, Candida, Malassezia, histoplasmosis). Capsule absorption variable; pulse dosing for onychomycosis.
  • Fluconazole: For Candida infections, tinea versicolor; good CSF penetration.
  • Griseofulvin: Older agent for tinea capitis (fungistatic); interferes with microtubule function. Discontinued in many countries.

6. Immunomodulators (Topical & Systemic)
Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors (TCIs)
  • Tacrolimus ointment, Pimecrolimus cream: Inhibit T‑cell activation by blocking calcineurin → ↓ IL‑2.
  • Uses: Atopic dermatitis (second‑line, steroid‑sparing for sensitive areas).
  • Side effects: Burning, stinging; black box warning for rare malignancy (risk vs benefit).
Systemic Immunomodulators
  • Methotrexate: Inhibits dihydrofolate reductase; used in psoriasis, atopic dermatitis, autoimmune bullous diseases. Monitor LFTs, CBC; supplement folic acid.
  • Cyclosporine: Calcineurin inhibitor; rapid onset for severe psoriasis, atopic dermatitis. Nephrotoxic, hypertension; short‑term use.
  • Azathioprine: Purine analog; used in autoimmune bullous diseases, atopic dermatitis, lupus. Check TPMT before use.
  • Mycophenolate mofetil: Inhibits inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase; used in autoimmune bullous diseases, eczema.

8. Other Dermatologic Drugs
Antiparasitics
  • Permethrin 5% cream: First‑line for scabies; safe in infants.
  • Ivermectin (oral): For scabies (especially crusted), head lice, rosacea (topical).
  • Crotamiton: Antipruritic and scabicidal.
  • Malathion, lindane: Second‑line for lice (neurotoxic).
Agents for Hyperpigmentation
  • Hydroquinone: Inhibits tyrosinase; used for melasma. Risk of ochronosis with long‑term use.
  • Azelaic acid: For acne, rosacea, melasma (tyrosinase inhibitor).
  • Tranexamic acid (oral/topical): For melasma (inhibits plasmin).
  • Kojic acid, arbutin: Cosmetic lightening agents.
Miscellaneous
  • Calcipotriene (vitamin D analog): For psoriasis; inhibits proliferation, induces differentiation. Often combined with betamethasone.
  • Tazarotene (retinoid): Already covered.
  • Pimecrolimus/tacrolimus: Covered.
  • Crisaborole (PDE4 inhibitor): Topical for mild‑moderate atopic dermatitis.
  • Ruxolitinib (JAK inhibitor): Topical for atopic dermatitis.
