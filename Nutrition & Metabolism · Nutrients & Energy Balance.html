1. Carbohydrates
Carbohydrates are the body's primary energy source, providing 4 kcal/g. They are classified as:
  • Monosaccharides: Glucose, fructose, galactose.
  • Disaccharides: Sucrose (glucose + fructose), lactose (glucose + galactose), maltose (glucose + glucose).
  • Polysaccharides: Starch (amylose, amylopectin), glycogen, fiber (cellulose, pectin).
Dietary fiber: Non‑digestible carbohydrates. Soluble fiber (pectin, β‑glucan) lowers cholesterol; insoluble fiber (cellulose) promotes bowel regularity.
2. Proteins & Amino Acids
  • Proteins provide 4 kcal/g. Composed of amino acids.
  • Essential amino acids: Histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, valine (PVT TIM HALL).
  • Complete proteins: Contain all essential amino acids (animal sources, soy, quinoa).
  • Nitrogen balance: Positive (growth, pregnancy), negative (starvation, illness).
3. Lipids (Fats)
  • Lipids provide 9 kcal/g. Essential for cell membranes, hormone synthesis, and energy storage.
  • Fatty acids: Saturated (no double bonds), monounsaturated (one double bond), polyunsaturated (multiple double bonds).
  • Essential fatty acids: Linoleic acid (omega‑6), α‑linolenic acid (omega‑3).
  • Triglycerides: Main storage form in adipose tissue.
  • Cholesterol: Not an energy source; precursor for steroid hormones, vitamin D, bile acids.
4. Vitamins
Fat‑Soluble Vitamins (A, D, E, K)
  • Vitamin A: Vision, epithelial integrity. Deficiency: night blindness, xerophthalmia.
  • Vitamin D: Calcium homeostasis, bone health. Deficiency: rickets (children), osteomalacia (adults).
  • Vitamin E: Antioxidant. Deficiency: hemolytic anemia, neuropathy (rare).
  • Vitamin K: Blood clotting (factors II, VII, IX, X). Deficiency: bleeding.
Water‑Soluble Vitamins (B‑complex, C)
  • Vitamin B1 (thiamine): Coenzyme in carbohydrate metabolism. Deficiency: beriberi, Wernicke‑Korsakoff.
  • Vitamin B2 (riboflavin): Component of FAD and FMN. Deficiency: cheilosis, glossitis.
  • Vitamin B3 (niacin): Component of NAD⁺/NADP⁺. Deficiency: pellagra (diarrhea, dermatitis, dementia).
  • Vitamin B5 (pantothenic acid): Component of CoA. Deficiency rare.
  • Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine): Transamination, neurotransmitter synthesis. Deficiency: neuropathy, sideroblastic anemia.
  • Vitamin B7 (biotin): Carboxylase cofactor. Deficiency: dermatitis, alopecia.
  • Vitamin B9 (folate): DNA synthesis, methylation. Deficiency: megaloblastic anemia, neural tube defects.
  • Vitamin B12 (cobalamin): Myelin synthesis, folate metabolism. Deficiency: pernicious anemia, subacute combined degeneration.
  • Vitamin C (ascorbic acid): Collagen synthesis, antioxidant. Deficiency: scurvy.

6. Digestion Process
  • Mouth: Salivary amylase (starch → maltose), lingual lipase.
  • Stomach: Gastric lipase, pepsin (protein digestion), HCl denatures proteins.
  • Small intestine:
    • Pancreatic enzymes: trypsin, chymotrypsin (proteins), pancreatic amylase, pancreatic lipase, nucleases.
    • Brush border enzymes: disaccharidases (sucrase, lactase, maltase), peptidases.
  • Bile (from liver) emulsifies fats.
7. Nutrient Absorption
  • Carbohydrates: Absorbed as monosaccharides (glucose, galactose via SGLT1; fructose via GLUT5).
  • Proteins: Absorbed as amino acids, di‑, tri‑peptides via various transporters (PEPT1).
  • Lipids: Micelles (bile salts) → diffusion into enterocytes → chylomicrons → lymph.
  • Vitamins: B12 requires intrinsic factor; fat‑soluble vitamins in micelles.
  • Minerals: Calcium (active, vitamin D‑dependent), iron (heme vs non‑heme), etc.
8. Energy Balance & Expenditure
Energy balance = Energy intake – Energy expenditure.
  • Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) = BMR + TEF + PA
  • Positive balance → weight gain; negative balance → weight loss.
9. Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)
  • Energy expended at rest (fasting, supine, thermoneutral).
  • Accounts for 60‑70% of TDEE.
  • Determined by lean body mass, age (↓ with age), gender (♂ > ♀), genetics.
  • Harris‑Benedict equation: Estimate BMR based on weight, height, age, sex.
10. Thermic Effect of Food (TEF)
  • Energy cost of digesting, absorbing, and metabolizing nutrients.
  • ~10% of energy intake; protein has highest TEF (~20‑30%), carbs ~5‑10%, fat ~0‑3%.
11. Fed State (Absorptive)
  • Lasts 2‑4 hours after meal; insulin dominant.
  • Glucose → glycogen (liver, muscle) or fat (adipose).
  • Amino acids → protein synthesis (muscle, liver).
  • Fatty acids stored as triglycerides in adipose tissue.
12. Fasted State (Post‑absorptive)
  • Glucagon dominant; insulin low.
  • Glycogenolysis (liver glycogen → glucose).
  • Gluconeogenesis (from lactate, glycerol, amino acids).
  • Lipolysis → fatty acids → energy; ketogenesis in prolonged fasting.
13. Hormonal Regulation
  • Insulin: Anabolic; promotes glucose uptake, glycogen synthesis, protein synthesis, fat storage.
  • Glucagon: Catabolic; promotes glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis, ketogenesis.
  • Epinephrine: Stress hormone; mobilizes glucose and fatty acids.
  • Cortisol: Promotes gluconeogenesis, protein catabolism.
  • Growth hormone: Promotes protein synthesis, lipolysis.
  • Thyroid hormones: Increase BMR, stimulate metabolism.
14. Body Composition Assessment
  • BMI (kg/m²): Underweight <18.5, normal 18.5‑24.9, overweight 25‑29.9, obese ≥30.
  • Waist circumference: >102 cm (men), >88 cm (women) → increased cardiometabolic risk.
  • Skinfold thickness: Estimates body fat %.
  • Bioelectrical impedance, DEXA, underwater weighing.
15. Adipose Tissue & Obesity
  • White adipose tissue: Energy storage, leptin secretion.
  • Brown adipose tissue: Thermogenesis (uncoupling protein UCP1).
  • Obesity: Excess body fat. Associated with insulin resistance, inflammation, cardiovascular disease.
  • Adipokines: Leptin (satiety), adiponectin (insulin‑sensitizing), resistin (pro‑inflammatory).
