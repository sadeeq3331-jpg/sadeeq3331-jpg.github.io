1. Cell Injury & Adaptation
Cells maintain homeostasis within a range of physiologic stress. When stress exceeds adaptive capacity, injury occurs.
Causes of Cell Injury
  • Hypoxia/Ischemia: Oxygen deprivation (ischemia worse than hypoxia).
  • Physical agents: Trauma, temperature, radiation.
  • Chemical agents: Toxins, drugs, pollutants.
  • Infectious agents: Viruses, bacteria, fungi, parasites.
  • Immunologic reactions: Autoimmunity, hypersensitivity.
  • Genetic abnormalities: Enzyme defects, mutations.
  • Nutritional imbalances: Deficiency or excess.
Cellular Adaptations
  • Atrophy: Decreased cell size (disuse, denervation, loss of nutrition).
  • Hypertrophy: Increased cell size (increased workload).
  • Hyperplasia: Increased cell number (hormonal, compensatory).
  • Metaplasia: Reversible change from one mature cell type to another (e.g., squamous metaplasia in smokers).
  • Dysplasia: Disordered growth (pre‑neoplastic).

3. Apoptosis
Apoptosis is programmed cell death, eliminating unwanted cells without inflammation.
  • Pathways:
    • Intrinsic (mitochondrial): Stress, DNA damage → BH3 proteins → cytochrome c release → caspase activation.
    • Extrinsic (death receptor): Fas‑FasL, TNF‑TNF receptor → caspase activation.
  • Morphology: Cell shrinkage, chromatin condensation (pyknosis), membrane blebbing, apoptotic bodies.
  • Examples: Embryonic development, hormone‑induced atrophy, cytotoxic T‑cell killing, viral hepatitis (Councilman bodies).
4. Autophagy
Autophagy is lysosomal digestion of cellular components; can be adaptive (survival under nutrient deprivation) or lead to autophagic cell death.
5. Acute Inflammation
Acute inflammation is a rapid, innate response to injury, characterized by vascular changes and neutrophil infiltration.
  • Vascular changes:
    • Transient vasoconstriction → vasodilation (histamine, NO) → increased blood flow (rubor, calor).
    • Increased vascular permeability (tumor, swelling) → exudate formation.
  • Cellular events:
    • Margination, rolling, adhesion (selectins, integrins).
    • Emigration (diapedesis) through vessel wall.
    • Chemotaxis (leukotrienes, chemokines).
    • Phagocytosis and degranulation.
  • Outcomes: Resolution, abscess formation, progression to chronic inflammation.
6. Chronic Inflammation
Chronic inflammation is prolonged, with lymphocytes, macrophages, plasma cells, and fibrosis.
  • Causes: Persistent infection (TB, viruses), autoimmune disease, prolonged toxin exposure, foreign bodies.
  • Cells: Macrophages (epithelioid, giant cells), lymphocytes, plasma cells, eosinophils (allergy, parasites).
  • Granulomatous inflammation: Compact collection of epithelioid macrophages, may form giant cells. Seen in TB, sarcoid, Crohn, fungal infections.

8. Tissue Repair & Regeneration
  • Regeneration: Replacement with identical cells (labile or stable cells). Requires intact stem cells and matrix.
  • Repair by connective tissue (scarring): When regeneration not possible.
  • Steps:
    • Angiogenesis (new vessels).
    • Fibroblast proliferation and collagen deposition.
    • Remodeling.
  • Growth factors: TGF‑β (fibrosis), PDGF, FGF, VEGF.
9. Fibrosis & Scarring
  • Excess collagen deposition in chronic inflammation or after severe injury.
  • Cirrhosis (liver), pulmonary fibrosis, myocardial fibrosis.
  • Keloid: Excessive scar beyond wound boundaries; genetic predisposition.
10. Edema & Effusions
  • Edema: Increased interstitial fluid. Mechanisms:
    • Increased hydrostatic pressure (heart failure).
    • Decreased oncotic pressure (hypoalbuminemia).
    • Increased capillary permeability (inflammation).
    • Lymphatic obstruction (filariasis).
  • Effusions: Fluid in body cavities (pleural, pericardial, peritoneal).
  • Transudate vs exudate: Transudate low protein, low specific gravity (hydrostatic); exudate high protein (inflammation).
11. Thrombosis & Embolism
Virchow's triad: Endothelial injury, stasis/turbulent flow, hypercoagulability.
  • Thrombus: Intravascular clot; arterial (platelet‑rich) vs venous (fibrin‑rich).
  • Fate of thrombus: Propagation, embolization, dissolution, organization (recanalization).
  • Embolism: Detached intravascular mass (thrombus, fat, air, amniotic fluid).
  • Infarction: Tissue death due to ischemia.
12. Infarction
  • Pale (white) infarct: Solid organs with end‑arterial supply (heart, kidney, spleen).
  • Red (hemorrhagic) infarct: Loose tissues (lung) or dual circulation (liver, intestine).
13. Shock
  • Hypovolemic: Hemorrhage, dehydration.
  • Cardiogenic: Pump failure.
  • Distributive: Septic, anaphylactic, neurogenic.
  • Obstructive: PE, tamponade, tension pneumothorax.
  • Pathology: Multi‑organ failure, DIC, ARDS.
14. Neoplasia: Definitions
  • Neoplasm: Abnormal mass of tissue, growth exceeds and is uncoordinated with normal tissue.
  • Benign: Well‑differentiated, slow growth, encapsulated, no metastasis.
  • Malignant: Poorly differentiated, rapid growth, invasive, metastatic.
  • Dysplasia: Disordered growth, not yet neoplastic.
  • Anaplasia: Lack of differentiation (hallmark of malignancy).
15. Carcinogenesis
  • Initiation: DNA mutation (e.g., from carcinogen).
  • Promotion: Proliferation of initiated cells (hormones, growth factors).
  • Progression: Accumulation of additional mutations, malignant behavior.
  • Oncogenes: Promote growth (RAS, MYC, HER2).
  • Tumor suppressor genes: Inhibit growth (p53, RB, APC).
  • DNA repair genes: e.g., BRCA, MSH2.
16. Tumor Progression & Metastasis
  • Invasion: Loss of cell adhesion, ECM degradation (proteases), migration.
  • Angiogenesis: VEGF, FGF – required for growth beyond 1‑2 mm.
  • Metastasis routes: Lymphatic (carcinomas), hematogenous (sarcomas), seeding (body cavities).
  • Epithelial‑mesenchymal transition (EMT): Cells gain migratory phenotype.
