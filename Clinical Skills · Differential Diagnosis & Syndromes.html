1. Introduction to Differential Diagnosis
Differential diagnosis (DDx) is the process of distinguishing a particular disease or condition from others that present with similar clinical features.
  • Generate a list: Based on epidemiology, risk factors, history, physical exam.
  • Prioritize: Consider life‑threatening (can't miss) diagnoses first, then common, then rare.
  • Use pattern recognition: Syndromes, clusters of symptoms.
  • Diagnostic tests: Use to confirm or rule out, considering sensitivity/specificity, likelihood ratios.
Mnemonic for acute chest pain: "PAIN" (Pericarditis, Angina/MI, Aortic dissection, Pneumonia/Pulmonary embolism). Always include "can't miss" diagnoses.
2. Bayesian Reasoning & Diagnostic Testing
  • Pretest probability: Estimate of disease likelihood before testing.
  • Sensitivity: Proportion of diseased who test positive (true positive rate).
  • Specificity: Proportion of non‑diseased who test negative (true negative rate).
  • Positive predictive value (PPV): Probability disease given positive test; depends on prevalence.
  • Negative predictive value (NPV): Probability no disease given negative test.
  • Likelihood ratio (LR): LR+ = sensitivity/(1‑specificity); LR‑ = (1‑sensitivity)/specificity. Post‑test odds = pretest odds × LR.
Example: D‑dimer for PE: high sensitivity, low specificity; good for ruling out when pretest probability low.
3. Chest Pain
Life‑threatening causes (must not miss)
  • Acute coronary syndrome (ACS): Crushing, substernal, radiation to left arm/jaw, diaphoresis; relieved by nitroglycerin.
  • Aortic dissection: Sudden tearing pain radiating to back; pulse or BP differential.
  • Pulmonary embolism: Pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea, tachypnea, hypoxia; risk factors (DVT, cancer).
  • Tension pneumothorax: Sudden dyspnea, hypotension, tracheal deviation, absent breath sounds.
  • Pericarditis: Sharp, positional (worse lying down), relieved by leaning forward; friction rub.
Other causes
  • Musculoskeletal: costochondritis (tender to palpation).
  • GI: GERD, esophageal spasm, peptic ulcer.
  • Respiratory: pneumonia, pleuritis.
  • Psychiatric: anxiety, panic.
4. Dyspnea
  • Cardiac: Heart failure (paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, orthopnea), ACS, arrhythmia.
  • Pulmonary: Asthma/COPD exacerbation, pneumonia, PE, interstitial lung disease.
  • Others: Anemia, metabolic acidosis (Kussmaul), deconditioning, anxiety.
5. Abdominal Pain
  • Right upper quadrant: Cholecystitis, hepatitis, cholangitis, duodenal ulcer.
  • Epigastric: Gastritis, GERD, pancreatitis, MI.
  • Left upper quadrant: Gastritis, pancreatitis, splenic infarction/rupture.
  • Periumbilical: Early appendicitis, small bowel obstruction, pancreatitis.
  • Right lower quadrant: Appendicitis, Crohn disease, ovarian pathology, ureteral stone.
  • Left lower quadrant: Diverticulitis, ulcerative colitis, ovarian pathology.
  • Diffuse: Peritonitis (surgical abdomen), mesenteric ischemia, IBS.
6. Headache
Dangerous causes
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage: Thunderclap, sudden, worst of life.
  • Meningitis: Fever, neck stiffness, photophobia.
  • Temporal arteritis: >50, jaw claudication, tender temporal artery, ↑ ESR.
  • Intracranial mass/tumor: Progressive, worse in morning, focal deficits.
  • Acute glaucoma: Eye pain, blurred vision, halos, hard globe.
Primary headaches
  • Migraine: throbbing, unilateral, nausea, photophobia.
  • Tension‑type: bilateral, pressing, mild to moderate.
  • Cluster: severe periorbital, autonomic features (tearing, rhinorrhea), multiple attacks.
7. Fever
  • Infectious: Localizing symptoms (cough, dysuria, etc.).
  • Non‑infectious: Autoimmune (lupus, Still), malignancy (lymphoma), drug fever, gout, PE, pancreatitis.
  • Fever of unknown origin (FUO): >38.3°C >3 weeks, no diagnosis after 1 week.
8. Fatigue
  • Sleep disorders: Sleep apnea, insomnia.
  • Psychiatric: Depression, anxiety.
  • Medical: Anemia, hypothyroidism, diabetes, CHF, CKD, chronic infection (EBV, HIV).
  • Medications: Beta‑blockers, sedatives.
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome: Diagnosis of exclusion.
9. Unintentional Weight Loss
  • Malignancy: GI, lung, lymphoma.
  • Endocrine: Diabetes, hyperthyroidism.
  • GI: Malabsorption (celiac, IBD), peptic ulcer.
  • Chronic infections: TB, HIV, fungal.
  • Psychiatric: Depression, eating disorders.
10. Syncope
  • Reflex (neurally mediated): Vasovagal, situational, carotid sinus.
  • Orthostatic hypotension: Volume depletion, autonomic dysfunction, drugs.
  • Cardiac: Arrhythmias (VT, heart block), structural (aortic stenosis, HCM).
  • Neurologic: Seizure, TIA.

12. Sepsis & SIRS
  • SIRS: Two or more: temp >38°C or <36°C, HR >90, RR >20 or PaCO₂ <32, WBC >12k or <4k or >10% bands.
  • Sepsis: SIRS + infection.
  • Severe sepsis: Sepsis + organ dysfunction.
  • Septic shock: Sepsis + hypotension despite adequate fluid resuscitation.
13. Multi‑organ Dysfunction Syndrome (MODS)
Progressive organ dysfunction in acutely ill patients, often due to uncontrolled inflammation (sepsis, trauma, pancreatitis). Criteria by organ system:
  • Respiratory: PaO₂/FiO₂ <200 (ARDS).
  • Renal: ↑ creatinine, ↓ urine output.
  • Hepatic: ↑ bilirubin, ↑ transaminases.
  • Cardiovascular: hypotension requiring vasopressors.
  • Hematologic: DIC, thrombocytopenia.
  • CNS: altered mental status.
14. Acute Coronary Syndromes
  • Unstable angina: Rest angina, new onset, increasing; no troponin rise.
  • NSTEMI: Positive troponin without ST elevation.
  • STEMI: ST elevation in contiguous leads; transmural ischemia.
  • Differential: Myocarditis, Takotsubo, aortic dissection, PE.
15. Heart Failure Syndromes
  • HFrEF (EF <40%): Ischemic, dilated cardiomyopathy.
  • HFpEF (EF ≥50%): Hypertension, aging, obesity.
  • Acute vs chronic exacerbation.
  • Right heart failure: Cor pulmonale, pulmonary hypertension; JVD, peripheral edema.
16. Arrhythmias
  • Bradyarrhythmias: Sick sinus, AV block (1st, 2nd, 3rd).
  • Supraventricular tachyarrhythmias: Atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, AVNRT, AVRT, focal atrial tachycardia.
  • Ventricular tachyarrhythmias: Monomorphic VT, polymorphic VT (torsade), VF.
17. Pneumonia
  • Community‑acquired (CAP): Typical (Strep pneumo, H flu) vs atypical (Mycoplasma, Legionella, Chlamydia).
  • Hospital‑acquired (HAP): >48h after admission; more resistant organisms.
  • Ventilator‑associated (VAP).
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Anaerobes, chemical pneumonitis.
18. Pulmonary Embolism
  • Massive PE: Hypotension/shock, RV dysfunction.
  • Submassive PE: RV dysfunction but normotensive.
  • Low‑risk PE: No RV dysfunction.

20. Upper & Lower GI Bleed
  • Upper (above ligament of Treitz): Hematemesis, coffee‑ground emesis, melena. Causes: PUD, varices, esophagitis, Mallory‑Weiss tear.
  • Lower: Hematochezia, maroon stool. Causes: diverticulosis, angiodysplasia, colitis, polyps, cancer.
  • Occult bleed: FOBT positive; anemia.
21. Jaundice
  • Pre‑hepatic (unconjugated): Hemolysis, Gilbert syndrome.
  • Hepatic (mixed): Hepatitis, cirrhosis, drug‑induced.
  • Post‑hepatic (obstructive): Choledocholithiasis, stricture, pancreatic cancer.
22. Acute & Chronic Diarrhea
  • Acute (<2 weeks): Infectious (viral, bacterial, protozoal).
  • Chronic (>4 weeks): IBS, IBD, malabsorption (celiac, pancreatic insufficiency), endocrine (hyperthyroid).
23. Stroke & TIA
  • Ischemic stroke: Sudden focal deficit; TOAST classification: large artery, cardioembolic, small vessel, other.
  • TIA: Transient (<1h) symptoms, no infarction on imaging.
  • Hemorrhagic stroke: ICH, SAH.
24. Seizure & Epilepsy
  • Generalized: Tonic‑clonic, absence, myoclonic, atonic.
  • Focal: Without dyscognitive features, with dyscognitive features (complex partial).
  • Provoked seizures: Electrolyte disturbance, drug withdrawal, infection, trauma.

26. DKA vs HHS
  • DKA: Hyperglycemia (>250), metabolic acidosis (pH<7.3, HCO₃<15), ketonemia, anion gap metabolic acidosis. More common in type 1.
  • HHS: Extreme hyperglycemia (>600), hyperosmolarity (>320), minimal ketosis, milder acidosis. Type 2.
27. Thyroid Storm vs Myxedema
  • Thyroid storm: Hyperpyrexia, tachycardia, altered mental status, nausea, diarrhea. Precipitated by infection, trauma.
  • Myxedema coma: Hypothermia, bradycardia, hypoventilation, altered mental status, hyponatremia.
28. Acute Monoarthritis vs Polyarthritis
  • Acute monoarthritis: Septic arthritis (emergency), gout, pseudogout, trauma.
  • Polyarthritis: RA, SLE, psoriatic, viral (parvovirus, hepatitis), reactive.
