1. Neurons – Structure & Function
What Is a Neuron?

Neurons are the fundamental units of the nervous system—specialized cells designed to receive, process, and transmit electrical and chemical signals. They are highly polarized, meaning different parts of the cell have distinct functions and structures [citation:8].

Structural Regions
  • Soma (cell body): Contains the nucleus and most of the protein‑synthesis machinery (rough ER, ribosomes). It is the metabolic center of the neuron.
  • Dendrites: Branched extensions that receive synaptic inputs from other neurons. Many dendrites contain ribosomes and can synthesize some proteins locally.
  • Axon: A single, long process that conducts action potentials away from the soma. It lacks ribosomes and relies on axonal transport for proteins and organelles [citation:8].
  • Axon terminals: Specialized endings that release neurotransmitters onto target cells.
Diagram of a neuron with labeled parts
Axonal Transport – The Neuron’s Highway

Because the axon cannot synthesize proteins, it depends on transport from the soma. Axonal transport moves organelles, vesicles, and proteins along microtubules [citation:8].

  • Fast anterograde transport: Moves vesicles and mitochondria from soma to terminals at ~400 mm/day, driven by kinesin.
  • Fast retrograde transport: Returns old membrane components, neurotrophic factors, and pathogens (e.g., tetanus toxin) to the soma, driven by dynein.
  • Slow transport: Moves cytoskeletal proteins (actin, tubulin) at 0.2–8 mm/day.
Clinical Pearl: Herpes simplex and rabies virus use retrograde transport to invade the CNS. Charcot‑Marie‑Tooth disease can result from mutations in kinesin genes.
Neuronal Classification

Neurons can be classified by:

  • Morphology: Unipolar, bipolar, pseudounipolar, multipolar.
  • Connectivity: Sensory, motor, interneurons.
  • Neurotransmitter: Glutamatergic, GABAergic, cholinergic, dopaminergic, etc.
  • Transcriptomic signature: Modern single‑cell RNA sequencing reveals dozens of molecularly distinct subtypes [citation:8].
2. Neuron‑Glial Cell Cooperation
Astrocytes

Astrocytes are star‑shaped glial cells that play a crucial role in homeostasis and synaptic function [citation:8].

  • Structural support: Form the blood‑brain barrier with endothelial cells.
  • Ion homeostasis: Take up excess K⁺ from extracellular space.
  • Neurotransmitter clearance: Remove glutamate (via EAAT transporters) and GABA.
  • Metabolic support: Supply lactate to neurons (lactate shuttle).
  • Synaptic modulation: Release gliotransmitters that influence synaptic transmission.
Oligodendrocytes & Myelin

Oligodendrocytes (CNS) and Schwann cells (PNS) form myelin sheaths around axons [citation:8].

  • Myelin structure: A lipid‑rich multilamellar wrap that increases membrane resistance and decreases capacitance.
  • Saltatory conduction: Action potentials jump between nodes of Ranvier, greatly increasing conduction velocity.
  • Myelin plasticity: Learning experiences can alter myelination, affecting circuit function [citation:8].
Demyelinating diseases: Multiple sclerosis (autoimmune attack on CNS myelin) and Guillain‑Barré syndrome (PNS) disrupt conduction, causing weakness, numbness, and visual disturbances.
Microglia

Microglia are the resident immune cells of the CNS. They constantly survey the environment, prune synapses during development, and respond to injury [citation:8].

3. Ionic Gradients & Membrane Potential
The Resting Membrane Potential

All cells have a voltage difference across their plasma membrane, typically –60 to –70 mV (inside negative) for neurons. This arises from [citation:8]:

  • Ion concentration gradients: Maintained by active pumps (Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase: 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in).
  • Selective permeability: At rest, membrane is most permeable to K⁺ (leak channels).
Nernst equation: Calculates the equilibrium potential for an ion:

Eion = (RT/zF) ln ([ion]out / [ion]in)

At 37°C, for K⁺, EK ≈ –90 mV; for Na⁺, ENa ≈ +60 mV.

The actual resting potential is close to EK because of high K⁺ permeability, but Na⁺ leak shifts it slightly positive. The Goldman‑Hodgkin‑Katz equation accounts for multiple permeant ions.

Electrochemical Gradient

The driving force on an ion is the difference between the membrane potential (Vm) and the ion’s equilibrium potential (Eion). When Vm ≠ Eion, a current flows if channels are open [citation:8].

4. Voltage‑Gated Channels & Action Potentials
The Action Potential – Hodgkin‑Huxley Model

The action potential (AP) is a rapid, all‑or‑none change in membrane potential that propagates along axons. It results from the coordinated opening and closing of voltage‑gated Na⁺ and K⁺ channels [citation:5][citation:8].

Phases
  1. Resting: Vm ≈ –70 mV; Na⁺ channels closed, K⁺ channels closed.
  2. Depolarization: Stimulus opens voltage‑gated Na⁺ channels; Na⁺ influx drives Vm toward ENa (≈ +30 mV).
  3. Repolarization: Na⁺ channels inactivate; voltage‑gated K⁺ channels open; K⁺ efflux restores negative potential.
  4. Hyperpolarization/afterpotential: K⁺ channels close slowly, sometimes causing a brief undershoot.
Action potential waveform with labeled phases
Voltage‑Gated Sodium Channels
  • Structure: α subunit forms the pore; β subunits modulate function. Contains voltage sensor (S4 segment) and inactivation gate (III‑IV linker) [citation:8].
  • States: Closed (resting) → open (depolarized) → inactivated (refractory).
  • Refractory periods: Absolute (Na⁺ channels inactivated) and relative (some Na⁺ channels recovered, but K⁺ conductance high).
Channelopathies: Mutations in Na⁺ channel genes (e.g., SCN1A) cause epilepsy syndromes (Dravet syndrome), paramyotonia congenita, and long QT syndrome [citation:5].
5. Action Potential Propagation
Continuous vs. Saltatory Conduction

In unmyelinated axons, APs propagate continuously—each segment depolarizes the next. In myelinated axons, APs regenerate only at nodes of Ranvier, where Na⁺ channels are concentrated. This saltatory conduction is faster and more energy‑efficient [citation:8].

6. Ion Channel Diversity & Channelopathies

Beyond Na⁺ and K⁺ channels, neurons express a vast array of ion channels with distinct properties [citation:5][citation:8]:

  • Voltage‑gated calcium channels: Cav1–3 families; mediate neurotransmitter release, gene expression.
  • Voltage‑gated potassium channels: Kv1–12; control repolarization, firing frequency.
  • Hyperpolarization‑activated cyclic nucleotide‑gated (HCN) channels: Generate pacemaker currents (Ih) in rhythmic neurons.
  • Two‑pore K⁺ channels (K2P): Responsible for background (leak) K⁺ currents.

Channelopathies (genetic ion channel defects) underlie many neurologic disorders: epilepsy, migraine, ataxia, periodic paralysis, and cardiac arrhythmias [citation:5].

7. Synaptic Transmission – Overview

Synaptic transmission is the process by which one neuron communicates with another (or with a muscle/gland). It occurs at specialized junctions called synapses [citation:5].

  • Electrical synapses: Gap junctions connect cytoplasm; rapid, bidirectional, often in escape circuits.
  • Chemical synapses: Neurotransmitter released from presynaptic terminal binds to postsynaptic receptors; slower but modulatable.
8. Neurotransmitters & Receptors

Neurotransmitters are chemicals that mediate synaptic transmission. They are classified by structure and function [citation:5][citation:8]:

9. Ionotropic Receptors (Fast Transmission)

Ionotropic receptors are ligand‑gated ion channels. Neurotransmitter binding directly opens the channel, causing rapid (millisecond) changes in membrane potential [citation:5][citation:8].

  • AMPA receptors: Permeable to Na⁺ and K⁺; mediate fast excitatory transmission.
  • NMDA receptors: Permeable to Na⁺, K⁺, and Ca²⁺; require both glutamate and depolarization (Mg²⁺ block); key for plasticity.
  • GABAA receptors: Permeable to Cl⁻; mediate fast inhibition (hyperpolarization).
  • Nicotinic ACh receptors: Permeable to Na⁺, K⁺; mediate fast excitation at neuromuscular junction.

Benzodiazepines and barbiturates potentiate GABAA receptors; ketamine blocks NMDA receptors (anesthetic, dissociative).

10. Metabotropic Receptors & Second Messengers

Metabotropic receptors are G‑protein‑coupled receptors (GPCRs). They act indirectly via G proteins and second messengers, producing slower but longer‑lasting effects [citation:5].

  • Gs: Activates adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP → activates PKA.
  • Gi: Inhibits adenylyl cyclase → ↓cAMP.
  • Gq: Activates phospholipase C → IP₃ and DAG → ↑Ca²⁺, PKC.
11. Neurotransmitter Release & Vesicle Cycling

Action potential invades the presynaptic terminal, opening voltage‑gated Ca²⁺ channels. Ca²⁺ influx triggers vesicle fusion via SNARE proteins (synaptobrevin, syntaxin, SNAP‑25) [citation:5][citation:8].

  • Vesicle pools: Readily releasable, recycling, and reserve pools.
  • Endocytosis: Vesicles retrieved (clathrin‑mediated) and refilled with neurotransmitter.

Tetanus and botulinum toxins are proteases that cleave SNARE proteins, blocking neurotransmitter release → spastic paralysis (tetanus) or flaccid paralysis (botulism).

12. Synaptic Plasticity (LTP, LTD)

Long‑term potentiation (LTP) and long‑term depression (LTD) are lasting changes in synaptic strength, considered cellular correlates of learning and memory [citation:5].

  • LTP: High‑frequency stimulation → NMDA receptor activation → Ca²⁺ influx → insertion of AMPA receptors, structural changes.
  • LTD: Low‑frequency stimulation → moderate Ca²⁺ rise → AMPA receptor internalization.
13. General Principles of Sensory Transduction

Sensory receptors convert stimulus energy (light, sound, touch, chemical) into electrical signals (receptor potential). Key concepts [citation:5]:

  • Adequate stimulus: Each receptor is most sensitive to one modality.
  • Receptive field: Area that, when stimulated, alters receptor firing.
  • Adaptation: Phasic receptors (rapidly adapting) vs. tonic receptors (slowly adapting).
  • Labeled line: Specific pathways for each modality.
14. Somatosensory System

Transmits touch, proprioception, temperature, and pain.

  • Dorsal column‑medial lemniscus pathway: Fine touch, vibration, proprioception; decussates in medulla.
  • Spinothalamic tract: Pain, temperature, crude touch; decussates at spinal cord.
15. Pain & Nociception

Nociceptors are free nerve endings responding to mechanical, thermal, or chemical stimuli. Substance P and glutamate are key neurotransmitters [citation:8].

  • Fast pain: Aδ fibers; sharp, localized.
  • Slow pain: C fibers; dull, burning, diffuse.
16. Vision – Photoreceptors & Retina

Photoreceptors (rods and cones) hyperpolarize in response to light. The phototransduction cascade [citation:5][citation:8]:

  1. Light activates rhodopsin → activates transducin (Gt).
  2. Transducin activates phosphodiesterase → cGMP breakdown.
  3. ↓cGMP closes Na⁺ channels (dark current stops) → hyperpolarization.

Retinitis pigmentosa involves progressive rod degeneration; gene therapy (RPE65) has shown success in some forms [citation:5].

17. Audition – Cochlea & Hair Cells

Hair cells in the organ of Corti transduce sound. Deflection of stereocilia opens mechanically gated channels (tip links) → depolarization → neurotransmitter release [citation:5].

18. Vestibular System
Detects head position and movement via otolith organs and semicircular canals; hair cells similar to cochlea.
19. Olfaction & Taste
Olfactory receptors are GPCRs; each neuron expresses one receptor type. Taste cells use ionotropic (salty, sour) and metabotropic (sweet, bitter, umami) mechanisms [citation:5].
20. Motor Unit & Muscle Contraction
A motor unit = one α‑motor neuron + all fibers it innervates. Excitation‑contraction coupling: AP → SR Ca²⁺ release → cross‑bridge cycling.
21. Spinal Reflexes
Monosynaptic (stretch reflex) and polysynaptic (flexor withdrawal) reflexes are mediated by spinal circuits [citation:2].
22. Brainstem Control of Posture
Vestibular nuclei, reticulospinal tracts maintain upright posture against gravity.
23. Motor Cortex & Corticospinal Tract
Primary motor cortex (M1) encodes force and direction; corticospinal tract decussates at medulla [citation:2].
24. Basal Ganglia

Basal ganglia (caudate, putamen, globus pallidus, subthalamic nucleus, substantia nigra) modulate movement via direct (facilitatory) and indirect (inhibitory) pathways [citation:2].

Parkinson disease: loss of nigrostriatal dopamine → hypokinesia, rigidity, tremor. Huntington disease: loss of striatal GABA neurons → hyperkinesia (chorea).

25. Cerebellum

Coordinates movement, balance, and motor learning. Purkinje cells provide sole output; cerebellar dysfunction causes ataxia, dysmetria, intention tremor [citation:2].

26. Autonomic Nervous System
Sympathetic (fight/flight) and parasympathetic (rest/digest) divisions use two‑neuron chains (preganglionic → postganglionic).
27. Sleep & EEG Rhythms
EEG records cortical activity. Sleep stages: NREM (slow waves) and REM (fast, desynchronized). Sleep cycles are regulated by brainstem and hypothalamic circuits [citation:4].
28. Learning & Memory (Engrams)

Memories are stored as engrams—sets of neurons activated during learning that can be reactivated during recall [citation:8]. Hippocampus is critical for declarative memory.

29. Language & Cognition
Broca (production) and Wernicke (comprehension) areas; aphasias result from lesions.
30. Consciousness & Brain Death
Consciousness requires intact brainstem arousal systems and cortical activity. Brain death = irreversible loss of all brain function (including brainstem).
