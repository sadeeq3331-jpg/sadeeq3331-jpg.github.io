1. Introduction to Hematology Pharmacology

Hematology pharmacology includes drugs that affect blood cell production, coagulation, and treatment of hematologic malignancies. Key areas:

  • Hematinics: Iron, vitamin B12, folate, erythropoietin.
  • Antithrombotics: Antiplatelets, anticoagulants, thrombolytics.
  • Hemostatic agents: Desmopressin, factor concentrates, antifibrinolytics.
  • Antineoplastics: Chemotherapy for leukemias, lymphomas, myeloma.
2. Drugs for Anemia
Iron Preparations
  • Oral iron: Ferrous sulfate, ferrous gluconate, ferrous fumarate. Absorbed in duodenum. Adverse effects: GI upset, constipation, dark stools.
  • IV iron: Iron dextran, ferric gluconate, iron sucrose. Used in malabsorption, chronic kidney disease, intolerance to oral iron. Risk of anaphylaxis (especially with dextran).
Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin)
  • Cyanocobalamin, hydroxocobalamin. Used for B12 deficiency (pernicious anemia, malabsorption). Route: oral (high doses) or IM. Hydroxocobalamin is preferred in the UK, longer duration.
Folic Acid
  • Used for folate deficiency. Given orally. In pregnancy, 400‑800 mcg/day prevents neural tube defects.
Erythropoiesis‑Stimulating Agents (ESAs)
  • Epoetin alfa, darbepoetin alfa. Recombinant human erythropoietin. Used in anemia of CKD, chemotherapy‑induced anemia, HIV. Adverse effects: hypertension, thrombosis, pure red cell aplasia (rare).

Clinical pearl: Iron studies should be monitored in patients on ESAs; functional iron deficiency may develop.

3. Antiplatelet Drugs
  • Aspirin: Irreversibly inhibits COX‑1, reducing thromboxane A₂ → inhibits platelet aggregation. Used for secondary prevention of MI, stroke. Adverse effects: GI bleeding, ulcers.
  • P2Y₁₂ inhibitors:
    • Clopidogrel, prasugrel, ticagrelor. Block ADP receptor. Clopidogrel is a prodrug (CYP2C19 activation); prasugrel more potent, contraindicated in prior stroke; ticagrelor reversible, direct‑acting.
    • Adverse effects: Bleeding, dyspnea (ticagrelor).
  • GP IIb/IIIa inhibitors: Abciximab, eptifibatide, tirofiban (IV). Used in acute coronary syndromes, PCI. Thrombocytopenia risk.
  • Dipyridamole: Inhibits phosphodiesterase, increases cAMP. Often combined with aspirin (Aggrenox) for stroke prevention.
4. Anticoagulants
Heparin and Derivatives
  • Unfractionated heparin (UFH): Activates antithrombin III, inhibiting thrombin and factor Xa. Monitored by aPTT. Antidote: protamine sulfate.
  • Low molecular weight heparin (LMWH): Enoxaparin, dalteparin. More predictable, anti‑Xa > anti‑IIa. SC, less HIT risk. Protamine only partially reverses.
  • Fondaparinux: Synthetic pentasaccharide, selective anti‑Xa via antithrombin. No HIT, no antidote.
Vitamin K Antagonists
  • Warfarin: Inhibits vitamin K‑dependent synthesis of factors II, VII, IX, X and proteins C, S. Monitored by INR (target 2‑3 for most). Antidote: vitamin K, fresh frozen plasma, PCC.
Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)
  • Direct thrombin inhibitor: Dabigatran (prodrug). Antidote: idarucizumab.
  • Factor Xa inhibitors: Rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban. Antidote: andexanet alfa.

Clinical pearl: Warfarin is teratogenic; DOACs contraindicated in mechanical valves.

5. Thrombolytics (Fibrinolytics)
  • Alteplase (tPA), reteplase, tenecteplase, streptokinase. Convert plasminogen to plasmin, degrading fibrin clots. Used in STEMI, acute ischemic stroke, massive PE.
  • Contraindications: Active bleeding, recent surgery, stroke, severe hypertension.
  • Adverse effects: Bleeding, anaphylaxis (streptokinase).
6. Drugs for Bleeding Disorders
  • Desmopressin (DDAVP): Increases vWF and factor VIII release from endothelium. Used in mild hemophilia A, von Willebrand disease type 1, and uremic bleeding.
  • Factor VIII concentrates: For hemophilia A (recombinant or plasma‑derived).
  • Factor IX concentrates: For hemophilia B.
  • von Willebrand factor concentrates: For severe vWD.
  • Fresh frozen plasma (FFP): Contains all coagulation factors; used for multiple factor deficiencies (DIC, liver disease).
  • Cryoprecipitate: Rich in factor VIII, vWF, fibrinogen, factor XIII. Used in fibrinogen deficiency, DIC.
  • Tranexamic acid, ε‑aminocaproic acid: Antifibrinolytics; inhibit plasminogen activation. Used in mucosal bleeding, dental extraction in hemophiliacs.
  • Phytonadione (vitamin K1): For warfarin reversal, vitamin K deficiency.
7. Chemotherapy Agents for Hematologic Malignancies
Acute Leukemia
  • Cytarabine (Ara‑C): Antimetabolite, used in AML and ALL. Side effects: myelosuppression, cerebellar toxicity.
  • Daunorubicin, idarubicin: Anthracyclines, topoisomerase II inhibitors; cardiotoxicity.
  • Methotrexate: Antifolate, used in ALL and lymphoma; leucovorin rescue.
  • 6‑Mercaptopurine (6‑MP): Purine antagonist; metabolized by TPMT.
  • Asparaginase: Depletes asparagine in ALL; pancreatitis, thrombosis.
Lymphoma
  • R‑CHOP: Rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, prednisone.
  • Brentuximab vedotin: Anti‑CD30 drug conjugate for Hodgkin lymphoma.
Multiple Myeloma
  • Bortezomib, carfilzomib: Proteasome inhibitors. Peripheral neuropathy (bortezomib).
  • Lenalidomide, pomalidomide: Immunomodulators (IMiDs). Teratogenic, thrombosis risk.
  • Daratumumab: Anti‑CD38 monoclonal antibody.
8. Immunomodulators in Hematology
  • Rituximab (anti‑CD20): Used in B‑cell lymphomas, CLL, ITP, autoimmune hemolytic anemia.
  • Eculizumab: Anti‑C5 monoclonal antibody; inhibits terminal complement. Used in paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH), atypical HUS.
  • Eltrombopag, romiplostim: Thrombopoietin receptor agonists for ITP. Risk of thrombosis, rebound thrombocytopenia.
9. Drugs for Sickle Cell Disease
  • Hydroxyurea: Increases HbF, reduces sickling. Reduces pain crises, acute chest syndrome. Adverse effects: myelosuppression, teratogenicity.
  • L‑glutamine: Reduces oxidative stress, decreases vaso‑occlusive crises.
  • Crizanlizumab: Anti‑P‑selectin monoclonal antibody, reduces pain crises.
  • Voxelotor: Inhibits HbS polymerization, increases hemoglobin.
10. Supportive Care Agents
  • Granulocyte colony‑stimulating factors (G‑CSF): Filgrastim, pegfilgrastim. Used for chemotherapy‑induced neutropenia. Bone pain.
  • Granulocyte‑macrophage CSF (GM‑CSF): Sargramostim.
  • Allopurinol, rasburicase: For tumor lysis syndrome (reduce uric acid).
  • Folinic acid (leucovorin): Rescues normal cells from methotrexate toxicity.
