1. Overview & Structure of the GI Tract
What Is the Gastrointestinal Tract?

The gastrointestinal (GI) tract is a continuous tube from mouth to anus that processes food: ingestion, digestion, absorption, and elimination. It includes the oral cavity, pharynx, esophagus, stomach, small intestine (duodenum, jejunum, ileum), large intestine, rectum, and anus. Accessory organs – salivary glands, liver, gallbladder, pancreas – secrete enzymes and bile.

Layers of the GI Wall

From inside out: mucosa (epithelium, lamina propria, muscularis mucosae), submucosa (connective tissue, blood vessels, submucosal plexus), muscularis externa (inner circular, outer longitudinal smooth muscle; myenteric plexus between them), serosa (connective tissue covered by visceral peritoneum).

Diagram of GI wall layers
Enteric Nervous System (ENS)

The ENS is the “little brain” of the gut, consisting of two plexuses:

  • Myenteric (Auerbach’s) plexus: between circular and longitudinal muscle; controls motility.
  • Submucosal (Meissner’s) plexus: in submucosa; controls secretion and blood flow.

The ENS receives input from the autonomic nervous system but can function independently.

2. GI Motility
Types of Movements
  • Peristalsis: Waves of contraction that propel contents aborally. Involves contraction behind and relaxation ahead (law of the gut).
  • Segmentation: Mixing contractions that chop and mix chyme; more frequent in small intestine.
  • Tonic contractions: Sustained contractions (e.g., sphincters).
Chewing and Swallowing

Chewing (mastication) mechanically breaks food, mixes with saliva. Swallowing (deglutition) has three phases: oral (voluntary), pharyngeal (involuntary, reflex), esophageal (peristalsis). The lower esophageal sphincter (LES) relaxes to allow bolus entry.

Gastric Motility

Receptive relaxation (vagovagal reflex) allows stomach to accommodate food. Peristaltic waves mix and grind chyme. Liquids empty faster; fats slow gastric emptying via enterogastric reflexes and hormones (CCK, secretin).

Migrating Motor Complex (MMC)

During fasting, cycles of strong peristalsis (every 90 min) sweep residual contents from stomach to colon. Disrupted in bacterial overgrowth.

Defecation

Distension of rectum initiates parasympathetic reflex, relaxes internal anal sphincter, voluntary relaxation of external sphincter allows expulsion.

3. Salivary Secretion

Saliva is produced by parotid (serous), submandibular (mixed), and sublingual (mucous) glands. Daily volume ~1‑1.5 L. Functions: lubrication, starch digestion (α‑amylase), antibacterial (lysozyme, IgA), buffer (bicarbonate).

Composition

Primary secretion (acini) is isotonic; as it flows through ducts, Na⁺ and Cl⁻ are reabsorbed, K⁺ and HCO₃⁻ secreted → hypotonic saliva. Aldosterone increases Na⁺ reabsorption.

Regulation

Exclusively neural: parasympathetic (ACh, M3) → abundant watery saliva; sympathetic (norepinephrine) → small volume, thick mucus. Sight, smell, thought of food stimulate salivation (cephalic phase).

4. Gastric Secretion

Gastric glands contain parietal cells (HCl, intrinsic factor), chief cells (pepsinogen), mucous cells (mucus), G cells (gastrin), D cells (somatostatin).

HCl Secretion

Parietal cells secrete H⁺ via H⁺/K⁺ ATPase. Stimulated by ACh (vagus), gastrin (G cells), histamine (paracrine from ECL cells). Inhibited by somatostatin (D cells), prostaglandins. H₂ blockers and PPIs inhibit acid.

Phases of Gastric Secretion
  • Cephalic: sight/smell/thought → vagus → stimulates parietal cells and G cells.
  • Gastric: distension, peptides → gastrin release → acid secretion.
  • Intestinal: arrival of chyme in duodenum triggers inhibitory hormones (secretin, CCK, GIP) to slow gastric emptying and secretion.

Zollinger‑Ellison syndrome: gastrinoma (usually pancreas) → massive acid → ulcers.

5. Pancreatic Secretion

Exocrine pancreas secretes ~1.5 L/day of alkaline fluid rich in digestive enzymes. Acinar cells secrete enzymes; ductal cells secrete bicarbonate.

Enzymes
  • Proteases (trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, procarboxypeptidase) – activated in duodenum by enterokinase.
  • Lipase, colipase, phospholipase A₂.
  • Amylase.
Bicarbonate Secretion

Ductal cells secrete HCO₃⁻ (via CFTR and Cl⁻/HCO₃⁻ exchanger) to neutralize gastric acid.

Regulation

Secretin (from S cells in duodenum in response to acid) stimulates HCO₃⁻. CCK (from I cells in response to fats/amino acids) stimulates enzyme secretion. Vagal stimulation also increases secretion.

6. Bile Secretion & Gallbladder Function

Bile (0.5‑1 L/day) is produced by hepatocytes, stored and concentrated in gallbladder. Contains bile salts (emulsify fats), phospholipids, cholesterol, bilirubin, electrolytes.

Bile Salts

Synthesized from cholesterol, conjugated with glycine or taurine. They form micelles essential for fat absorption. Enterohepatic circulation recycles bile salts.

Gallbladder Emptying

CCK causes gallbladder contraction and relaxation of sphincter of Oddi, delivering bile into duodenum. Vagal stimulation also promotes contraction.

7. Intestinal Secretion

Crypt cells secrete isotonic fluid rich in HCO₃⁻. Villus cells absorb nutrients and electrolytes. Secretion is stimulated by luminal distension, toxins (e.g., cholera toxin via cAMP), and hormones.

Cholera toxin permanently activates Gs → ↑cAMP → massive fluid secretion → watery diarrhea.

8. Carbohydrate Digestion & Absorption

Starch (amylose, amylopectin) broken down by salivary and pancreatic α‑amylase to maltose, maltotriose, α‑dextrins. Brush border enzymes (maltase, sucrase, lactase, isomaltase) split disaccharides into monosaccharides.

Glucose and galactose are absorbed via SGLT1 (Na⁺‑dependent). Fructose via GLUT5 (facilitated). All exit via GLUT2.

Lactase deficiency → lactose intolerance (bloating, diarrhea).

9. Protein Digestion & Absorption

Pepsin (stomach) begins digestion. Pancreatic proteases (trypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase, carboxypeptidases) produce oligopeptides and amino acids. Brush border peptidases further break down to amino acids, di‑, tripeptides.

Absorption: amino acids via Na⁺‑dependent transporters; di/tripeptides via PEPT1 (H⁺‑dependent).

10. Fat Digestion & Absorption

Triglycerides emulsified by bile salts. Pancreatic lipase (with colipase) hydrolyzes to fatty acids and monoglycerides. Bile salts form micelles that ferry lipids to enterocyte surface.

Inside enterocyte, re‑esterified to triglycerides, packaged into chylomicrons (with apolipoproteins), secreted into lymph (lacteals).

Steatorrhea: fat in stool due to malabsorption (e.g., pancreatic insufficiency, bile duct obstruction).

11. Water & Electrolyte Absorption

Water follows osmotic gradients – absorbed passively throughout small and large intestine. Na⁺ absorbed via multiple mechanisms (Na⁺/H⁺ exchange, Na⁺‑nutrient cotransport, ENaC in colon). Cl⁻ absorbed via Cl⁻/HCO₃⁻ exchange. K⁺ is secreted in colon.

12. Vitamin Absorption

Fat‑soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K): incorporated into micelles, absorbed with fats.

Vitamin B12: binds intrinsic factor (IF) in stomach, absorbed in terminal ileum via IF‑B12 receptors.

Folate, thiamine, other B vitamins: absorbed by specific transporters.

13. Liver Physiology

The liver is the largest internal organ. Key functions:

  • Metabolic: glucose homeostasis (glycogenesis, gluconeogenesis), lipid metabolism (synthesis of cholesterol, lipoproteins), protein synthesis (albumin, clotting factors).
  • Detoxification: phase I (cytochrome P450) and phase II (conjugation) reactions.
  • Bile production: as above.
  • Storage: glycogen, iron, vitamins.
Liver Lobule

The classic lobule is centered on a central vein, with portal triads at the periphery. Blood flows from portal venule and hepatic arteriole through sinusoids to central vein. Hepatocytes are arranged in plates; bile canaliculi carry bile to bile ducts.

Hepatic encephalopathy results from failure to detoxify ammonia and other neurotoxins.

15. Gut‑Brain Axis & Integrated Responses

The gut and brain communicate bidirectionally via vagus, sympathetic nerves, and hormones (e.g., ghrelin, leptin). This axis regulates appetite, satiety, and gut function.

Vago‑vagal reflexes: gastric distension → vagal afferents → brainstem → vagal efferents → altered motility/secretion.

Entero‑enteric reflexes: local reflexes within ENS coordinate peristalsis and secretion.
