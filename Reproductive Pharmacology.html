1. Introduction to Reproductive Pharmacology

Reproductive pharmacology encompasses drugs that modulate the hypothalamic‑pituitary‑gonadal (HPG) axis, sex hormones, and their receptors, as well as agents used in contraception, infertility, menopause, and reproductive cancers.

  • Hormonal contraceptives: estrogens + progestins (or progestin‑only).
  • SERMs (Selective Estrogen Receptor Modulators) have tissue‑specific effects.
  • GnRH agonists/antagonists used for hormone‑sensitive cancers and infertility.
2. Estrogens & Progestins
Estrogens
  • Natural estrogens: Estradiol, estrone, estriol.
  • Synthetic: Ethinyl estradiol (oral contraceptive), conjugated equine estrogens (HRT).
  • Mechanism: Bind estrogen receptors (ERα, ERβ), regulate gene transcription.
  • Clinical uses: Hormone replacement therapy (HRT), contraception, osteoporosis prevention, atrophic vaginitis.
  • Adverse effects: Thromboembolism, breast tenderness, nausea, endometrial hyperplasia (if unopposed), increased risk of breast cancer with long‑term use.
Progestins
  • Natural progesterone: Micronized progesterone (oral, vaginal).
  • Synthetic progestins: Medroxyprogesterone acetate, norethindrone, levonorgestrel, drospirenone.
  • Mechanism: Bind progesterone receptors; convert proliferative endometrium to secretory.
  • Clinical uses: Contraception, HRT (with estrogen), endometriosis, amenorrhea, luteal phase support.
  • Adverse effects: Weight gain, depression, breakthrough bleeding, fluid retention; drospirenone (spironolactone analog) may cause hyperkalemia.

Clinical pearl: Unopposed estrogen increases endometrial cancer risk; adding progestin mitigates this.

3. Oral Contraceptives & Hormonal Contraception
Combined Oral Contraceptives (COCs)
  • Contain estrogen (ethinyl estradiol) + progestin.
  • Mechanism: Suppress LH/FSH (negative feedback), thicken cervical mucus, alter endometrium.
  • Non‑contraceptive benefits: reduce acne, dysmenorrhea, ovarian/endometrial cancer risk.
  • Adverse effects: Thromboembolism (↑ risk with smoking), hypertension, migraine, weight gain.
Progestin‑Only Pills (POPs)
  • Mechanism: Thicken cervical mucus, thin endometrium; may not consistently suppress ovulation.
  • Advantage: No estrogen, safe for breastfeeding, smokers.
Long‑Acting Reversible Contraceptives (LARCs)
  • Levonorgestrel IUD (Mirena): Local progestin; lasts 5‑7 years.
  • Copper IUD (Paragard): Spermicidal; lasts 10 years; may increase bleeding.
  • Etonogestrel implant (Nexplanon): Subdermal; lasts 3 years.
  • Medroxyprogesterone acetate injection (Depo‑Provera): IM every 3 months; weight gain, bone density loss (reversible).
Emergency Contraception
  • Levonorgestrel (Plan B): Progestin‑only; effective up to 72 hours.
  • Ulipristal acetate (Ella): Progesterone receptor modulator; effective up to 120 hours.
  • Copper IUD: Most effective emergency contraception.

Clinical pearl: COCs increase risk of DVT/PE, especially in smokers over 35. Screening for contraindications essential.

4. Androgens & Anti‑Androgens
Androgens
  • Testosterone: Available as injectable, transdermal, buccal, gel. Used for male hypogonadism, delayed puberty.
  • Adverse effects: Erythrocytosis, acne, sleep apnea, prostate enlargement, gynecomastia (via aromatization).
Anabolic Steroids
  • Modified androgens with greater anabolic:androgenic ratio. Abuse can cause hepatotoxicity, dyslipidemia, infertility, mood changes.
Anti‑Androgens
  • Cyproterone acetate: Progestin with anti‑androgen effects; used for hirsutism, prostate cancer.
  • Spironolactone: Aldosterone antagonist; also blocks androgen receptors; used for hirsutism, acne, female pattern hair loss.
  • Flutamide, bicalutamide: Non‑steroidal androgen receptor antagonists; used for prostate cancer. Hepatotoxicity (flutamide).
  • Finasteride, dutasteride: 5α‑reductase inhibitors; block conversion of testosterone to DHT; used for BPH, male pattern baldness.

Clinical pearl: Finasteride reduces PSA by ~50%; adjust PSA values.

5. Drugs for Infertility
  • Clomiphene citrate: SERM; blocks estrogen negative feedback → ↑ FSH/LH → ovulation induction. Used in anovulatory infertility. Side effects: hot flashes, multiple pregnancy, visual disturbances.
  • Letrozole (aromatase inhibitor): Off‑label for ovulation induction; reduces estrogen, increasing FSH. May have lower multiple pregnancy risk.
  • Gonadotropins: FSH (follitropin alfa, beta), LH, hCG (trigger ovulation). Used in assisted reproduction. Risk: OHSS (ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome).
  • GnRH agonists (leuprolide, buserelin): Initially stimulate, then downregulate gonadotropins; used to prevent premature LH surge in IVF.
  • GnRH antagonists (cetrorelix, ganirelix): Immediate blockade; used in IVF to prevent LH surge.
  • Bromocriptine, cabergoline: Dopamine agonists; lower prolactin in hyperprolactinemic infertility.

Clinical pearl: OHSS presents with abdominal pain, ascites, hemoconcentration; risk factors: high estradiol, PCOS.

6. Drugs for Menopause & Osteoporosis
  • Hormone replacement therapy (HRT): Estrogen ± progestin for menopausal symptoms (hot flashes, vaginal atrophy). Risks: thromboembolism, breast cancer (with combined therapy), endometrial cancer (if estrogen alone).
  • Tibolone: Synthetic steroid with estrogenic, progestogenic, and androgenic activity; used for menopausal symptoms.
  • SERMs (raloxifene, tamoxifen): Raloxifene used for osteoporosis (agonist in bone, antagonist in breast/uterus); tamoxifen for breast cancer prevention (agonist in bone, antagonist in breast, partial agonist in uterus).
  • Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate): Inhibit bone resorption; used for osteoporosis.
  • Denosumab: RANKL inhibitor; for osteoporosis.
  • Teriparatide: PTH analog; anabolic for severe osteoporosis.

Clinical pearl: Raloxifene does not stimulate endometrium, so no increased risk of endometrial cancer.

7. Drugs for Labor & Lactation
Oxytocics (Uterine Stimulants)
  • Oxytocin: Stimulates uterine contractions; used for labor induction, augmentation, and postpartum hemorrhage. Adverse effects: water intoxication (due to ADH‑like effect).
  • Ergot alkaloids (ergonovine, methylergonovine): Increase uterine tone; used for postpartum hemorrhage. Contraindicated in hypertension, preeclampsia (vasoconstriction).
  • Prostaglandins (dinoprostone PGE₂, misoprostol PGE₁): Ripen cervix, induce labor; misoprostol also used for postpartum hemorrhage.
Tocolytics (Uterine Relaxants)
  • Beta‑2 agonists (terbutaline): Relax uterus; used for preterm labor (off‑label). Side effects: tachycardia, hyperglycemia.
  • Magnesium sulfate: Tocolytic; also used for eclampsia prophylaxis. Monitor for magnesium toxicity (loss of DTRs, respiratory depression).
  • Nifedipine (calcium channel blocker): Inhibits uterine contractions; used for preterm labor.
  • Indomethacin (NSAID): Inhibits prostaglandin synthesis; short‑term tocolytic. Avoid after 32 weeks (constricts ductus arteriosus).
Lactation
  • Cabergoline, bromocriptine: Dopamine agonists; suppress prolactin, used to inhibit lactation (e.g., stillbirth).
8. Drugs for Reproductive Cancers
Breast Cancer
  • SERMs (tamoxifen): First‑line for ER+ breast cancer (adjuvant, metastatic). Side effects: hot flashes, thromboembolism, endometrial cancer.
  • Aromatase inhibitors (anastrozole, letrozole, exemestane): Inhibit peripheral estrogen synthesis; used in postmenopausal women. Side effects: arthralgia, osteoporosis.
  • Fulvestrant: ER antagonist (downregulator); used in metastatic breast cancer.
  • Trastuzumab (HER2/neu antibody): For HER2+ breast cancer; cardiotoxicity (monitor LVEF).
  • Palbociclib, ribociclib: CDK4/6 inhibitors; used with aromatase inhibitors.
Prostate Cancer
  • GnRH agonists (leuprolide, goserelin): Medical castration; initial flare (first few weeks) – use with anti‑androgen to prevent flare.
  • GnRH antagonists (degarelix): No flare; immediate suppression.
  • Anti‑androgens (flutamide, bicalutamide): Block androgen receptor; used with GnRH agonists.
  • Abiraterone: Inhibits CYP17 (androgen synthesis).
  • Enzalutamide: Potent androgen receptor antagonist.
Ovarian/Uterine Cancers
  • Platinum agents (carboplatin, cisplatin) + taxanes (paclitaxel): First‑line for ovarian cancer.
  • Bevacizumab (anti‑VEGF): Angiogenesis inhibitor.
  • PARP inhibitors (olaparib, niraparib): For BRCA‑mutated ovarian cancer.
9. Drugs for Erectile Dysfunction
  • PDE‑5 inhibitors: Sildenafil, tadalafil, vardenafil, avanafil. Increase cGMP, enhancing NO‑mediated vasodilation in corpus cavernosum.
  • Onset: 30‑60 min; duration: sildenafil 4‑6h, tadalafil up to 36h.
  • Adverse effects: Headache, flushing, dyspepsia, nasal congestion, priapism (rare).
  • Contraindication: Concurrent nitrates (severe hypotension).
  • Alprostadil: Prostaglandin E₁; intracavernosal injection or urethral suppository; second‑line.

Clinical pearl: Tadalafil also indicated for benign prostatic hyperplasia (relaxes bladder neck).

10. Drugs for Menstrual Disorders
  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen): Inhibit prostaglandin synthesis; used for dysmenorrhea.
  • Tranexamic acid: Antifibrinolytic; reduces heavy menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia).
  • Hormonal therapies: COCs, progestins, IUD (levonorgestrel) for menorrhagia, dysmenorrhea, endometriosis.
  • GnRH agonists (leuprolide): Used for endometriosis (temporary, add‑back therapy to prevent bone loss).
  • Danazol: Synthetic androgen; used for endometriosis (now less common due to androgenic side effects).
