1. Principles of Topical Therapy

Topical dermatological preparations consist of a vehicle (base) and an active drug. The vehicle influences drug penetration and skin hydration.

  • Vehicles: Ointments (greasy, occlusive, best penetration), creams (emulsions, water washable), gels, lotions, solutions, foams.
  • Penetration: Stratum corneum is the main barrier. Penetration enhanced by hydration, occlusion, inflammation, and vehicle choice.
  • Potency: Topical corticosteroids are classified by vasoconstrictor assay (classes 1‑7, 1 = super‑high potency).

Clinical pearl: Ointments are more occlusive and penetrate better than creams; they are preferred for dry, lichenified lesions.

2. Topical Corticosteroids

Topical corticosteroids are anti‑inflammatory, immunosuppressive, and antiproliferative. They are the mainstay of treatment for eczema, psoriasis, and many inflammatory dermatoses.

Mechanism
  • Bind cytoplasmic glucocorticoid receptors, translocate to nucleus, modulate gene expression (↓ pro‑inflammatory cytokines, ↑ anti‑inflammatory proteins).
Potency Classes (USA)
  • Class 1 (Super‑high): Clobetasol propionate 0.05%, betamethasone dipropionate 0.05% in optimized vehicle.
  • Class 2‑3 (High): Mometasone furoate 0.1%, triamcinolone acetonide 0.1%.
  • Class 4‑5 (Medium): Betamethasone valerate 0.1%, fluocinolone acetonide 0.025%.
  • Class 6‑7 (Low): Hydrocortisone 1‑2.5%, desonide 0.05%.
Adverse Effects
  • Local: Skin atrophy, telangiectasia, striae, acneiform eruptions, perioral dermatitis, hypopigmentation, tinea incognito.
  • Systemic (with prolonged use on large areas): HPA axis suppression, Cushing syndrome, growth retardation in children.

Clinical pearl: Use lowest potency that controls disease; avoid high‑potency steroids on face, intertriginous areas, and in infants.

3. Retinoids

Retinoids are vitamin A derivatives that regulate cell proliferation, differentiation, and inflammation. Used in acne, psoriasis, photoaging, and skin cancer prevention.

Topical Retinoids
  • Tretinoin (all‑trans retinoic acid): Acne, photoaging; irritation common.
  • Adapalene: Third‑generation, less irritant; available OTC.
  • Tazarotene: For acne, psoriasis; more potent, more irritation; pregnancy category X.
Systemic Retinoids
  • Isotretinoin: For severe nodulocystic acne. Reduces sebum production, inhibits comedogenesis, anti‑inflammatory. Teratogenic (iPLEDGE program). Side effects: cheilitis, xerosis, dry eyes, hypertriglyceridemia, mood changes, pseudotumor cerebri.
  • Acitretin: For psoriasis. Also teratogenic; may cause hypertriglyceridemia, hepatotoxicity.
  • Bexarotene: For cutaneous T‑cell lymphoma.

Clinical pearl: Isotretinoin requires monthly monitoring of lipids and liver enzymes. Two forms of contraception required.

4. Topical Antibiotics & Antiseptics
  • Mupirocin: Inhibits bacterial isoleucyl‑tRNA synthetase. Active against Staph. aureus, Strep. pyogenes. Used for impetigo, MRSA decolonization. Minimal systemic absorption.
  • Retapamulin: Pleuromutilin antibiotic, active against MRSA and Strep. pyogenes; for impetigo.
  • Clindamycin: Lincosamide, used for acne (topical solution/gel). Can cause diarrhea; risk of C. difficile.
  • Erythromycin: Macrolide, used for acne; resistance common.
  • Metronidazole (topical): For rosacea (anti‑inflammatory, antiparasitic).
  • Benzoyl peroxide: Bactericidal (oxidizing agent) against C. acnes; keratolytic; no bacterial resistance. Often combined with clindamycin or adapalene.
  • Ivermectin (topical): For rosacea (anti‑inflammatory, anti‑Demodex).
  • Gentamicin, neomycin: Aminoglycosides; risk of allergic contact dermatitis.
5. Antiviral Agents in Dermatology
  • Acyclovir, valacyclovir, famciclovir: Inhibit viral DNA polymerase. Used for herpes simplex (orolabial, genital) and herpes zoster. Valacyclovir has higher oral bioavailability.
  • Penciclovir (topical): For herpes labialis.
  • Docosanol (topical): Inhibits viral entry; OTC for herpes labialis.
  • Imiquimod: Immune response modifier (TLR7 agonist). Induces local cytokine production (interferon‑α). Used for external genital warts, actinic keratosis, superficial BCC. Side effects: local inflammation, flu‑like symptoms.
6. Antifungal Agents
Topical Antifungals
  • Azoles: Clotrimazole, miconazole, econazole, ketoconazole, sertaconazole. Inhibit ergosterol synthesis. Broad spectrum (dermatophytes, Candida).
  • Allylamines: Terbinafine, naftifine. Inhibit squalene epoxidase, fungicidal. Excellent for dermatophytes.
  • Polyenes: Nystatin (for Candida only).
  • Ciclopirox: Broad spectrum, also anti‑inflammatory. Used for tinea versicolor, onychomycosis (nail lacquer).
  • Butenafine: Benzylamine, similar to allylamines.
Systemic Antifungals
  • Terbinafine (oral): For dermatophyte onychomycosis, tinea capitis. Hepatotoxicity (rare).
  • Griseofulvin: For dermatophyte infections (tinea capitis). Binds tubulin, disrupts mitosis. Fewer drug interactions, but less effective.
  • Itraconazole, fluconazole: For systemic and superficial mycoses. Drug interactions (CYP inhibitors).
7. Immunomodulators
Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors
  • Tacrolimus ointment, pimecrolimus cream: Inhibit T‑cell activation by blocking calcineurin → ↓ IL‑2. Used for atopic dermatitis (second‑line). No skin atrophy; safe for face and intertriginous areas. Black box warning for rare malignancy risk (but causal link not established).
Other Topical Immunomodulators
  • Crisaborole: PDE‑4 inhibitor; used for mild‑to‑moderate atopic dermatitis. Burning, stinging.
  • Ruxolitinib (topical): JAK inhibitor; for atopic dermatitis, vitiligo.
8. Systemic Therapies for Acne & Rosacea
  • Oral antibiotics: Tetracyclines (doxycycline, minocycline) – anti‑inflammatory and antibacterial. Minocycline can cause hyperpigmentation, lupus‑like syndrome, dizziness. Doxycycline: photosensitivity. Also used for rosacea (subantimicrobial doses).
  • Isotretinoin (covered earlier).
  • Spironolactone: Anti‑androgen; used off‑label for hormonal acne in women (blocks androgen receptors, reduces sebum). Side effects: menstrual irregularities, hyperkalemia.
  • Oral contraceptives: Estrogen/progestin combinations reduce androgen production; used for acne in women.
  • Ivermectin (oral): For rosacea (papulopustular) – anti‑Demodex effect.
9. Biologics in Dermatology

Biologics are used for moderate‑to‑severe psoriasis, atopic dermatitis, and hidradenitis suppurativa.

  • TNF‑α inhibitors: Adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab. For psoriasis, hidradenitis suppurativa. Risk of infections, demyelination, heart failure.
  • IL‑12/23 inhibitors: Ustekinumab (binds p40 subunit). For psoriasis.
  • IL‑17 inhibitors: Secukinumab, ixekizumab, brodalumab. Highly effective for psoriasis. Risk of candidiasis, exacerbation of IBD.
  • IL‑23 inhibitors: Guselkumab, tildrakizumab, risankizumab. For psoriasis.
  • IL‑4/13 inhibitors: Dupilumab (blocks IL‑4 receptor). For atopic dermatitis. Side effects: conjunctivitis, injection site reactions.
  • IL‑31 inhibitor: Nemolizumab (for prurigo nodularis).
10. Drugs for Skin Cancer & Phototherapy
Topical Chemotherapy
  • 5‑Fluorouracil (5‑FU): Thymidylate synthase inhibitor; used for actinic keratoses, superficial BCC. Causes intense inflammation.
  • Imiquimod (covered earlier).
  • Diclofenac (topical): For actinic keratoses (mechanism unclear).
  • Ingenol mebutate: Induces cell death and inflammation; for actinic keratoses. Withdrawn in some countries due to cancer risk concerns.
Phototherapy & Photochemotherapy
  • PUVA (psoralen + UVA): Psoralens (methoxsalen) intercalate DNA, then UVA activation. Used for psoriasis, vitiligo, CTCL. Side effects: nausea, phototoxicity, long‑term skin cancer risk.
  • Narrowband UVB (NB‑UVB): For psoriasis, vitiligo, atopic dermatitis.
  • Photodynamic therapy (PDT): Topical photosensitizer (aminolevulinic acid) + light; for actinic keratoses, superficial BCC, acne.
11. Antihistamines & Antipruritics
  • H1 antihistamines (oral): First‑generation (diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine) – sedating, anticholinergic. Second‑generation (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine) – less sedating.
  • Topical antihistamines: Not recommended due to sensitization risk.
  • Doxepin (topical): Tricyclic antidepressant with potent H1 and H2 antagonism; for atopic dermatitis and pruritus.
  • Capsaicin: Depletes substance P; for post‑herpetic neuralgia, pruritus.
  • Pramoxine: Topical anesthetic with antipruritic effect.
12. Miscellaneous Agents
  • Keratolytics: Salicylic acid, urea – soften and desquamate stratum corneum. Used for ichthyosis, psoriasis, warts.
  • Coal tar: For psoriasis, eczema. Antiproliferative, anti‑inflammatory. Unpleasant odor, stains.
  • Anthralin (dithranol): For psoriasis; irritant, stains.
  • Podophyllin / podophyllotoxin: Antimitotic; for genital warts.
  • Cantharidin: Blistering agent for molluscum contagiosum.
  • Bichloroacetic / trichloroacetic acid: For warts.
  • Botox (onabotulinumtoxinA): For hyperhidrosis (inhibits ACh release).
