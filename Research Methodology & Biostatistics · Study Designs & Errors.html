1. Random vs Systematic Error
Two main types of error in research:
  • Random error: Unpredictable variation due to chance. Decreases with larger sample size. Can be quantified using p‑values and confidence intervals.
  • Systematic error (bias): Consistent, directional error that is not reduced by increasing sample size. Threatens internal validity.
2. Bias (Systematic Error)
Selection Bias
  • Berkson bias: Hospital‑based controls may have different exposure rates than population.
  • Non‑response bias: Those who respond differ from non‑responders.
  • Attrition bias: Loss to follow‑up differs between groups.
  • Healthy worker effect: Workers healthier than general population.
Information Bias
  • Recall bias: Cases remember exposures differently than controls.
  • Interviewer bias: Interviewer probes differently based on knowledge.
  • Misclassification bias: Errors in categorizing exposure or outcome (non‑differential vs differential).
Other Biases
  • Lead‑time bias: Early detection falsely prolongs survival.
  • Length‑time bias: Screening more likely to detect slower‑growing tumors.
  • Publication bias: Positive results more likely to be published.
3. Confounding
A confounder is a variable that is associated with both the exposure and the outcome, and is not an intermediate in the causal pathway.
  • Example: Age confounds the relationship between gray hair and heart disease (age causes both).
  • Methods to control:
    • Randomization (in RCTs).
    • Restriction (limit study to specific levels of confounder).
    • Matching (in case‑control or cohort).
    • Stratification (analyze within strata).
    • Multivariable analysis (e.g., logistic regression).
4. Effect Modification (Interaction)
Effect modification occurs when the association between exposure and outcome differs across levels of a third variable.
  • Example: Smoking and asbestos have multiplicative effect on lung cancer risk (synergistic).
  • Distinction: Confounding is a bias to be removed; effect modification is a real finding to be reported.
5. Validity (Internal & External)
  • Internal validity: Degree to which study results are correct for the studied population (free from bias).
  • External validity (generalizability): Degree to which results can be applied to other populations.
6. Test Characteristics (Sensitivity, Specificity, PPV, NPV)

  • Sensitivity: TP/(TP+FN) – proportion of diseased correctly identified.
  • Specificity: TN/(TN+FP) – proportion of non‑diseased correctly identified.
  • Positive Predictive Value (PPV): TP/(TP+FP) – probability disease if test positive. Depends on prevalence.
  • Negative Predictive Value (NPV): TN/(TN+FN) – probability no disease if test negative.
  • High sensitivity → good for ruling out (SnOUT).
  • High specificity → good for ruling in (SpIN).
7. Likelihood Ratios
  • Positive likelihood ratio (LR+): sensitivity/(1‑specificity). Indicates how much a positive test increases odds of disease.
  • Negative likelihood ratio (LR‑): (1‑sensitivity)/specificity. Indicates how much a negative test decreases odds of disease.
  • LR >10 (or <0.1) generate large changes from pre‑ to post‑test probability.
  • Post‑test odds = pre‑test odds × LR.
8. ROC Curves & AUC
  • ROC (Receiver Operating Characteristic) curve plots sensitivity vs 1‑specificity for all possible cutoffs.
  • AUC (Area Under the Curve): Measure of overall test performance. 1 = perfect, 0.5 = worthless.
9. Agreement (Kappa)
  • Cohen's kappa measures agreement between two raters beyond chance.
  • κ = (observed agreement – expected agreement) / (1 – expected agreement).
  • Interpretation: 0‑0.2 = slight, 0.2‑0.4 = fair, 0.4‑0.6 = moderate, 0.6‑0.8 = substantial, 0.8‑1 = almost perfect.
10. Risk & Odds
  • Risk (incidence proportion): Number of new cases / population at risk.
  • Odds: Probability / (1 – probability).
11. Relative Risk & Odds Ratio
  • Relative Risk (RR): Risk exposed / Risk unexposed. Used in cohort studies.
  • Odds Ratio (OR): Odds exposed / Odds unexposed. Used in case‑control studies (and logistic regression).
  • If disease rare, OR ≈ RR.
12. Attributable Risk, NNT, NNH
  • Attributable Risk (AR): Risk exposed – Risk unexposed. Excess risk due to exposure.
  • Number Needed to Treat (NNT): 1 / AR (if treatment beneficial). Number needed to treat to prevent one adverse outcome.
  • Number Needed to Harm (NNH): 1 / AR (if harmful).
13. Systematic Review & Meta‑analysis
  • Systematic review: Comprehensive, reproducible summary of all evidence on a question.
  • Meta‑analysis: Statistical pooling of results from multiple studies to obtain a combined estimate.
  • Heterogeneity: Variability in study results; I² statistic measures inconsistency (0‑100%).
14. Forest Plot & Funnel Plot
  • Forest plot: Displays effect estimates and confidence intervals from individual studies and pooled result.
  • Funnel plot: Scatter plot of effect vs study size; asymmetry suggests publication bias.
15. Critical Appraisal Tools
  • RCTs: CONSORT checklist.
  • Observational studies: STROBE checklist.
  • Diagnostic studies: STARD checklist.
  • Systematic reviews: AMSTAR, PRISMA.
