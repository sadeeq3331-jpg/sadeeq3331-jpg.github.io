Cardiac Cycle
What Is the Cardiac Cycle?

The cardiac cycle refers to the sequence of mechanical and electrical events that occur from the beginning of one heartbeat to the next. It includes systole (contraction) and diastole (relaxation) of the atria and ventricles.

A complete cycle lasts about 0.8 seconds at a heart rate of 75 beats per minute.

Phases of the Cardiac Cycle
  1. Atrial systole: Atria contract, contributing the last 20–30% of ventricular filling. Produces the "a" wave in atrial pressure tracing.
  2. Isovolumetric ventricular contraction: Ventricles contract, AV valves close (S1). No change in volume because all valves are closed; pressure rises rapidly.
  3. Rapid ejection: When ventricular pressure exceeds aortic pressure, semilunar valves open. Blood is ejected rapidly into the aorta and pulmonary artery.
  4. Reduced ejection: Ventricular pressure begins to fall, ejection slows.
  5. Isovolumetric relaxation: Ventricles relax, pressure drops below aortic pressure; semilunar valves close (S2). All valves closed, no volume change.
  6. Rapid filling: AV valves open; blood that had accumulated in atria rushes into ventricles (may cause S3).
  7. Diastasis: Slow filling period.
  8. Last filling (atrial systole): Atria contract again, completing the cycle.

Pressure‑Volume Loop

The left ventricular pressure‑volume loop plots pressure against volume during one cardiac cycle.

  • A → B (filling): Ventricular filling (end‑diastolic volume, EDV).
  • B → C (isovolumetric contraction): Pressure rises, volume constant.
  • C → D (ejection): Volume decreases, pressure rises then falls (end‑systolic volume, ESV).
  • D → A (isovolumetric relaxation): Pressure falls, volume constant.

Stroke volume = EDV – ESV. Ejection fraction = SV/EDV.

Key Points
  • Atrial systole contributes ~20% of ventricular filling.
  • Isovolumetric phases occur when all valves are closed.
  • S1 = AV valve closure; S2 = semilunar valve closure.
  • EDV ~120 mL, ESV ~50 mL, SV ~70 mL.
Electrocardiography
What Is an ECG?

Electrocardiography records the electrical activity of the heart from electrodes placed on the body surface. The ECG provides information about heart rate, rhythm, conduction, and myocardial ischemia/infarction.

ECG Waves and Intervals
  • P wave: Atrial depolarization.
  • PR interval: Time from atrial depolarization to ventricular depolarization (includes AV nodal delay). Normal 0.12–0.20 s.
  • QRS complex: Ventricular depolarization. Normal duration <0.12 s.
  • ST segment: Early ventricular repolarization; normally isoelectric.
  • T wave: Ventricular repolarization.
  • QT interval: Total duration of ventricular depolarization and repolarization (corrected for heart rate).
Normal ECG tracing with labeled waves and intervals
ECG Paper

Standard ECG paper speed is 25 mm/s. Each small square (1 mm) = 0.04 s, each large square (5 mm) = 0.2 s. Voltage calibration: 10 mm = 1 mV.

Clinical Correlations

ST elevation: Acute myocardial infarction, pericarditis.

ST depression: Ischemia, digoxin effect, hypokalemia.

T wave inversion: Ischemia, ventricular hypertrophy, electrolyte disturbances.

Electrophysiology of Heart
Cardiac Action Potentials

Two main types: fast response (atrial and ventricular myocytes, Purkinje fibers) and slow response (SA and AV nodes).

Fast Response Action Potential
  • Phase 0: Rapid depolarization – opening of fast Na⁺ channels.
  • Phase 1: Early repolarization – transient K⁺ efflux.
  • Phase 2: Plateau – Ca²⁺ influx through L‑type channels balances K⁺ efflux.
  • Phase 3: Repolarization – Ca²⁺ channels close, K⁺ efflux dominates.
  • Phase 4: Resting membrane potential (stable).
Slow Response Action Potential (SA/AV Node)
  • Phase 0: Depolarization via L‑type Ca²⁺ channels (slower).
  • Phase 3: Repolarization by K⁺ efflux.
  • Phase 4: Spontaneous diastolic depolarization due to "funny current" (If) and T‑type Ca²⁺ channels.
Autonomic Effects

Sympathetic stimulation (norepinephrine, β1 receptors) → ↑ cAMP → ↑ Ca²⁺ influx → increased heart rate (positive chronotropy), increased conduction velocity (positive dromotropy), increased contractility (positive inotropy).

Parasympathetic stimulation (acetylcholine, M2 receptors) → ↑ K⁺ conductance, ↓ cAMP → hyperpolarization, ↓ slope of phase 4 → decreased heart rate, decreased AV nodal conduction.

Bipolar Limb Leads
Einthoven's Leads

Bipolar limb leads record voltage between two electrodes placed on the limbs:

  • Lead I: Left arm (+) – Right arm (–)
  • Lead II: Left leg (+) – Right arm (–)
  • Lead III: Left leg (+) – Left arm (–)

These leads form Einthoven's triangle. Lead II usually shows the largest QRS because its axis aligns with the main cardiac vector.

Einthoven's law: Lead II = Lead I + Lead III (voltage differences).

Augmented Unipolar Limb Leads
Goldberger's Leads

Augmented leads use a combination of limb electrodes to create a reference point. They are unipolar (positive electrode on one limb, negative is a combination of the other two).

  • aVR: Right arm positive
  • aVL: Left arm positive
  • aVF: Left leg positive

These leads view the heart from different angles in the frontal plane.

Chest Leads (Precordial Leads)
Placement
  • V1: 4th intercostal space, right sternal border
  • V2: 4th intercostal space, left sternal border
  • V3: Midway between V2 and V4
  • V4: 5th intercostal space, midclavicular line
  • V5: 5th intercostal space, anterior axillary line
  • V6: 5th intercostal space, midaxillary line

These leads record electrical activity in the horizontal plane. V1 and V2 face the right ventricle and septum, V3 and V4 the anterior wall, V5 and V6 the lateral wall.

QRS Complex Nomenclature
Naming Deflections

If the first deflection is negative, it is called a Q wave. The first positive deflection is an R wave. A negative deflection after an R is an S wave. Subsequent positive or negative deflections are labeled R' or S'. Lowercase letters (q, r, s) indicate small deflections.

QRS Complex in Chest Leads
Normal Patterns

V1: rS pattern (small R, deep S) because the main ventricular vector moves away from V1.

V6: qR pattern (small q, tall R) because the vector moves toward V6.

The transition zone (where R and S are equal) is normally at V3–V4.

Abnormalities of R Wave Progression
Poor R Wave Progression

R wave amplitude normally increases from V1 to V5. Poor progression (R < S in V3–V4) may indicate anterior myocardial infarction, left ventricular hypertrophy, or cardiomyopathy.

Reversed R Wave Progression

R waves become smaller from V1 to V6; suggests right ventricular hypertrophy, dextrocardia, or misplacement of leads.

First Heart Sound (S1)
Origin

S1 is produced by closure of the mitral (M1) and tricuspid (T1) valves at the onset of ventricular systole. It is best heard at the apex (mitral area) and left lower sternal border (tricuspid area).

Factors Affecting Intensity
  • Loud S1: Short PR interval (e.g., WPW), tachycardia (increased flow), mitral stenosis (mobile leaflets), hyperdynamic states (fever, anemia).
  • Soft S1: Long PR interval (first‑degree heart block), mitral regurgitation, aortic regurgitation, calcified immobile mitral valve, heart failure.
  • Variable S1: Atrial fibrillation, complete heart block, ventricular tachycardia.
Second Heart Sound (S2)
Origin

S2 results from closure of the aortic (A2) and pulmonary (P2) valves at the end of systole. A2 normally precedes P2.

Splitting of S2
  • Physiologic splitting: During inspiration, venous return to the right heart increases, delaying P2. Heard only in pulmonary area.
  • Wide splitting (fixed or variable): Causes: RBBB, pulmonary stenosis, MR (early A2), VSD (early A2). Fixed splitting is classic for ASD.
  • Paradoxical splitting: A2 is delayed, so P2 precedes A2; splitting occurs in expiration. Causes: aortic stenosis, LBBB, HCM.
Third & Fourth Heart Sounds
S3 (Ventricular Gallop)

S3 is a low‑pitched sound heard just after S2 during rapid ventricular filling. It is normal in children and young adults, but in older adults it indicates ventricular failure (dilated ventricle) or volume overload (MR, TR).

S4 (Atrial Gallop)

S4 occurs just before S1, caused by atrial contraction against a stiff ventricle (due to hypertrophy, ischemia, or restrictive cardiomyopathy). It is always pathological.

Arrhythmias
Mechanisms
  • Enhanced automaticity: Abnormal pacemaker activity (e.g., ischemia, catecholamines).
  • Triggered activity: Early or delayed afterdepolarizations (e.g., digitalis toxicity, long QT).
  • Reentry: Circus movement due to unidirectional block and slow conduction (e.g., AVNRT, WPW, atrial flutter).

Myocardial Contractility
Definition

Contractility is the intrinsic ability of cardiac muscle to generate force at a given muscle length (preload). It is influenced by sympathetic tone, circulating catecholamines, drugs, and disease.

Cellular Mechanism

Increased intracellular Ca²⁺ during systole enhances cross‑bridge formation. Positive inotropic agents (sympathetic, digitalis) increase Ca²⁺ availability. Negative inotropic agents (β‑blockers, heart failure) decrease it.

Indices of Contractility
  • Ejection fraction (EF = SV/EDV)
  • dP/dt max (rate of pressure rise in ventricle)
  • Ventricular function curves (Frank‑Starling relationship shifted up or down)
Cardiac Output
Definition

Cardiac output (CO) = heart rate (HR) × stroke volume (SV). Normal: 4–8 L/min.

Determinants of Stroke Volume
  • Preload: End‑diastolic volume (Frank‑Starling law).
  • Afterload: Aortic pressure (inverse relation).
  • Contractility: Intrinsic inotropic state.
Fick Principle

CO = O₂ consumption / (arterial O₂ content – venous O₂ content).

Heart Sounds & Murmurs
Classification of Murmurs
  • Systolic murmurs: Mid‑systolic (ejection) – aortic stenosis, pulmonary stenosis; holosystolic – MR, TR, VSD; late systolic – MVP.
  • Diastolic murmurs: Early diastolic – AR, PR; mid‑diastolic – MS, TS; late diastolic (presystolic) – MS.
  • Continuous murmurs: PDA, AV fistula, venous hum.
Maneuvers

Valsalva: Decreases venous return → most murmurs decrease, but HCM murmur increases.

Squatting: Increases venous return and afterload → most murmurs increase, but HCM murmur decreases.

Inspiration: Increases right‑sided murmurs (Carvallo's sign).

Systolic Clicks
Ejection Clicks (Early Systolic)

Produced by opening of abnormal semilunar valves (bicuspid aortic valve, pulmonary stenosis). They occur just after S1 and are best heard at the base.

Non‑ejection Clicks (Mid‑systolic)

Classic of mitral valve prolapse. Caused by sudden tensing of chordae as the leaflet prolapses. May be followed by a late systolic murmur.

Diastolic Sounds
Opening Snap

Heard in mitral stenosis (pliable valve). Occurs shortly after S2 due to abrupt halt of opening mitral valve. The shorter the S2‑OS interval, the more severe the stenosis.

Tumor Plop

Rare sound in atrial myxoma when the tumor strikes the valve or chamber wall.

Pericardial Knock

Early diastolic sound in constrictive pericarditis due to sudden cessation of ventricular filling.

Diastolic Murmurs
Early Diastolic

Aortic regurgitation: Decrescendo, high‑pitched, best heard with diaphragm at left sternal border, patient leaning forward.

Graham Steell murmur: Pulmonary regurgitation due to pulmonary hypertension.

Mid‑Diastolic

Mitral stenosis: Low‑pitched rumbling, best heard at apex with bell, left lateral position. May have presystolic accentuation.

Tricuspid stenosis: Similar at lower left sternal border, increases with inspiration.

Austin Flint murmur: Mid‑diastolic rumble in severe aortic regurgitation due to functional mitral stenosis (anterior leaflet fluttering).

Carey Coombs murmur: Mid‑diastolic murmur in acute rheumatic fever due to mitral valvulitis.

Wolff‑Parkinson‑White Syndrome
What Is WPW?

WPW is a pre‑excitation syndrome caused by an accessory pathway (Bundle of Kent) connecting atria and ventricles, bypassing the AV node.

ECG Features
  • Short PR interval (<0.12 s).
  • Delta wave (slurred upstroke of QRS).
  • Wide QRS (>0.10 s).
  • Secondary ST‑T changes.
Types
  • Type A: Positive delta wave in V1 (left‑sided pathway).
  • Type B: Negative delta wave in V1 (right‑sided pathway).
Tachyarrhythmias

Orthodromic AVRT: Impulse goes down AV node, up accessory pathway → narrow QRS tachycardia.

Antidromic AVRT: Impulse goes down accessory pathway, up AV node → wide QRS tachycardia.

Atrial fibrillation with rapid conduction via accessory pathway can degenerate to ventricular fibrillation.

Treatment: Avoid AV nodal blockers (adenosine, verapamil) in atrial fibrillation with WPW – they may enhance conduction via the accessory pathway. Use procainamide or cardioversion.

Jugular Venous Pressure
Normal Waveform
  • a wave: Atrial contraction (right atrium).
  • c wave: Tricuspid valve bulging into atrium during isovolumetric contraction.
  • x descent: Atrial relaxation and downward displacement of tricuspid valve.
  • v wave: Passive filling of atrium during systole (venous return).
  • y descent: Rapid atrial emptying after AV valve opening.
Abnormalities
  • Large a wave: Tricuspid stenosis, pulmonary hypertension, right ventricular hypertrophy.
  • Cannon a wave: Atria contract against closed tricuspid valve (complete heart block, ventricular tachycardia).
  • Absent a wave: Atrial fibrillation.
  • Prominent v wave: Tricuspid regurgitation (fused cv wave, loss of x descent).
  • Steep y descent: Constrictive pericarditis, severe tricuspid regurgitation.
  • Kussmaul sign: Paradoxical rise in JVP during inspiration (constrictive pericarditis, right ventricular failure).
