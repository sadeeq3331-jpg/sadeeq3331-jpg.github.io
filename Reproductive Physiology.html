1. Introduction to Reproductive Physiology

The reproductive system ensures continuation of the species through production of gametes, fertilization, and nurturing of offspring. It is regulated by the hypothalamic‑pituitary‑gonadal (HPG) axis.

Key Hormones
  • GnRH (Gonadotropin‑Releasing Hormone): Secreted by hypothalamus in pulses; stimulates anterior pituitary to release LH and FSH.
  • FSH (Follicle‑Stimulating Hormone): Promotes gametogenesis (spermatogenesis in males, follicular development in females).
  • LH (Luteinizing Hormone): Triggers ovulation and stimulates sex hormone production (testosterone in males, estrogen/progesterone in females).
  • Sex steroids: Testosterone, estrogen, progesterone – mediate development of secondary sexual characteristics and reproductive function.
  • Inhibin: Inhibits FSH secretion via negative feedback.

Clinical pearl: Kallmann syndrome (GnRH deficiency) causes hypogonadotropic hypogonadism and anosmia.

2. Male Reproductive System
Testicular Structure
  • Seminiferous tubules: Site of spermatogenesis. Contain Sertoli cells (support) and germ cells.
  • Leydig cells (interstitial cells): Produce testosterone under LH stimulation.
Spermatogenesis
  • Takes ~64‑72 days. Spermatogonia (2n) → primary spermatocytes → secondary spermatocytes → spermatids → spermatozoa.
  • FSH acts on Sertoli cells to support spermatogenesis.
Testosterone Synthesis & Actions
  • Cholesterol → pregnenolone → ... → testosterone (in Leydig cells).
  • Testosterone converted to DHT (5α‑reductase) for external genitalia development and prostate; aromatized to estradiol in some tissues.
  • Actions: male sexual differentiation, spermatogenesis, secondary sexual characteristics, anabolic effects.
Feedback Regulation
  • Testosterone inhibits GnRH and LH.
  • Inhibin from Sertoli cells inhibits FSH.

Clinical pearl: 5α‑reductase deficiency causes ambiguous genitalia; testosterone normal, DHT low.

3. Female Reproductive System
Ovarian Structure
  • Follicles: Primordial → primary → secondary (antral) → Graafian (preovulatory).
  • Theca cells: Under LH, produce androstenedione (precursor to estrogen).
  • Granulosa cells: Under FSH, aromatize androstenedione to estrogen; also produce inhibin.
Oogenesis
  • Oogonia (2n) → primary oocytes (arrest in prophase I) → at ovulation, meiosis I completes → secondary oocyte (arrest in metaphase II) → fertilized completes meiosis II.

Clinical pearl: Primary oocytes are arrested from fetal life until ovulation; atresia occurs continuously.

4. Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle averages 28 days and consists of ovarian and uterine cycles.

Ovarian Cycle
  • Follicular phase (days 1‑14): FSH stimulates follicular growth; estrogen rises.
  • Ovulation (day 14): Estrogen surge triggers LH surge → rupture of follicle.
  • Luteal phase (days 15‑28): Corpus luteum secretes progesterone and estrogen.
Uterine (Menstrual) Cycle
  • Menstrual phase (days 1‑5): Shedding of endometrium due to fall of estrogen/progesterone.
  • Proliferative phase (days 6‑14): Estrogen stimulates endometrial proliferation.
  • Secretory phase (days 15‑28): Progesterone induces secretory changes, preparing for implantation.

Clinical pearl: The LH surge precedes ovulation by 36 hours; used in ovulation prediction kits.

5. Gametogenesis

Gametogenesis is the process of forming haploid gametes via meiosis.

  • Spermatogenesis: Continuous after puberty; millions of sperm produced daily.
  • Oogenesis: Begins in fetal life, arrests, completes after fertilization.
  • Meiosis I: reduces chromosome number (2n → n).
  • Meiosis II: separates sister chromatids.
6. Fertilization & Implantation

Fertilization occurs in the ampulla of the fallopian tube. Sperm capacitation and acrosome reaction are required.

  • Capacitation: Changes in sperm membrane (removal of cholesterol, proteins).
  • Acrosome reaction: Release of enzymes to penetrate zona pellucida.
  • Implantation: Blastocyst implants in endometrium ~6‑7 days after fertilization.

Clinical pearl: hCG (from syncytiotrophoblast) maintains corpus luteum in early pregnancy; basis of pregnancy tests.

7. Pregnancy & Placental Hormones

The placenta produces several hormones essential for maintaining pregnancy.

  • hCG (human Chorionic Gonadotropin): Maintains corpus luteum; peaks at 8‑10 weeks.
  • hPL (human Placental Lactogen): Modifies maternal metabolism (anti‑insulin, increases lipolysis).
  • Estrogen (estriol): From placental aromatization of fetal DHEA‑S; promotes uterine growth.
  • Progesterone: Maintains endometrium, suppresses uterine contractions.

Clinical pearl: Low estriol may indicate fetal distress (e.g., in preeclampsia).

8. Parturition & Lactation
Parturition
  • Initiated by fetal cortisol surge → increased estrogen/progesterone ratio → prostaglandin synthesis → uterine contractions.
  • Oxytocin from posterior pituitary stimulates contractions (positive feedback).
Lactation
  • Prolactin: Stimulates milk synthesis (high during pregnancy but inhibited by estrogen).
  • Oxytocin: Causes milk ejection (let‑down reflex).

Clinical pearl: Suckling stimulates prolactin and oxytocin release.

9. Puberty & Menopause

Puberty is initiated by increased GnRH pulsatility, leading to gonadarche and adrenarche.

  • Gonadarche: Activation of HPG axis → sex steroid production.
  • Adrenarche: Increased adrenal androgens (DHEA) before gonadarche.
  • Menopause: Ovarian follicle depletion → loss of estrogen, high FSH/LH.

Clinical pearl: Delayed puberty may be due to hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (Kallmann) or hypergonadotropic hypogonadism (Turner).

10. Reproductive Aging

Aging affects reproductive function in both sexes.

  • Male: Gradual decline in testosterone (andropause), reduced fertility, increased SHBG.
  • Female: Menopause (average age 51); loss of fertility, increased risk of osteoporosis, cardiovascular disease.

Clinical pearl: Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) for menopausal symptoms has risks (thrombosis, breast cancer) and benefits.
