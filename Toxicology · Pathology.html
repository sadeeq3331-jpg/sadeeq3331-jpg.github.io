1. Introduction to Toxicologic Pathology
Toxicologic pathology is the study of structural and functional changes in cells, tissues, and organs caused by toxic agents. It integrates principles of pathology and toxicology to understand mechanisms of injury and disease.
Key aims: identify target organs, characterize lesions, establish dose‑response relationships, and extrapolate findings to human risk.

Basic Definitions

  • Lesion: A pathological change in tissue.
  • Degeneration: Reversible cell injury.
  • Necrosis: Irreversible cell death.
  • Apoptosis: Programmed cell death.
  • Adaptive responses: Atrophy, hypertrophy, hyperplasia, metaplasia.
2. Mechanisms of Cell Injury
Toxicants can cause cell injury through several fundamental mechanisms:
  • ATP depletion: Hypoxia, mitochondrial poisons (cyanide) → reduced ATP → Na+/K+ pump failure → cell swelling.
  • Mitochondrial damage: Opening of mitochondrial permeability transition pore → loss of membrane potential, release of pro‑apoptotic factors.
  • Calcium overload: Sustained increase in cytosolic Ca²⁺ activates phospholipases, proteases, endonucleases → membrane damage, cytoskeletal disruption.
  • Oxidative stress: Generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) → lipid peroxidation, protein oxidation, DNA damage.
  • Membrane damage: Direct attack (phospholipases, detergents) → loss of integrity.
  • DNA damage: Alkylation, strand breaks → activation of p53, apoptosis or carcinogenesis.

Example: Acetaminophen overdose depletes glutathione, allowing NAPQI to bind mitochondrial proteins, causing oxidative stress and necrosis.

Morphologic Types of Necrosis

  • Coagulative necrosis: Most common; architecture preserved (e.g., ischemia, many toxins).
  • Liquefactive necrosis: Enzymatic digestion (e.g., brain).
  • Caseous necrosis: TB, fungal; granular, soft.
  • Fat necrosis: Enzymatic breakdown of adipose tissue (pancreatic lipase).
  • Fibrinoid necrosis: Immune complex deposition in vessel walls.
4. Liver Pathology
The liver is the primary site of xenobiotic metabolism, making it a common target for toxicity.
Hepatocellular Necrosis
  • Zonal necrosis: Centrilobular (zone 3) – acetaminophen, CCl₄, chloroform; due to high CYP activity.
  • Midzonal (zone 2): Some plant toxins.
  • Periportal (zone 1): Allyl alcohol, phosphorus.
  • Massive necrosis: Fulminant hepatitis (e.g., high‑dose acetaminophen).
Steatosis (Fatty Liver)
  • Microvesicular: Ethanol, valproate, tetracycline – mitochondria impaired; small fat droplets.
  • Macrovesicular: CCl₄, ethanol – large droplet displaces nucleus.
Cholestasis
  • Bile plugs in canaliculi, hepatocyte feathery degeneration. Causes: chlorpromazine, estrogens, anabolic steroids.
Cirrhosis
  • Chronic injury leads to fibrosis, regenerative nodules. Ethanol, methotrexate, arsenic.
Vascular Lesions
  • Veno‑occlusive disease (pyrrolizidine alkaloids) → sinusoidal obstruction, hepatomegaly, ascites.
  • Peliosis hepatis: blood‑filled cavities (anabolic steroids).

Acetaminophen toxicity: Centrilobular necrosis (zone 3). Early stage: coagulative necrosis; later: inflammation, collapse. Massive dose can cause bridging necrosis.

5. Kidney Pathology
  • Acute tubular necrosis (ATN): Proximal tubule damage – aminoglycosides, cisplatin, mercury. Histology: loss of brush border, flattened epithelium, casts.
  • Osmotic nephrosis: Swelling of proximal tubule cells (mannitol, IV contrast).
  • Interstitial nephritis: Inflammatory infiltrate (NSAIDs, penicillins).
  • Papillary necrosis: Coagulative necrosis of renal papillae (NSAIDs, phenacetin).
  • Glomerular injury: Membranous GN (gold, penicillamine); minimal change (NSAIDs).

Heavy Metal Nephropathy

  • Lead: Proximal tubule dysfunction, intranuclear inclusions.
  • Cadmium: Chronic tubular injury, interstitial fibrosis.
  • Mercury: Acute proximal tubule necrosis (inorganic); immune‑complex GN (organic).
6. Lung Pathology
  • Pulmonary edema: Irritant gases (chlorine, phosgene) → increased permeability.
  • ARDS: Diffuse alveolar damage (paraquat, bleomycin, oxygen toxicity).
  • Pulmonary fibrosis: Interstitial fibrosis (asbestos, silica, bleomycin).
  • Bronchiolitis obliterans: Constrictive bronchiolitis (nitrogen dioxide, diacetyl).
  • Granulomatous inflammation: Berylliosis (non‑caseating granulomas).
  • Lung cancer: Asbestos (with smoking), arsenic, nickel, chromium.
7. Nervous System Pathology
  • Neuronopathy: Direct neuronal injury – mercury (granule cell loss in cerebellum), methylmercury, lead (developmental).
  • Axonopathy: Distal axon degeneration (acrylamide, organophosphates, vincristine).
  • Myelinopathy: Segmental demyelination (hexachlorophene, diphtheria toxin).
  • Neurotransmitter depletion: MPTP → substantia nigra neuron loss (Parkinsonism).
  • Edema: Lead encephalopathy (vasogenic edema), triethyltin (intramyelinic edema).
8. Cardiovascular Pathology
  • Myocyte necrosis: Catecholamines (contraction band necrosis), cocaine (ischemia).
  • Cardiomyopathy: Doxorubicin (vacuolization, myofibrillar loss), ethanol (dilated cardiomyopathy), cobalt.
  • Endocardial fibrosis: Serotonin (carcinoid), methysergide.
  • Vasculitis: Polyarteritis nodosa (drug‑induced: amphetamines, minocycline).
9. Skin Pathology
  • Contact dermatitis: Irritant (direct cytotoxicity) vs allergic (type IV hypersensitivity – poison ivy).
  • Photosensitivity: Phototoxic vs photoallergic – tetracycline, amiodarone.
  • Pigmentation: Arsenic (hyperpigmentation, rain‑drop depigmentation); silver (argyria).
  • Mucocutaneous ulceration: Arsenic, methotrexate (stomatitis).
10. Bone Marrow & Blood
  • Bone marrow suppression: Benzene, alkylating agents, chloramphenicol (aplastic anemia).
  • Leukemia: Benzene, alkylating agents (secondary AML).
  • Methemoglobinemia: Nitrites, dapsone – brown blood.
  • Hemolysis: Oxidant drugs in G6PD deficiency (Heinz bodies, bite cells).
  • Thrombocytopenia: Heparin (HIT), quinidine, gold.
11. Acetaminophen (Paracetamol)
Mechanism: CYP2E1 metabolizes to N‑acetyl‑p‑benzoquinone imine (NAPQI). Normally conjugated by glutathione. Overdose depletes glutathione → NAPQI binds mitochondrial proteins → oxidative stress, necrosis.
Pathology: Centrilobular (zone 3) coagulative necrosis within hours; later inflammatory infiltrate. Massive dose causes bridging necrosis. Can progress to fulminant hepatic failure.
12. Carbon Tetrachloride (CCl₄)
  • CYP2E1 converts to trichloromethyl radical (•CCl₃) → lipid peroxidation, membrane damage.
  • Centrilobular necrosis and steatosis (macrovesicular).
  • Chronic exposure can cause cirrhosis.
13. Ethanol
  • Hepatic: Steatosis (macrovesicular), alcoholic hepatitis (ballooning, Mallory‑Denk bodies, neutrophils), cirrhosis.
  • Cardiac: Dilated cardiomyopathy.
  • Neurologic: Wernicke encephalopathy (thiamine deficiency – petechial hemorrhages in mamillary bodies), Korsakoff syndrome.
  • GI: Gastritis, pancreatitis.
14. Heavy Metals
Lead
  • Basophilic stippling of RBCs, intranuclear inclusions in proximal tubule cells (lead‑protein complex).
  • Nervous system: encephalopathy (edema, petechiae), peripheral neuropathy (segmental demyelination).
Mercury
  • Inorganic: acute proximal tubule necrosis.
  • Organic (methylmercury): neuronal necrosis in cerebral cortex, cerebellum (granule cells).
Arsenic
  • Hyperkeratosis, hyperpigmentation, Mee's lines (nails).
  • Peripheral neuropathy, squamous cell carcinoma.
15. Organophosphates
  • Irreversible acetylcholinesterase inhibition → cholinergic excess.
  • Pathology: No specific histologic lesion; acute death from respiratory failure.
  • Delayed neuropathy: organophosphate‑induced delayed polyneuropathy (OPIDP) due to inhibition of neuropathy target esterase (NTE) → axonal degeneration.
16. Paraquat
  • Selectively accumulates in lung via polyamine uptake.
  • Redox cycling generates ROS → diffuse alveolar damage (DAD) progressing to pulmonary fibrosis.
  • Histology: early edema, hyaline membranes; later fibroblast proliferation, collagen deposition.
17. Methanol & Ethylene Glycol
Methanol: Metabolized to formaldehyde → formic acid. Pathology: retinal and optic nerve necrosis, cerebral edema, hemorrhages in putamen.
Ethylene glycol: Metabolized to oxalic acid → calcium oxalate crystals deposited in renal tubules (acute tubular necrosis) and brain (cerebral edema, hemorrhages). Crystals are birefringent under polarized light.
