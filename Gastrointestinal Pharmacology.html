1. Antacids
What Are Antacids?

Antacids are weak bases that neutralize gastric acid, providing rapid symptomatic relief in dyspepsia and gastroesophageal reflux. They do not heal erosive esophagitis and are not first‑line for chronic therapy.

2. H₂ Receptor Antagonists

H₂ blockers competitively inhibit histamine at H₂ receptors on parietal cells, reducing basal and stimulated acid secretion.

Examples
  • Cimetidine: First generation, inhibits CYP450, antiandrogenic effects (gynecomastia).
  • Ranitidine: More potent, fewer drug interactions.
  • Famotidine: Potent, long duration, minimal side effects.
  • Nizatidine: Similar to ranitidine.

Uses: GERD, peptic ulcer, Zollinger‑Ellison (adjunct), stress ulcer prophylaxis. Tolerance may develop with prolonged use.

3. Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)

PPIs irreversibly inhibit H⁺/K⁺ ATPase on parietal cells, the final step of acid secretion. They are prodrugs activated in acidic environment of secretory canaliculus.

Examples
  • Omeprazole, esomeprazole, lansoprazole, pantoprazole, rabeprazole.
Pharmacokinetics

Best taken 30‑60 minutes before meals (max effect when proton pumps are active). Long duration (up to 24h), but full acid suppression takes 3‑4 days.

Side Effects
  • Generally well tolerated. Long‑term use associated with: increased risk of C. difficile infection, pneumonia, osteoporosis/fractures, hypomagnesemia, vitamin B12 deficiency.

Uses: GERD, erosive esophagitis, peptic ulcer (including NSAID‑associated), Zollinger‑Ellison, H. pylori eradication (with antibiotics).

4. Cytoprotective Agents
Sucralfate

Forms a viscous gel in acid environment, binding to ulcer base and protecting against pepsin, bile, and acid. Not absorbed. Given on empty stomach (1h before meals). Constipation is main side effect.

Misoprostol

Synthetic prostaglandin E₁ analogue. Stimulates mucus and bicarbonate secretion, increases mucosal blood flow. Used to prevent NSAID‑induced ulcers. Contraindicated in pregnancy (abortifacient). Side effects: diarrhea, abdominal cramps.

5. Helicobacter pylori Eradication

Standard triple therapy (PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin or metronidazole) for 14 days. Quadruple therapy (PPI + bismuth + metronidazole + tetracycline) used for resistant cases or penicillin allergy.

Levofloxacin‑based regimens are second‑line options. Eradication improves ulcer healing and reduces gastric cancer risk.

7. Prokinetic Agents
  • Metoclopramide: Dopamine antagonist + 5‑HT₄ agonist; increases LES tone, gastric emptying. Used in gastroparesis, GERD. Side effects: EPS, tardive dyskinesia (black box warning).
  • Domperidone: Peripheral D₂ antagonist; does not cross BBB, so no CNS side effects. Prokinetic and antiemetic.
  • Macrolides (erythromycin): Motilin receptor agonist, stimulates gastric emptying (acute use only; tachyphylaxis develops).

9. Antidiarrheals
  • Loperamide: Peripheral µ‑opioid agonist; inhibits peristalsis, increases anal sphincter tone. Does not cross BBB → no CNS effects.
  • Diphenoxylate/atropine: Diphenoxylate is an opioid; atropine added to discourage abuse. May cause CNS effects.
  • Bismuth subsalicylate: Antimicrobial, antisecretory; used for traveler's diarrhea. May cause dark stools, tinnitus.
  • Octreotide: Somatostatin analogue; reduces secretion in carcinoid, VIPoma, AIDS‑related diarrhea.
10. Drugs for Inflammatory Bowel Disease
5‑Aminosalicylates (5‑ASA)
  • Sulfasalazine: Azo bond cleaved by colonic bacteria to release 5‑ASA (active) and sulfapyridine (carrier). Used in UC and Crohn’s colitis.
  • Mesalamine (5‑ASA): Various formulations (Asacol, Pentasa, Lialda) that deliver drug to different intestinal segments.
  • Balsalazide, olsalazine: Prodrugs of 5‑ASA.
Corticosteroids
  • Prednisone, budesonide (entocort): Budesonide has high first‑pass metabolism, fewer systemic effects; used for mild‑moderate Crohn’s.
Immunomodulators
  • Azathioprine, 6‑mercaptopurine: Inhibit purine synthesis; used for steroid‑sparing, maintenance. Slow onset (weeks).
  • Methotrexate: Used in Crohn’s.
Biologics
  • Anti‑TNF: Infliximab, adalimumab, certolizumab. Neutralize TNF‑α, induce and maintain remission.
  • Anti‑integrin: Vedolizumab (gut‑selective).
  • Anti‑IL12/23: Ustekinumab.
  • JAK inhibitors: Tofacitinib (for UC).
11. IBS & Antispasmodics
  • Anticholinergics/antispasmodics: Dicyclomine, hyoscyamine – relieve abdominal cramping by reducing smooth muscle spasm.
  • Peppermint oil: Natural antispasmodic.
  • Linaclotide, lubiprostone: Guanylate cyclase‑C agonist (linaclotide) and chloride channel activator (lubiprostone) – increase fluid secretion, used for IBS‑C.
  • Alosetron (5‑HT₃ antagonist): For severe IBS‑D in women (restricted).
  • Eluxadoline: Mixed µ‑opioid agonist, δ‑antagonist – for IBS‑D.
12. Gallstone Dissolution

Ursodeoxycholic acid (ursodiol): Reduces cholesterol secretion into bile and promotes formation of cholesterol‑poor micelles. Used for dissolution of small, radiolucent cholesterol gallstones in patients with functioning gallbladder. Takes months to years; high recurrence rate. Also used in primary biliary cholangitis.

13. Pancreatic Enzyme Replacement

Pancrelipase (Creon, Zenpep): Contains lipase, protease, amylase. Used in pancreatic insufficiency (cystic fibrosis, chronic pancreatitis, post‑pancreatectomy). Taken with meals. High doses may cause fibrosing colonopathy.
