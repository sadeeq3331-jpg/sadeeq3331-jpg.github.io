1. Introduction & Functions of the Kidney

The kidneys are paired retroperitoneal organs that maintain homeostasis by filtering blood, reabsorbing essential substances, and excreting waste. They also have endocrine functions.

Major Functions
  • Regulation of water and electrolyte balance: Adjust urine concentration and ion excretion to match intake.
  • Excretion of metabolic wastes: Urea (from protein metabolism), creatinine (from muscle), uric acid, and other toxins.
  • Acid‑base balance: Excrete H⁺ and reabsorb HCO₃⁻.
  • Blood pressure regulation: Via renin‑angiotensin‑aldosterone system (RAAS) and extracellular fluid volume control.
  • Endocrine functions: Produce erythropoietin (stimulates RBC production), activate vitamin D (calcitriol), and secrete renin.

Clinical pearl: Chronic kidney disease leads to anemia (↓ erythropoietin), hyperphosphatemia (↓ calcitriol), and metabolic acidosis.

2. Renal Anatomy

Each kidney contains about 1 million nephrons – the functional units. Nephrons consist of a renal corpuscle (glomerulus + Bowman's capsule) and a tubular system.

Nephron Segments
  • Glomerulus: Capillary tuft where ultrafiltration occurs.
  • Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT): Major site of reabsorption (Na⁺, Cl⁻, water, glucose, amino acids, bicarbonate).
  • Loop of Henle: Descending limb (water permeable), thin ascending limb, thick ascending limb (TAL) – active NaCl reabsorption, diluting segment.
  • Distal convoluted tubule (DCT): Fine tuning of Na⁺, Ca²⁺ reabsorption.
  • Collecting duct (CD): Principal cells (Na⁺ reabsorption, K⁺ secretion) and intercalated cells (acid‑base transport).

Clinical pearl: Diuretics act on specific segments: loop diuretics on TAL, thiazides on DCT, potassium‑sparing on CD.

3. Glomerular Filtration

Glomerular filtration is the first step of urine formation. The glomerular filtration rate (GFR) is the volume of plasma filtered per unit time (normal ~125 mL/min).

Determinants of GFR
  • Net filtration pressure = PGC – PBC – πGC (PGC = glomerular capillary hydrostatic pressure; PBC = Bowman's capsule pressure; πGC = glomerular capillary oncotic pressure).
  • Kf (filtration coefficient): Product of hydraulic conductivity and surface area.
Regulation of GFR
  • Autoregulation: Maintains GFR despite BP fluctuations (60‑160 mmHg). Two mechanisms:
    • Myogenic mechanism: Afferent arteriole constricts when stretched.
    • Tubuloglomerular feedback: Macula densa senses NaCl delivery; if high, signals afferent arteriole to constrict (via adenosine).
  • Sympathetic nervous system: Strong stimulation constricts afferent arterioles, ↓ GFR (e.g., during severe hemorrhage).
  • Angiotensin II: Constricts efferent arteriole to maintain GFR when BP low.

Clinical pearl: In renal artery stenosis, angiotensin II helps maintain GFR via efferent constriction; ACE inhibitors can cause acute kidney injury by removing this effect.

Memory aid: “Myogenic – muscle sense stretch; Tubuloglomerular – macula densa tells arteriole.”

4. Tubular Transport Mechanisms

Reabsorption and secretion occur via passive diffusion, facilitated diffusion, active transport, and co‑transport.

Proximal Tubule (PCT)
  • Reabsorbs ~65% of filtered Na⁺ and water, and almost all glucose, amino acids, and bicarbonate.
  • SGLT2 (Na⁺‑glucose co‑transporter) on apical membrane; basolateral GLUT2 and Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase.
  • Na⁺/H⁺ exchanger (NHE3): Mediates Na⁺ reabsorption and H⁺ secretion (involved in HCO₃⁻ reclamation).
Loop of Henle
  • Thick ascending limb (TAL): Apical Na‑K‑2Cl co‑transporter (NKCC2) reabsorbs NaCl; loop diuretics (furosemide) block it. This segment is water‑impermeable, creating dilute urine.
Distal Convoluted Tubule (DCT)
  • Na‑Cl co‑transporter (NCC) reabsorbs NaCl; thiazide diuretics block it.
Collecting Duct (CD)
  • Principal cells: Na⁺ reabsorption via ENaC (amiloride‑sensitive), K⁺ secretion via ROMK.
  • α‑intercalated cells: Secrete H⁺ (via H⁺‑ATPase) and reabsorb bicarbonate.
  • β‑intercalated cells: Secrete bicarbonate (less common).

Clinical pearl: Glucose appears in urine (glycosuria) when plasma glucose exceeds renal threshold (~180 mg/dL) due to SGLT saturation.

5. Renal Handling of Sodium, Potassium, Calcium, Phosphate
Sodium

Na⁺ reabsorption is tightly regulated. PCT (65%), TAL (25%), DCT (5%), CD (4%). Aldosterone increases Na⁺ reabsorption in CD.

Potassium
  • Most K⁺ reabsorbed in PCT and TAL; K⁺ secretion occurs in principal cells of CD (regulated by aldosterone).
  • Dietary K⁺ loading increases aldosterone, enhancing secretion.
Calcium
  • ~60% reabsorbed in PCT (passive), 25% in TAL (paracellular), and DCT/CNT regulated by PTH (stimulates Ca²⁺ reabsorption via TRPV5).
  • PTH increases Ca²⁺ reabsorption in DCT, and decreases PO₄³⁻ reabsorption in PCT.
Phosphate
  • Reabsorbed in PCT via Na⁺‑phosphate co‑transporters (NPT2a, NPT2c). Inhibited by PTH (phosphaturia).

Clinical pearl: Hyperkalemia can be caused by aldosterone deficiency, K⁺‑sparing diuretics, or renal failure. Hypocalcemia may result from hypoparathyroidism or vitamin D deficiency.

6. Renal Acid‑Base Balance

The kidneys regulate pH by reabsorbing filtered HCO₃⁻ and excreting fixed acids (via titratable acids and NH₄⁺).

Bicarbonate Reabsorption
  • In PCT: H⁺ secreted (via NHE3) combines with filtered HCO₃⁻ to form H₂CO₃, then CO₂ and H₂O; HCO₃⁻ is indirectly reabsorbed (new HCO₃⁻ generated).
Excretion of H⁺
  • Intercalated cells (α) secrete H⁺ via H⁺‑ATPase; H⁺ is buffered in urine by phosphate (titratable acid) and ammonia (NH₃ → NH₄⁺).

Clinical pearl: In metabolic acidosis, the kidney increases NH₃ production to excrete more acid. Renal tubular acidosis (RTA) involves defects in H⁺ secretion or HCO₃⁻ reabsorption.

Memory aid: “PCT reabsorbs HCO₃⁻; collecting duct secretes H⁺.”

7. Urine Concentration & Dilution

The countercurrent multiplier system (loop of Henle) creates a hypertonic medullary interstitium, allowing water reabsorption in collecting duct under ADH control.

Countercurrent Mechanism
  • Loop of Henle: Descending limb permeable to water (concentrates), ascending limb impermeable to water, actively transports NaCl into interstitium.
  • Vasa recta: Countercurrent exchange preserves medullary gradient.
Role of ADH
  • ADH (vasopressin) increases water permeability of collecting duct by inserting aquaporin‑2 channels.
  • In absence of ADH (diabetes insipidus), dilute urine (low osmolality).

Clinical pearl: Loop diuretics disrupt the countercurrent gradient, impairing concentrating ability. Lithium can cause nephrogenic diabetes insipidus.

8. Renal Hormones
  • Renin: Secreted by juxtaglomerular cells in response to low BP, low Na⁺, or sympathetic stimulation. Converts angiotensinogen to angiotensin I.
  • Angiotensin II: Potent vasoconstrictor, stimulates aldosterone release, increases thirst.
  • Aldosterone: Acts on principal cells to increase Na⁺ reabsorption and K⁺ secretion.
  • Erythropoietin (EPO): Stimulates RBC production; deficiency causes anemia in CKD.
  • Calcitriol (1,25‑dihydroxyvitamin D): Activated in proximal tubule; increases intestinal Ca²⁺ absorption.
  • Prostaglandins: Vasodilators that protect renal blood flow during stress.
9. Micturition

Urine storage and voiding involve autonomic and somatic nerves.

  • Detrusor muscle: Relaxes during filling (sympathetic β₃), contracts during voiding (parasympathetic M₃).
  • Internal urethral sphincter: Smooth muscle, sympathetic α₁ maintains contraction.
  • External urethral sphincter: Skeletal muscle, somatic (pudendal nerve) maintains voluntary control.

Clinical pearl: Spinal cord injury above sacral level causes spastic bladder; damage to sacral parasympathetic causes flaccid bladder.

10. Renal Function Tests
  • GFR: Measured by inulin clearance (gold standard), or estimated by creatinine clearance or eGFR (CKD‑EPI, MDRD).
  • Renal plasma flow (RPF): Measured by PAH clearance.
  • Filtration fraction = GFR / RPF (normal ~0.2).
  • Urine osmolality, specific gravity: Assess concentrating ability.
  • Fractional excretion of Na⁺ (FeNa): Differentiates prerenal (<1%) from intrinsic renal (>2%) AKI.

Clinical pearl: FeNa <1% suggests prerenal azotemia; >2% suggests acute tubular necrosis.
