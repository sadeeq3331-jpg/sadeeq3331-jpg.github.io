1. Fat‑Soluble Vitamins (A, D, E, K)
Vitamin A (Retinol, Retinoic Acid)
  • Preparations: Retinol, retinyl palmitate, tretinoin (topical for acne, photoaging), isotretinoin (oral for severe acne).
  • Uses: Deficiency treatment (night blindness, xerophthalmia). Isotretinoin for cystic acne.
  • Mechanism: Essential for vision (11‑cis retinal), epithelial integrity, gene expression.
  • Toxicity (hypervitaminosis A): Acute: nausea, headache, blurred vision. Chronic: alopecia, dry skin, hepatotoxicity, bone pain, increased ICP (pseudotumor cerebri). Teratogenic (isotretinoin – iPLEDGE program).
Vitamin D (Cholecalciferol, Ergocalciferol)
  • Preparations: Vitamin D₂ (ergocalciferol), vitamin D₃ (cholecalciferol), calcitriol (1,25‑OH₂D₃ active form), calcipotriene (topical for psoriasis).
  • Uses: Deficiency (rickets, osteomalacia), hypoparathyroidism, CKD (with phosphate binders), osteoporosis (with calcium).
  • Mechanism: Increases calcium absorption in gut, promotes bone mineralization.
  • Toxicity (hypervitaminosis D): Hypercalcemia → nausea, vomiting, weakness, confusion, nephrocalcinosis.
Vitamin E (Tocopherol)
  • Uses: Deficiency (prematurity, fat malabsorption, abetalipoproteinemia). Antioxidant.
  • Toxicity: High doses may increase bleeding risk (vitamin K antagonism).
Vitamin K (Phytonadione, Menadione)
  • Preparations: Vitamin K₁ (phytonadione) – oral, SC, IV. Vitamin K₂ (menaquinone) – gut bacteria.
  • Uses: Newborn prophylaxis (hemorrhagic disease), warfarin reversal, deficiency (malabsorption, liver disease).
  • Mechanism: Cofactor for γ‑carboxylation of factors II, VII, IX, X, protein C, protein S.
  • Toxicity: IV phytonadione can cause anaphylactoid reactions (rare).
2. Water‑Soluble Vitamins (B‑complex, C)
Thiamine (Vitamin B1)
  • Uses: Wernicke‑Korsakoff syndrome (especially in alcoholism), beriberi.
  • Dosing: IV/IM for acute deficiency; always give before glucose to prevent Wernicke encephalopathy.
  • Mechanism: Cofactor for transketolase, pyruvate dehydrogenase, α‑ketoglutarate dehydrogenase.
Riboflavin (Vitamin B2)
  • Uses: Deficiency (cheilosis, glossitis).
Niacin (Vitamin B3) – Nicotinic Acid, Niacinamide
  • Uses: Pellagra (dermatitis, diarrhea, dementia). Also used for dyslipidemia (nicotinic acid lowers LDL, raises HDL).
  • Side effects (nicotinic acid): Flushing (prostaglandin‑mediated; aspirin can reduce), hepatotoxicity, hyperglycemia, hyperuricemia.
  • Niacinamide: Does not cause flushing or dyslipidemia effects; used for pellagra only.
Pyridoxine (Vitamin B6)
  • Uses: Isoniazid‑induced neuropathy (prophylaxis), sideroblastic anemia, pyridoxine‑dependent seizures.
  • Mechanism: Cofactor for transaminases, decarboxylases (neurotransmitter synthesis).
Folate (Vitamin B9) – Folic Acid
  • Uses: Megaloblastic anemia (with B12 deficiency, give B12 first to avoid precipitating neurologic symptoms), pregnancy to prevent neural tube defects.
  • Mechanism: Converted to tetrahydrofolate; essential for DNA synthesis.
  • Note: Methotrexate is a folate antagonist; leucovorin (folinic acid) bypasses DHFR inhibition.
Cobalamin (Vitamin B12) – Cyanocobalamin, Hydroxocobalamin
  • Uses: Pernicious anemia, B12 deficiency, also as antidote for cyanide poisoning (hydroxocobalamin).
  • Administration: Oral (only if deficiency not due to malabsorption), IM/SC for pernicious anemia.
  • Mechanism: Cofactor for methionine synthase and methylmalonyl‑CoA mutase.
Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)
  • Uses: Scurvy, wound healing, urinary acidification.
  • Side effects: Diarrhea (high doses), kidney stones (oxalate).
3. Minerals & Trace Elements
Iron Preparations
  • Oral: Ferrous sulfate, ferrous gluconate, ferrous fumarate.
  • Parenteral: Iron dextran, iron sucrose, ferric carboxymaltose.
  • Uses: Iron deficiency anemia.
  • Side effects: GI upset, constipation, black stools; parenteral can cause anaphylactoid reactions.
Zinc
  • Uses: Zinc deficiency, acrodermatitis enteropathica, Wilson disease (as copper chelator).
  • Mechanism: Cofactor for many enzymes; also inhibits copper absorption.
  • Side effects: GI irritation, copper deficiency with chronic use.
Copper
  • Uses: Copper deficiency (rare).
  • Copper chelators: Penicillamine, trientine (for Wilson disease).
Selenium
  • Uses: Selenium deficiency (Keshan disease), part of multivitamin preparations.
Iodine
  • Uses: Iodine deficiency (goiter), radioactive iodine (¹³¹I) for hyperthyroidism/thyroid cancer, topical antiseptic.
Calcium
  • Preparations: Calcium carbonate, calcium citrate, calcium gluconate, calcium chloride.
  • Uses: Osteoporosis prevention/treatment, hypocalcemia (IV calcium gluconate/chloride).
  • IV forms: Calcium chloride has 3× more elemental calcium than gluconate but is more irritating.
Magnesium
  • Preparations: Magnesium oxide, hydroxide (antacid), sulfate (IV for eclampsia, hypomagnesemia).
  • Uses: Hypomagnesemia, eclampsia (MgSO₄).
Phosphate
  • Uses: Hypophosphatemia (oral or IV), phosphate supplementation in parenteral nutrition.
4. Centrally Acting Anti‑Obesity Drugs
  • Phentermine (sympathomimetic amine): Schedule IV, approved for short‑term use (≤12 weeks). Increases norepinephrine, suppresses appetite. Side effects: tachycardia, hypertension, insomnia, dry mouth.
  • Diethylpropion, benzphetamine: Similar to phentermine; also short‑term.
  • Liraglutide (GLP‑1 agonist): Also used for type 2 diabetes; at higher doses (Saxenda) for weight loss. Delays gastric emptying, increases satiety. Side effects: nausea, vomiting, pancreatitis, gallbladder disease.
  • Semaglutide (GLP‑1 agonist): (Wegovy) once weekly for obesity; similar to liraglutide.
  • Naltrexone/bupropion (Contrave): Combination: naltrexone (opioid antagonist) + bupropion (dopamine/norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor). Acts on hypothalamic appetite centers. Side effects: nausea, headache, seizures (lower seizure threshold).
5. Peripherally Acting Drugs
  • Orlistat (Xenical, Alli): Inhibits gastric and pancreatic lipase → reduces fat absorption (~30%). Side effects: oily stools, fecal urgency, flatulence, malabsorption of fat‑soluble vitamins (supplement recommended). Contraindicated in cholestasis.
  • Setmelanotide: Melanocortin‑4 receptor agonist; used for rare genetic obesity (POMC, PCSK1, LEPR deficiencies).
6. Combination Products
  • Phentermine/topiramate (Qsymia): Topiramate (antiepileptic) adds appetite suppression. Side effects: paresthesia, cognitive impairment, metabolic acidosis, teratogenic (cleft lip).
  • Naltrexone/bupropion (already covered).
7. Antidiabetic Drugs (Review)
  • Biguanides: Metformin – first‑line for type 2 diabetes; reduces hepatic glucose output, improves insulin sensitivity. Side effects: GI upset, lactic acidosis (rare; avoid in renal failure).
  • Sulfonylureas: Glipizide, glyburide – stimulate insulin secretion. Risk of hypoglycemia.
  • Thiazolidinediones: Pioglitazone – increase insulin sensitivity (PPAR‑γ). Fluid retention, weight gain, fracture risk.
  • DPP‑4 inhibitors: Sitagliptin – increase incretins (GLP‑1, GIP). Well tolerated.
  • GLP‑1 agonists: Exenatide, liraglutide, semaglutide – also promote weight loss (used in obesity).
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: Empagliflozin, dapagliflozin – block renal glucose reabsorption; also promote weight loss.
8. Lipid‑Lowering Agents
  • Statins (HMG‑CoA reductase inhibitors): Atorvastatin, simvastatin, rosuvastatin – first‑line for LDL reduction. Side effects: myopathy, hepatotoxicity (rare).
  • Ezetimibe: Inhibits cholesterol absorption (NPC1L1).
  • PCSK9 inhibitors: Evolocumab, alirocumab – monoclonal antibodies increase LDL receptor recycling.
  • Fibrates (PPAR‑α agonists): Fenofibrate, gemfibrozil – lower triglycerides, increase HDL. Gemfibrozil contraindicated with statins (myopathy risk).
  • Niacin (nicotinic acid): Lowers VLDL, raises HDL; side effects: flushing, hepatotoxicity, hyperglycemia.
  • Omega‑3 fatty acids: Prescription (icosapent ethyl, omega‑3 ethyl esters) for hypertriglyceridemia.
9. Anti‑Gout Drugs
  • Acute gout:
    • NSAIDs (indomethacin, naproxen) – first‑line.
    • Colchicine – inhibits microtubule polymerization, reduces neutrophil chemotaxis. Side effects: GI toxicity, myelosuppression.
    • Corticosteroids – oral or intra‑articular.
  • Chronic gout (urate‑lowering):
    • Xanthine oxidase inhibitors: Allopurinol, febuxostat – reduce uric acid production. Allopurinol hypersensitivity (HLA‑B*5801).
    • Uricosurics: Probenecid – inhibits URAT1, increases uric acid excretion.
    • Recombinant uricase: Pegloticase – for refractory gout; contraindicated in G6PD deficiency.
10. Appetite Stimulants
  • Megestrol acetate (progestin): Used for appetite stimulation in cancer/AIDS cachexia. Mechanism unknown. Side effects: adrenal suppression, hyperglycemia, thromboembolism.
  • Dronabinol (cannabinoid): Synthetic THC; used for anorexia in AIDS, chemotherapy‑induced nausea. Side effects: euphoria, dizziness.
  • Cyproheptadine (antihistamine with serotonin antagonism): Used for appetite stimulation in children, also for carcinoid syndrome.
  • Oxandrolone (anabolic steroid): Promotes weight gain in catabolic states.
11. Psychotropics in Eating Disorders
  • Anorexia nervosa: No FDA‑approved drugs; SSRIs (fluoxetine) may help prevent relapse after weight restoration, but not for acute weight gain. Olanzapine may help with weight gain and reduce obsessive thoughts.
  • Bulimia nervosa: Fluoxetine (high dose 60 mg/day) is FDA‑approved; reduces binge‑purge cycles.
  • Binge‑eating disorder: Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse) – a stimulant, FDA‑approved for moderate‑severe binge eating. Also SSRIs, topiramate.
12. Enteral Nutrition
  • Feeding via GI tract (oral, nasogastric, gastrostomy, jejunostomy). Preferred over parenteral when GI functional.
  • Formulations: Standard (polymeric), elemental (peptides/amino acids), disease‑specific (diabetes, renal, hepatic).
  • Complications: Aspiration, diarrhea, tube dislodgement, refeeding syndrome.
13. Parenteral Nutrition
  • IV nutrition for patients with non‑functioning GI tract.
  • Components: Dextrose, amino acids, lipids (soybean oil, olive oil, fish oil), electrolytes, vitamins, trace elements.
  • Complications: Catheter‑related infections, thrombosis, metabolic (hyperglycemia, electrolyte abnormalities, hypertriglyceridemia), liver dysfunction (PN‑associated cholestasis), refeeding syndrome.
14. Refeeding Syndrome
Potentially fatal metabolic complication when feeding is reintroduced to malnourished patients after a period of starvation.
  • Pathophysiology: Insulin surge → cellular uptake of phosphate, potassium, magnesium → hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia; fluid retention, thiamine deficiency.
  • Clinical: Cardiac arrhythmias, heart failure, respiratory failure, neurological symptoms, death.
  • Prevention: Identify at‑risk patients, start feeding slowly, monitor electrolytes, supplement phosphate, potassium, magnesium, and thiamine before feeding.
