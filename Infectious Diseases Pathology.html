1. Respiratory Tract Infections
Upper Respiratory Infections
  • Common cold: Rhinovirus, coronavirus, RSV. Mild, self‑limited.
  • Pharyngitis: Viral (adenovirus, EBV) or bacterial (Group A Strep – Streptococcus pyogenes). Strep pharyngitis can lead to rheumatic fever, post‑streptococcal glomerulonephritis.
  • Otitis media: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Moraxella catarrhalis.
  • Sinusitis: Same pathogens as otitis; chronic sinusitis may involve anaerobes.
Lower Respiratory Infections
  • Acute bronchitis: Usually viral (influenza, parainfluenza, RSV).
  • Pneumonia:
    • Community‑acquired (CAP): Strep. pneumoniae (most common), H. influenzae, Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Chlamydophila pneumoniae, Legionella.
    • Hospital‑acquired (HAP): MRSA, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Enterobacteriaceae.
    • Aspiration pneumonia: Anaerobes (oral flora).
Tuberculosis
  • Etiology: Mycobacterium tuberculosis (acid‑fast bacillus).
  • Primary TB: Ghon focus (subpleural) + involved lymph node = Ghon complex. Usually heals.
  • Secondary (reactivation) TB: Apical lesions, cavitation, caseous necrosis.
  • Miliary TB: Hematogenous dissemination, millet‑seed granulomas.

Clinical pearl: PPD (tuberculin skin test) measures delayed hypersensitivity; positive in latent or active TB.

2. Cardiovascular Infections
Infective Endocarditis
  • Acute: Staph. aureus – destructive, often normal valves.
  • Subacute: Viridans streptococci (S. sanguis, S. mitis) – damaged valves, dental procedures.
  • IV drug users: S. aureus (tricuspid valve).
  • Prosthetic valve: Early (<2 months) – Staph. epidermidis, S. aureus; late – similar to native valve.
  • Pathology: Vegetations (fibrin, platelets, bacteria). Complications: valve destruction, emboli, abscess.
Myocarditis
  • Viral (Coxsackie B, influenza), Chagas disease (Trypanosoma cruzi), Lyme disease (Borrelia burgdorferi).
Pericarditis
  • Viral, TB, purulent (bacterial), post‑MI.
3. Central Nervous System Infections
Meningitis
  • Bacterial: Strep. pneumoniae (adults), Neisseria meningitidis (adolescents), H. influenzae type b (children, vaccine preventable), Group B Strep (neonates), Listeria monocytogenes (neonates, elderly). CSF: low glucose, high protein, neutrophils.
  • Viral (aseptic): Enteroviruses, HSV, arboviruses. CSF: normal glucose, mild protein, lymphocytosis.
  • Tuberculous: Basilar exudates, cranial nerve palsies; CSF: low glucose, high protein, lymphocytes.
  • Fungal: Cryptococcus neoformans (HIV), India ink positive, cryptococcal antigen.
Encephalitis
  • HSV‑1 (temporal lobe, hemorrhagic necrosis), arboviruses (West Nile, Eastern equine), rabies.
Brain Abscess
  • Often polymicrobial (streptococci, anaerobes, staph). From contiguous spread (sinusitis, otitis) or hematogenous.
4. Gastrointestinal Infections
Esophagitis
  • Candida albicans: White plaques, immunocompromised.
  • HSV: Ulcers, vesicles.
  • CMV: Linear ulcers, immunocompromised.
Gastritis & Peptic Ulcer
  • Helicobacter pylori: Gram‑negative spiral, urease positive, causes chronic gastritis, PUD, MALToma, gastric adenocarcinoma.
Enterocolitis / Diarrhea
  • E. coli: ETEC (traveler's diarrhea), EPEC, EHEC (O157:H7 – HUS), EIEC, EAEC.
  • Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, Yersinia.
  • Vibrio cholerae: Cholera toxin → watery diarrhea.
  • Clostridium difficile: Pseudomembranous colitis, toxin A/B, after antibiotics.
  • Rotavirus, Norovirus, Adenovirus.
5. Urinary Tract Infections
  • Cystitis: E. coli (most common), Staph. saprophyticus (young women), Klebsiella, Proteus.
  • Pyelonephritis: Ascending infection, fever, flank pain, WBC casts.
  • Catheter‑associated UTI: E. coli, Pseudomonas, Enterococcus, Candida.
6. Reproductive Tract Infections & STIs
  • Gonorrhea: Neisseria gonorrhoeae – urethritis, cervicitis, PID, disseminated infection (arthritis‑dermatitis).
  • Chlamydia: Chlamydia trachomatis – urethritis, cervicitis, PID, lymphogranuloma venereum (LGV).
  • Syphilis: Treponema pallidum – primary (chancre), secondary (rash, condyloma lata), tertiary (gumma, neurosyphilis, cardiovascular).
  • Chancroid: Haemophilus ducreyi – painful ulcer, suppurative lymphadenopathy.
  • HPV: Condyloma acuminata (low‑risk), cervical dysplasia/cancer (high‑risk 16,18).
  • Herpes simplex: HSV‑2 (most genital), painful vesicles, recurrent.
  • Trichomoniasis: Trichomonas vaginalis – frothy yellow discharge, motile organisms.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID): Polymicrobial (gonorrhea, chlamydia, anaerobes) → tubal scarring, infertility, ectopic pregnancy.
7. Skin & Soft Tissue Infections
  • Cellulitis: Strep. pyogenes, Staph. aureus.
  • Erysipelas: Superficial, group A strep, well‑demarcated, raised.
  • Folliculitis, furuncle, carbuncle: S. aureus.
  • Necrotizing fasciitis: Strep. pyogenes (type II), polymicrobial (type I). Surgical emergency.
  • Gas gangrene: Clostridium perfringens – myonecrosis, gas on X‑ray.
  • Diabetic foot infections: Polymicrobial (staph, strep, anaerobes, Gram‑negatives).
8. Systemic Infections & Sepsis
Sepsis
  • Life‑threatening organ dysfunction caused by dysregulated host response to infection. Gram‑negative endotoxin (LPS) triggers cytokine storm.
Toxic Shock Syndrome
  • Staphylococcal TSS: TSST‑1 superantigen, fever, rash, hypotension, multiorgan failure. Associated with tampon use, wounds.
  • Streptococcal TSS: Strep. pyogenes, invasive infection, rapid progression.
Infectious Mononucleosis
  • EBV, fever, pharyngitis, lymphadenopathy, atypical lymphocytes, heterophile antibodies.
Measles, Mumps, Rubella
  • Measles: Koplik spots, rash; mumps: parotitis; rubella: congenital syndrome.
9. Congenital & Perinatal Infections

TORCH infections cause congenital malformations, growth restriction, and neonatal disease.

  • Toxoplasmosis: Chorioretinitis, intracranial calcifications, hydrocephalus.
  • Other (syphilis, HIV, VZV, parvovirus B19).
  • Rubella: Cataracts, deafness, PDA, blueberry muffin rash.
  • CMV: Most common congenital infection – petechiae, jaundice, hepatosplenomegaly, microcephaly, chorioretinitis.
  • HSV: Disseminated, CNS, or localized (skin, eye, mouth) infection in newborn.
10. Opportunistic Infections

Infections occurring in immunocompromised hosts (HIV, transplant, chemotherapy).

  • Pneumocystis jirovecii: Pneumonia (PJP) – ground‑glass opacities, CD4 <200.
  • Toxoplasma gondii: Brain abscess in HIV.
  • CMV: Retinitis, esophagitis, colitis, pneumonitis.
  • Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC): Disseminated in advanced HIV.
  • Cryptococcus neoformans: Meningitis.
  • Aspergillus: Invasive pulmonary aspergillosis in neutropenic patients.
  • Candida: Esophagitis, candidemia.
