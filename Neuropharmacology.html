1. Drugs for Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's disease results from degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra, leading to striatal dopamine deficiency. Pharmacotherapy aims to restore dopamine activity or modulate other neurotransmitters.

Drug ClassExamplesMechanismKey Points
Levodopa (L‑DOPA)Levodopa (with carbidopa)Precursor of dopamine; crosses BBBMost effective; always combined with carbidopa (peripheral decarboxylase inhibitor) to reduce peripheral conversion. Long‑term use leads to motor fluctuations and dyskinesias.
Dopamine agonistsPramipexole, ropinirole, rotigotine, apomorphineDirectly stimulate dopamine D₂ receptorsLess potent than levodopa; used as monotherapy in early disease or adjunct. May cause impulse control disorders.
MAO‑B inhibitorsSelegiline, rasagilineInhibit metabolism of dopamineMild efficacy; may be used early or as adjunct. Selegiline metabolized to amphetamine (insomnia).
COMT inhibitorsEntacapone, tolcaponeInhibit peripheral metabolism of levodopaUsed with levodopa to reduce “off” time. Tolcapone can cause hepatotoxicity (monitor LFTs).
AnticholinergicsBenztropine, trihexyphenidylBlock central muscarinic receptorsUseful for tremor, but side effects (dry mouth, confusion, constipation). Limited use in elderly.
AmantadineAmantadineAntiviral with weak dopamine agonist and anticholinergic effects; also blocks NMDA receptorsReduces dyskinesias; used as adjunct.

Pearl: Levodopa is the gold standard, but long‑term therapy leads to motor complications. COMT inhibitors and MAO‑B inhibitors can prolong levodopa effect. Avoid abrupt withdrawal of antiparkinsonian drugs (may cause neuroleptic malignant‑like syndrome).

2. Drugs for Alzheimer's Disease

Alzheimer's disease is associated with loss of cholinergic neurons and accumulation of amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles. Current drugs provide symptomatic benefit.

Cholinesterase Inhibitors
  • Donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine: Inhibit acetylcholinesterase, increasing synaptic ACh in brain.
  • Uses: Mild to moderate Alzheimer's disease (donepezil also for severe).
  • Adverse effects: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, bradycardia, syncope (vagotonic effects).
NMDA Receptor Antagonist
  • Memantine: Non‑competitive antagonist of NMDA receptors; reduces excitotoxicity.
  • Uses: Moderate to severe Alzheimer's disease; often combined with donepezil.
  • Adverse effects: Dizziness, headache, constipation.

Pearl: Cholinesterase inhibitors can cause bradycardia and should be used with caution in patients with sick sinus syndrome or on beta‑blockers.

Antiepileptics suppress neuronal hyperexcitability by modulating ion channels or neurotransmitter systems. Choice depends on seizure type.

4. Antipsychotics

Antipsychotics are used for schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and other psychoses. They are classified as typical (first‑generation) and atypical (second‑generation).

ClassExamplesMechanismAdverse Effects
Typical (FGA)Haloperidol, chlorpromazine, fluphenazineD₂ receptor antagonism (strong)Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS): dystonia, parkinsonism, akathisia, tardive dyskinesia. Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS). Anticholinergic effects (chlorpromazine more).
Atypical (SGA)Clozapine, risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole, ziprasidone, paliperidoneD₂ + 5‑HT₂A antagonism (partial agonist at D₂ for aripiprazole)Less EPS (except risperidone high dose). Metabolic syndrome: weight gain, diabetes, dyslipidemia (most with clozapine, olanzapine). Clozapine: agranulocytosis (monitor WBC), myocarditis, sialorrhea.
Key Points
  • Clozapine: Reserved for treatment‑resistant schizophrenia due to agranulocytosis risk; requires regular blood monitoring.
  • EPS: More common with high‑potency typicals (haloperidol). Atypicals are preferred for first‑line therapy.
  • NMS: Life‑threatening; presents with rigidity, fever, autonomic instability, elevated CK. Treatment: dantrolene, bromocriptine, supportive care.

Pearl: SSRIs are first‑line for depression. Avoid MAOIs with other serotonergics. TCAs are effective but dangerous in overdose.

6. Mood Stabilizers
  • Lithium: Mechanism unclear; modulates inositol phosphate, G proteins, and neuroprotection. Used for bipolar disorder (mania and maintenance). Narrow therapeutic index (0.6‑1.2 mEq/L). Toxicity: polyuria, polydipsia, tremor, nausea, ataxia, seizures, coma, arrhythmias. Monitor renal, thyroid function, and lithium levels.
  • Valproate: Effective for acute mania. Also used in epilepsy. Adverse effects: hepatotoxicity, thrombocytopenia, weight gain.
  • Carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine: Anticonvulsants with mood‑stabilizing properties. Carbamazepine induces its own metabolism; may cause SIADH, rash.
  • Lamotrigine: More effective for bipolar depression than mania; slow titration to avoid rash.

8. Opioid Analgesics

Opioids act on μ, κ, and δ receptors. μ receptor activation produces analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, and physical dependence.

DrugPropertiesKey Points
MorphinePrototypical μ agonistMetabolite morphine‑6‑glucuronide active. Histamine release (pruritus, hypotension).
CodeineProdrug (CYP2D6 to morphine)Weak analgesic; also antitussive.
Oxycodone, hydrocodoneSemi‑syntheticHigh abuse potential.
MethadoneLong half‑life, NMDA antagonistUsed for chronic pain and opioid addiction; QT prolongation.
FentanylHigh potency (100× morphine)Transdermal, IV; rapid onset; risk of respiratory depression.
Meperidine (pethidine)μ agonistMetabolite normeperidine causes seizures; avoid in renal impairment, with MAOIs (serotonin syndrome).
TramadolWeak μ agonist + inhibits reuptake of 5‑HT/NESeizure risk; serotonin syndrome with SSRIs.
Naloxoneμ antagonist (antidote)IV/IM; short half‑life (may need repeat). Precipitates withdrawal.
BuprenorphinePartial μ agonistUsed for addiction; ceiling effect on respiratory depression.

Adverse effects: Respiratory depression (cause of death), constipation, nausea, sedation, tolerance, dependence. Miosis (except meperidine).

9. General Anesthetics
Inhaled Anesthetics
  • Halothane: Hepatotoxicity (halothane hepatitis), sensitizes myocardium to catecholamines.
  • Isoflurane, desflurane, sevoflurane: Low metabolism, rapid recovery. Desflurane irritates airways.
  • Nitrous oxide: Weak anesthetic, analgesic; can cause megaloblastic anemia with prolonged use (oxidizes vitamin B12).
Intravenous Anesthetics
  • Propofol: GABAA potentiation; rapid onset/offset; used for induction/maintenance; pain on injection, respiratory depression, hypotension. Risk of propofol infusion syndrome (high doses).
  • Thiopental (barbiturate): GABAA; used for induction; respiratory depression, hypotension.
  • Etomidate: Minimal hemodynamic effects; adrenal suppression (even single dose).
  • Ketamine: NMDA antagonist; dissociative anesthesia; sympathetic stimulation (increases HR, BP); emergence reactions.
  • Midazolam, diazepam: Benzodiazepines; used for sedation, induction (less common).
Neuromuscular Blockers (NMB)
  • Depolarizing: Succinylcholine – causes fasciculations, hyperkalemia, malignant hyperthermia trigger, prolonged apnea in pseudocholinesterase deficiency.
  • Non‑depolarizing: Rocuronium, vecuronium, atracurium, cisatracurium – competitive antagonists; reversal with neostigmine (anticholinesterase) plus glycopyrrolate or sugammadex (for rocuronium).
10. Local Anesthetics

Local anesthetics block voltage‑gated Na⁺ channels, preventing nerve conduction. They are classified as esters or amides.

  • Esters: Cocaine, procaine, benzocaine, tetracaine. Metabolized by pseudocholinesterase; may cause allergic reactions.
  • Amides: Lidocaine, bupivacaine, ropivacaine, mepivacaine. Metabolized by liver; rarely allergic.
Key Points
  • Lidocaine: Also antiarrhythmic (Class Ib). Maximum dose with epinephrine 7 mg/kg, without 4.5 mg/kg.
  • Bupivacaine: Long duration; more cardiotoxic (ventricular arrhythmias) if injected IV.
  • Cocaine: Only local anesthetic with vasoconstrictor effect (inhibits NE reuptake); used for nasal procedures.
  • Toxicity: CNS: numbness, tingling, seizures, coma. CVS: hypotension, arrhythmias. Treatment: lipid emulsion (especially for bupivacaine).
11. CNS Stimulants
  • Amphetamines: Increase dopamine and norepinephrine release; used for ADHD, narcolepsy. Side effects: insomnia, anorexia, tachycardia, abuse potential.
  • Methylphenidate: Blocks dopamine and norepinephrine reuptake; similar uses and side effects.
  • Modafinil: Promotes wakefulness; used for narcolepsy, shift work disorder; mechanism unclear (dopamine reuptake inhibition). Low abuse potential.
  • Caffeine: Adenosine receptor antagonist; mild stimulant; tolerance, withdrawal headache.
12. Migraine Drugs
Acute (Abortive) Therapy
  • Triptans: Sumatriptan, rizatriptan, etc. – 5‑HT₁B/1D agonists, vasoconstrict cerebral vessels. Contraindicated in CAD, stroke, uncontrolled hypertension.
  • Ergot alkaloids: Ergotamine, dihydroergotamine – non‑selective serotonin agonists; also vasoconstrict. Risk of ergotism.
  • NSAIDs, acetaminophen, antiemetics.
Preventive (Prophylactic) Therapy
  • Beta‑blockers: Propranolol, timolol.
  • Anticonvulsants: Valproate, topiramate.
  • Antidepressants: Amitriptyline, venlafaxine.
  • Calcium channel blockers: Verapamil.
  • CGRP monoclonal antibodies: Erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab – novel preventive.
13. Muscle Relaxants
  • Baclofen: GABAB agonist; used for spasticity (MS, spinal cord injury). Side effects: sedation, weakness, withdrawal syndrome.
  • Tizanidine: α₂ agonist (centrally acting); for spasticity. Sedation, hypotension, hepatotoxicity.
  • Dantrolene: Directly acts on skeletal muscle ryanodine receptors, inhibiting Ca²⁺ release; used for malignant hyperthermia, spasticity. Hepatotoxicity.
  • Cyclobenzaprine, carisoprodol, methocarbamol: Centrally acting; used for acute muscle spasms. Sedation, anticholinergic effects. Carisoprodol metabolized to meprobamate (abuse potential).
14. Drugs for Substance Abuse
  • Alcohol dependence: Naltrexone (opioid antagonist reduces craving), acamprosate (modulates glutamate), disulfiram (inhibits acetaldehyde dehydrogenase; causes aversive reaction with alcohol).
  • Opioid dependence: Methadone (long‑acting μ agonist), buprenorphine (partial μ agonist), naltrexone (antagonist).
  • Nicotine dependence: Nicotine replacement (patch, gum), varenicline (partial nicotinic agonist), bupropion.
