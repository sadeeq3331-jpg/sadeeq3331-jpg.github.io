1. Introduction to Toxicology
Toxicology is the study of the adverse effects of chemical, physical, or biological agents on living organisms and the environment. It encompasses the detection, mechanisms, and treatment of poisoning.

Key Definitions

  • Toxin: A poison of biological origin (e.g., bacterial toxins, venom).
  • Toxicant: A synthetic or man‑made poison (e.g., industrial chemicals).
  • Xenobiotic: Any foreign substance entering the body.
  • LD₅₀ (median lethal dose): The dose required to kill 50% of a test population.
  • ED₅₀ (median effective dose): The dose producing a desired effect in 50%.
  • Therapeutic index: TD₅₀/ED₅₀ (or LD₅₀/ED₅₀); a measure of safety.
Paracelsus (16th century) famously stated: “All things are poison, and nothing is without poison; the dose alone makes a thing not a poison.” This underscores the concept that toxicity is dose‑dependent.
2. Toxicokinetics
Toxicokinetics describes how the body handles a toxicant: absorption, distribution, metabolism (biotransformation), and excretion (ADME).
Absorption
  • Oral (GI tract): Passive diffusion, active transport. First‑pass metabolism (liver) reduces bioavailability.
  • Inhalation: Rapid absorption via lungs (large surface area).
  • Dermal: Lipid‑soluble compounds penetrate skin.
  • Parenteral: IV, IM, SC – direct entry.
Distribution
  • Depends on blood flow, protein binding (albumin, α₁‑acid glycoprotein), tissue affinity (e.g., lead in bone, lipophilic compounds in fat).
  • Volume of distribution (Vd) – high Vd suggests extensive tissue binding.
Metabolism (Biotransformation)
  • Phase I: Oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis (cytochrome P450 enzymes) – often introduces or exposes a functional group.
  • Phase II: Conjugation (glucuronidation, sulfation, glutathione, acetylation) – increases water solubility for excretion.
  • Some compounds undergo bioactivation (e.g., acetaminophen → NAPQI; methanol → formaldehyde → formic acid).
Excretion
  • Renal: Glomerular filtration, tubular secretion, passive reabsorption.
  • Biliary: For larger molecules (>300 Da), enterohepatic recirculation may occur.
  • Pulmonary: Gases and volatile compounds (e.g., ethanol, CO).
  • Other: Sweat, milk, saliva.
3. Toxicodynamics
Toxicodynamics refers to the mechanisms by which toxicants exert their effects at the molecular, cellular, or organ level.
  • Receptor‑mediated: Agonist/antagonist actions (e.g., organophosphates inhibit acetylcholinesterase).
  • Enzyme inhibition: Cyanide inhibits cytochrome oxidase; lead inhibits ALA dehydratase.
  • Interference with ion channels: Local anesthetics block Na⁺ channels; DDT alters Na⁺ channel kinetics.
  • Oxidative stress: Generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), depletion of glutathione (e.g., acetaminophen).
  • Mitochondrial dysfunction: Uncoupling oxidative phosphorylation (e.g., dinitrophenol).
  • DNA damage: Alkylating agents, intercalators; may lead to carcinogenesis.
  • Protein synthesis inhibition: Ricin, diphtheria toxin.
4. Hepatotoxicity
The liver is especially vulnerable because of its role in xenobiotic metabolism and its portal blood supply. Injury can be:
  • Hepatocellular necrosis: Centrilobular (acetaminophen, carbon tetrachloride), midzonal, periportal.
  • Steatosis (fatty liver): Ethanol, methotrexate, valproate – due to impaired lipid metabolism.
  • Cholestasis: Bile duct injury (chlorpromazine, anabolic steroids).
  • Granulomatous hepatitis: Sulfonamides, allopurinol.
  • Vascular lesions: Veno‑occlusive disease (pyrrolizidine alkaloids).

Acetaminophen hepatotoxicity: CYP2E1 converts to NAPQI, which depletes glutathione. Overdose >4 g/day can cause centrilobular necrosis. N‑acetylcysteine is antidote.

5. Nephrotoxicity
  • Proximal tubule injury: Heavy metals (mercury, cadmium), aminoglycosides, cisplatin.
  • Glomerular injury: NSAIDs, gold, penicillamine (membranous nephropathy).
  • Interstitial nephritis: NSAIDs, penicillin, proton pump inhibitors.
  • Osmotic nephrosis: IV contrast, mannitol.
  • Renal papillary necrosis: NSAIDs (especially phenacetin).

Mechanisms often involve transport and accumulation (e.g., aminoglycosides are taken up by proximal tubular cells).

6. Neurotoxicity
  • Neurotransmitter interference: Organophosphates (AChE inhibition), botulinum toxin (inhibits ACh release), amphetamines (monoamine release).
  • Ion channel blockade: Tetrodotoxin, saxitoxin (Na⁺ channel blockers) → paralysis.
  • Myelin damage: Hexachlorophene, lead, solvents.
  • Axonal degeneration: Acrylamide, organophosphates (delayed neuropathy), vinca alkaloids.
  • Neurotransmitter depletion: Reserpine (catecholamines), MPTP (dopamine neuron destruction).
7. Cardiotoxicity
  • Arrhythmogenesis: Digoxin (Na/K‑ATPase inhibition), tricyclic antidepressants (Na⁺ channel blockade → QRS widening), β‑agonists (tachycardia).
  • Myocardial ischemia: Cocaine (vasospasm), carbon monoxide (hypoxia).
  • Cardiomyopathy: Anthracyclines (doxorubicin – free radical damage), ethanol, cobalt.
  • QT prolongation: Class Ia/III antiarrhythmics, antipsychotics, methadone, fluoroquinolones – risk of torsade de pointes.
8. Pulmonary Toxicity
  • Direct irritation: Chlorine, ammonia, phosgene → pulmonary edema.
  • Fibrosis: Bleomycin, amiodarone, asbestos, silica.
  • Asthma/ hypersensitivity: Toluene diisocyanate, plicatic acid (western red cedar).
  • ARDS: Paraquat (oxidative lung injury).
9. Hematotoxicity
  • Methemoglobinemia: Nitrites, nitrates, dapsone, benzocaine – Fe²⁺ oxidized to Fe³⁺ → hypoxia.
  • Hemolysis: G6PD deficiency triggered by oxidative drugs (primaquine, sulfonamides).
  • Bone marrow suppression: Benzene, alkylating agents, chloramphenicol (aplastic anemia).
  • Thrombocytopenia: Heparin (HIT), quinidine, gold.
  • Coagulopathy: Rodenticides (superwarfarins), salicylates.

12. Organic Solvents
  • Toluene: Euphoria, CNS depression, peripheral neuropathy (chronic), renal tubular acidosis.
  • Benzene: Bone marrow suppression, aplastic anemia, leukemia.
  • Methanol: Metabolized to formic acid → metabolic acidosis, optic neuropathy.
  • Ethylene glycol: Metabolized to oxalic acid → metabolic acidosis, renal failure, calcium oxalate crystals.
  • Carbon tetrachloride: Hepatotoxicity, nephrotoxicity (via free radicals).
13. Natural Toxins
  • Bacterial toxins: Botulinum (inhibits ACh release → flaccid paralysis); tetanospasmin (inhibits GABA/glycine release → spastic paralysis); cholera toxin (ADP‑ribosylates Gs → watery diarrhea).
  • Mycotoxins: Aflatoxin (hepatocarcinogen), ochratoxin (nephrotoxin).
  • Plant toxins: Ricin (ribosome inactivation), atropine (anticholinergic), digoxin (Na/K‑ATPase inhibition).
  • Animal venoms: Snake (neurotoxic, hemotoxic, myotoxic); scorpion; spider (e.g., latrodectus – α‑latrotoxin causes neurotransmitter release).
14. Drugs of Abuse
  • Opioids: Respiratory depression, miosis, coma. Overdose reversed by naloxone.
  • Stimulants (cocaine, amphetamines): Tachycardia, hypertension, hyperthermia, seizures, myocardial ischemia.
  • Benzodiazepines/ethanol: CNS depression, respiratory depression in overdose.
  • Hallucinogens (LSD, psilocybin): Altered perception, tachycardia, hypertension; rarely lethal.
  • Cannabinoids: Tachycardia, conjunctival injection, anxiety, hyperemesis syndrome.
