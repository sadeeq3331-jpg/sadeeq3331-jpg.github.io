1. Introduction to Musculoskeletal Pathology

Musculoskeletal pathology encompasses diseases of bones, joints, muscles, and soft tissues. Key categories include metabolic disorders, infections, inflammatory conditions, tumors, and developmental abnormalities.

  • Bone: Osteoporosis, osteomalacia, Paget's, tumors, infections.
  • Joints: Osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, gout, septic arthritis.
  • Muscles: Dystrophies, myositis, metabolic myopathies.
  • Soft tissues: Tumors (lipoma, sarcoma), fibromatoses.
2. Metabolic Bone Diseases
Osteoporosis
  • Definition: Decreased bone mass and microarchitectural deterioration → increased fracture risk.
  • Types: Primary (postmenopausal, senile), secondary (steroids, hyperparathyroidism, immobilization).
  • Pathology: Thinning of trabeculae, reduced cortical thickness; bone formation normal but resorption exceeds formation.
  • Clinical: Fractures (hip, spine, wrist), back pain, loss of height.
Osteomalacia / Rickets
  • Definition: Defective mineralization of osteoid due to vitamin D deficiency or hypophosphatemia.
  • Rickets: In children, affects growth plates → bowing, widened metaphyses.
  • Osteomalacia: In adults, osteoid accumulates (increased osteoid seams), fractures, bone pain.
  • Causes: Vitamin D deficiency (nutritional, malabsorption, renal disease), hypophosphatemia, drugs.
Hyperparathyroidism (Bone Effects)
  • Primary: Increased PTH → osteoclast activity → subperiosteal resorption (especially phalanges), brown tumors (cystic lesions), "salt‑and‑pepper" skull.
  • Secondary: Due to renal failure; may cause osteitis fibrosa cystica.

Clinical pearl: Osteoporosis = low bone mass, normal mineralization; osteomalacia = normal bone mass, defective mineralization.

3. Osteomyelitis & Bone Infections

Osteomyelitis is inflammation of bone caused by infection (pyogenic, granulomatous).

  • Pyogenic osteomyelitis: Usually Staphylococcus aureus (most common), also Strep, E. coli, Pseudomonas. Routes: hematogenous (children, long bones), contiguous spread (diabetic foot), direct inoculation (trauma, surgery).
  • Pathology: Acute inflammation, necrosis, sequestrum (dead bone), involucrum (new bone formation), draining sinus.
  • Chronic osteomyelitis: Persistent infection with sequestra, sinus tracts, may have Brodie abscess (localized abscess).
  • Tuberculous osteomyelitis: Pott disease (spine), caseating granulomas, cold abscess.

Clinical pearl: Fever, bone pain, elevated ESR/CRP, positive blood cultures. X‑ray changes appear late (10‑14 days).

4. Arthritis & Joint Diseases
Osteoarthritis (OA)
  • Degenerative joint disease: Loss of articular cartilage, eburnation, osteophyte formation, subchondral cysts.
  • Pathology: Fibrillation, erosion of cartilage; later, full‑thickness loss, bone rubbing on bone.
  • Risk factors: Age, obesity, trauma, repetitive use, genetic.
Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)
  • Chronic systemic autoimmune disease with symmetric inflammatory synovitis.
  • Pathology: Synovial hyperplasia, lymphocytic infiltrate (CD4+ T cells, plasma cells), pannus formation (granulation tissue eroding cartilage), rheumatoid nodules (subcutaneous).
  • Extra‑articular: Vasculitis, pleuritis, pericarditis, Felty syndrome (RA + splenomegaly + neutropenia).
  • Lab: Rheumatoid factor (anti‑IgG), anti‑CCP (more specific).
Gout
  • Deposition of urate crystals in joints due to hyperuricemia.
  • Pathology: Needle‑shaped monosodium urate crystals (negatively birefringent), tophi (collections of crystals in soft tissue), acute inflammation (neutrophils).
  • Stages: Asymptomatic hyperuricemia → acute gouty arthritis → intercritical periods → chronic tophaceous gout.
Pseudogout (CPPD)
  • Calcium pyrophosphate dihydrate crystals (rhomboid, weakly positive birefringence).
  • Associated with aging, hyperparathyroidism, hemochromatosis.
Septic Arthritis
  • Bacterial infection (gonococcal, staph, strep) → acute monoarthritis, purulent synovial fluid.
Ankylosing Spondylitis
  • Seronegative spondyloarthropathy, HLA‑B27 associated. Sacroiliitis, syndesmophytes (bamboo spine).
5. Muscular Dystrophies & Myopathies
Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD)
  • X‑linked recessive, mutation in dystrophin gene → absent dystrophin.
  • Pathology: Muscle fibers show necrosis, regeneration, fiber size variation, endomysial fibrosis, fatty infiltration. Dystrophin stain negative.
  • Clinical: Onset age 2‑5, proximal weakness, calf pseudohypertrophy, Gower sign, cardiomyopathy, death by 20s (respiratory/cardiac).
Becker Muscular Dystrophy (BMD)
  • Milder, dystrophin reduced/abnormal, onset later, slower progression.
Myotonic Dystrophy (DM1)
  • Autosomal dominant, CTG repeat in DMPK gene. Myotonia, distal weakness, cataracts, cardiac conduction defects, frontal balding, insulin resistance.
Limb‑Girdle Muscular Dystrophies
  • Heterogeneous group, autosomal recessive/dominant, mutations in sarcoglycans, dysferlin, etc.
Facioscapulohumeral Dystrophy (FSHD)
  • Autosomal dominant, facial and scapular weakness.
6. Myositis & Muscle Inflammation
Polymyositis
  • Cell‑mediated autoimmune attack on muscle fibers; proximal weakness, elevated CK. Pathology: CD8+ T cells invading non‑necrotic fibers.
Dermatomyositis
  • Humoral immune process with complement deposition; characteristic rash (heliotrope, Gottron papules). Pathology: perifascicular atrophy, B cell infiltrate, MAC deposition.
Inclusion Body Myositis
  • Most common in older men; distal and proximal weakness, vacuoles with inclusions (rimmed vacuoles). Refractory to therapy.
7. Bone Tumors

Bone tumors are classified by cell origin (bone‑forming, cartilage‑forming, fibrous, etc.).

Bone‑formingOsteoid osteoma, osteoblastomaOsteosarcoma
Cartilage‑formingOsteochondroma, enchondroma, chondroblastomaChondrosarcoma
FibrousFibrous cortical defect, non‑ossifying fibromaMalignant fibrous histiocytoma
Giant cell tumorGiant cell tumor (may be locally aggressive)Malignant GCT
Marrow tumors—Ewing sarcoma, multiple myeloma, lymphoma
Osteosarcoma
  • Most common primary malignant bone tumor (excluding myeloma). Occurs in metaphysis of long bones (distal femur, proximal tibia). Peak incidence: adolescents (growth spurt) and elderly (Paget's, radiation). Pathology: malignant spindle cells producing osteoid.
Ewing Sarcoma
  • Primitive neuroectodermal tumor; t(11;22) translocation (EWS‑FLI1). Diaphysis, onion‑skin periosteal reaction. Small blue round cells, PAS+ (glycogen).
Osteochondroma
  • Most common benign bone tumor; cartilage‑capped exostosis. Can be multiple in hereditary multiple exostoses.

Clinical pearl: Osteoid osteoma causes night pain relieved by NSAIDs; has a radiolucent nidus.

8. Soft Tissue Tumors
  • Lipoma: Benign adipose tumor; common.
  • Liposarcoma: Malignant; myxoid, pleomorphic, well‑differentiated.
  • Leiomyosarcoma: Smooth muscle origin.
  • Rhabdomyosarcoma: Skeletal muscle malignancy; embryonal (children), alveolar (worse prognosis, PAX‑FOXO1 fusion), pleomorphic (adults).
  • Fibrosarcoma: Malignant fibroblasts.
  • Malignant fibrous histiocytoma (undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma): Older adults, extremities.
  • Synovial sarcoma: t(X;18) translocation; biphasic (epithelial and spindle cells).
  • Giant cell tumor of tendon sheath (nodular tenosynovitis): Benign, hemosiderin‑laden cells.
9. Paget's Disease of Bone
  • Disorder of excessive bone remodeling (increased osteoclast and osteoblast activity).
  • Etiology: Possibly viral (paramyxovirus) + genetic susceptibility (SQSTM1 mutations).
  • Pathology: Mosaic pattern of lamellar bone (cement lines), osteoclasts large with many nuclei.
  • Stages: Lytic (osteoclastic) → mixed → sclerotic (osteoblastic).
  • Clinical: Bone pain, deformity, fractures, increased head size, deafness, high‑output heart failure (rare). Elevated alkaline phosphatase, normal calcium.
  • Complications: Osteosarcoma (rare, 1%).
10. Developmental & Pediatric Conditions
  • Osteogenesis imperfecta: Collagen type I mutation (COL1A1, COL1A2). Blue sclerae, brittle bones, hearing loss, dentinogenesis imperfecta.
  • Achondroplasia: FGFR3 mutation; rhizomelic dwarfism, normal trunk, frontal bossing.
  • Marfan syndrome: FBN1 mutation (fibrillin). Tall, long limbs, arachnodactyly, lens dislocation, aortic root dilation.
  • Ehlers‑Danlos syndrome: Collagen defects; hypermobility, skin hyperextensibility, vascular fragility.
  • Fibrous dysplasia: GNAS mutation; fibrous replacement of bone, ground‑glass appearance on X‑ray, café‑au‑lait spots, precocious puberty (McCune‑Albright).
  • Congenital clubfoot (talipes equinovarus): Adduction, varus, equinus.
