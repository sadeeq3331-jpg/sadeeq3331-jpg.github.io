1. Introduction to Renal Pharmacology

The kidneys are essential for fluid and electrolyte balance, acid‑base regulation, and excretion of drugs. Renal pharmacology focuses on drugs that alter kidney function (diuretics), modulate renal hormones (RAAS inhibitors), and manage complications of kidney disease (anemia, mineral metabolism, uremia).

  • Diuretics increase urine output by inhibiting sodium reabsorption at specific nephron sites.
  • RAAS inhibitors lower blood pressure and slow CKD progression.
  • CKD complications require phosphate binders, erythropoiesis‑stimulating agents, and vitamin D analogs.
2. Diuretics: Overview & Sites of Action

Diuretics act on specific segments of the nephron to inhibit sodium reabsorption, leading to increased urine output. The major classes and their sites:

Loop diureticsThick ascending limb (TAL)Inhibit Na‑K‑2Cl co‑transporter (NKCC2)
ThiazidesDistal convoluted tubule (DCT)Inhibit Na‑Cl co‑transporter (NCC)

Clinical pearl: The efficacy of diuretics depends on kidney function. Loop diuretics remain effective in CKD; thiazides lose efficacy when GFR <30 mL/min.

3. Loop Diuretics

Loop diuretics are the most potent diuretics. They inhibit the Na‑K‑2Cl co‑transporter (NKCC2) in the thick ascending limb, blocking NaCl reabsorption and reducing the medullary osmotic gradient.

Examples
  • Furosemide (most common), bumetanide, torsemide, ethacrynic acid.
Pharmacokinetics
  • Rapidly absorbed orally, but bioavailability variable (furosemide 50%). Highly protein‑bound, secreted into proximal tubule via organic anion transporters.
  • Short duration (4‑6 hours).
Clinical Uses
  • Pulmonary edema, heart failure, chronic kidney disease, hypertension (add‑on), hypercalcemia (increase Ca²⁺ excretion).
Adverse Effects
  • Hypokalemia (due to increased distal Na⁺ delivery and K⁺ secretion).
  • Ototoxicity (especially ethacrynic acid, dose‑related).
  • Dehydration, hypotension, metabolic alkalosis (contraction alkalosis).
  • Hypomagnesemia, hyperuricemia (compete for uric acid secretion).

Clinical pearl: In heart failure, loop diuretics relieve congestion but do not reduce mortality. They may cause ototoxicity if given rapidly IV in high doses.

Memory aid: “Loop diuretics loop you out of K⁺ and Mg²⁺.”

4. Thiazide Diuretics

Thiazides inhibit the Na‑Cl co‑transporter (NCC) in the distal convoluted tubule. They are less potent than loop diuretics but have longer duration.

Examples
  • Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ), chlorthalidone, metolazone, indapamide.
Clinical Uses
  • Hypertension (first‑line), heart failure (mild), nephrolithiasis (reduce calcium excretion), nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (paradoxical antidiuretic effect).
Adverse Effects
  • Hypokalemia, hyponatremia, hyperuricemia, hypercalcemia (decreased excretion).
  • Hyperglycemia (impairs insulin secretion), hyperlipidemia.
  • Photosensitivity, rash.

Clinical pearl: Metolazone is effective even in CKD (still works at low GFR). Thiazides can be combined with loop diuretics for synergistic effect.

5. Potassium‑Sparing Diuretics

Potassium‑sparing diuretics act in the collecting duct to reduce K⁺ secretion. They are weak diuretics but important for K⁺ retention.

Aldosterone Antagonists
  • Spironolactone: Competitive antagonist of aldosterone. Causes gynecomastia, anti‑androgenic effects.
  • Eplerenone: Selective aldosterone antagonist; fewer endocrine effects.
ENaC Blockers
  • Amiloride, triamterene: Block epithelial sodium channels (ENaC).
Clinical Uses
  • Hypertension (especially with hypokalemia), heart failure (reduce mortality with aldosterone antagonists), ascites, Conn syndrome.
Adverse Effects
  • Hyperkalemia (major risk).
  • Spironolactone: gynecomastia, menstrual irregularities.

Clinical pearl: Avoid K⁺‑sparing diuretics in patients with renal impairment or on ACE inhibitors/ARBs due to hyperkalemia risk.

6. Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors

Acetazolamide inhibits carbonic anhydrase in the proximal tubule, reducing HCO₃⁻ reabsorption and causing alkaline diuresis.

Clinical Uses
  • Glaucoma (reduce aqueous humor production), metabolic alkalosis, altitude sickness, idiopathic intracranial hypertension.
Adverse Effects
  • Metabolic acidosis, hypokalemia, paresthesia, sulfa allergy (acetazolamide is a sulfonamide).
7. Osmotic Diuretics

Mannitol is filtered at the glomerulus but not reabsorbed, creating an osmotic force that draws water into the tubule.

Clinical Uses
  • Reduction of intracranial pressure, cerebral edema, prevention of acute kidney injury (controversial).
Adverse Effects
  • Volume overload, pulmonary edema, hyponatremia (initially), hypernatremia (later).
8. Renin‑Angiotensin‑Aldosterone System (RAAS) Inhibitors

RAAS inhibitors reduce blood pressure, slow CKD progression, and improve heart failure outcomes.

ACE Inhibitors
  • Examples: Captopril, enalapril, lisinopril, ramipril, etc.
  • Mechanism: Block conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II; also increase bradykinin.
  • Clinical uses: Hypertension, heart failure, diabetic nephropathy, post‑MI.
  • Adverse effects: Cough (due to bradykinin), angioedema, hyperkalemia, acute kidney injury (in renal artery stenosis).
Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers (ARBs)
  • Examples: Losartan, valsartan, candesartan, irbesartan.
  • Mechanism: Block AT₁ receptors.
  • Adverse effects: Similar to ACE inhibitors but no cough, lower risk of angioedema.
Direct Renin Inhibitors
  • Aliskiren: Binds renin, reducing angiotensin I formation.
  • Avoid combination with ACE inhibitors/ARBs in diabetes (increased adverse events).

Clinical pearl: ACE inhibitors are renoprotective in diabetic nephropathy; monitor potassium and renal function.

9. Drugs for Chronic Kidney Disease Complications
Phosphate Binders
  • Calcium‑based: Calcium carbonate, calcium acetate – bind phosphate in gut.
  • Non‑calcium: Sevelamer, lanthanum carbonate – avoid hypercalcemia, also lower LDL.
Erythropoiesis‑Stimulating Agents (ESA)
  • Epoetin alfa, darbepoetin alfa: Recombinant erythropoietin for anemia of CKD.
  • Side effects: hypertension, thrombosis, pure red cell aplasia (rare).
Vitamin D Analogs
  • Calcitriol, paricalcitol, doxercalciferol: Treat secondary hyperparathyroidism in CKD.
  • Monitor calcium, phosphate; risk of hypercalcemia.
Calcimimetics
  • Cinacalcet: Increases sensitivity of calcium‑sensing receptor, lowering PTH.
  • Side effects: hypocalcemia, nausea.
10. Drugs for Nephrolithiasis & Miscellaneous

Management of kidney stones depends on stone composition.

  • Calcium stones: Thiazides (reduce hypercalciuria), allopurinol (for hyperuricosuria), potassium citrate (alkalinizes urine).
  • Uric acid stones: Allopurinol, potassium citrate, high fluid intake.
  • Cystine stones: Tiopronin, penicillamine (bind cysteine), captopril (contains thiol group).
  • Struvite stones: Antibiotics, acetohydroxamic acid (urease inhibitor).

Clinical pearl: Allopurinol prevents uric acid and calcium oxalate stones; potassium citrate prevents both calcium and uric acid stones by increasing urine pH.
