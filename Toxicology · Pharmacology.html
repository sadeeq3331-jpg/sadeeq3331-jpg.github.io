1. Principles of Toxicological Pharmacology
Toxicological pharmacology focuses on the mechanisms by which xenobiotics produce toxicity and the therapeutic interventions (antidotes, supportive care) used to manage poisoning.
General Approach to the Poisoned Patient
  • Resuscitation: Airway, breathing, circulation (ABCs).
  • Risk assessment: Identify substance, dose, time, and patient factors.
  • Decontamination: Limit absorption (e.g., activated charcoal).
  • Antidote administration: Specific reversal agents.
  • Enhanced elimination: Hemodialysis, urinary alkalinization, multiple‑dose activated charcoal.

Antidote Categories

  • Functional antagonists: Reverse toxic effect (e.g., naloxone for opioids).
  • Chemical antagonists: Bind and inactivate toxin (e.g., chelators).
  • Metabolic modulators: Alter biotransformation (e.g., fomepizole).
  • Receptor blockers: e.g., atropine for organophosphates.
2. Decontamination & Enhanced Elimination
Gastrointestinal Decontamination
  • Activated charcoal: Binds many toxins; dose 1 g/kg; most effective within 1‑2 hours. Not for heavy metals, iron, lithium, alcohols.
  • Gastric lavage: Only if life‑threatening overdose within 1 hour and airway protected; rarely used.
  • Whole bowel irrigation: Polyethylene glycol for sustained‑release or iron, lithium.
Enhanced Elimination
  • Hemodialysis: For alcohols, lithium, salicylates, valproate, theophylline.
  • Urinary alkalinization: Salicylates, phenobarbital – IV bicarbonate to increase urine pH, promoting ion trapping.
  • Multiple‑dose activated charcoal: For drugs with enterohepatic recirculation (e.g., carbamazepine, theophylline, dapsone).
  • Lipid emulsion therapy: For lipophilic drug toxicity (local anesthetics, some calcium channel blockers).
3. Acetaminophen (Paracetamol)
  • Toxic mechanism: CYP2E1 metabolizes to NAPQI, depleted glutathione → hepatotoxicity.
  • Antidote: N‑acetylcysteine (NAC) – replenishes glutathione, binds NAPQI. Best if started within 8 hours, but effective later.
  • Dosing: IV or oral (loading 140 mg/kg, then 70 mg/kg q4h x 17 doses).
  • Rumack‑Matthew nomogram: Guides need for NAC based on level and time.
4. Opioids
  • Toxicity: Respiratory depression, miosis, coma.
  • Antidote: Naloxone (competitive antagonist).
  • Dosing: 0.04‑0.4 mg IV/IM/SC, repeated as needed; may require continuous infusion for long‑acting opioids.
  • Caution: can precipitate withdrawal; duration shorter than many opioids, so monitor for re‑sedation.
5. Benzodiazepines
  • Toxicity: Sedation, ataxia, respiratory depression (rare alone, synergistic with other CNS depressants).
  • Antidote: Flumazenil (competitive antagonist).
  • Indications: Reversal of procedural sedation; not routinely used in overdose due to risk of seizures (in mixed overdose or benzodiazepine‑dependent).
6. Organophosphates & Carbamates
  • Toxicity: Acetylcholinesterase inhibition → cholinergic excess (SLUDGE syndrome, muscle fasciculations, paralysis).
  • Antidote: Atropine (muscarinic antagonist) + pralidoxime (reactivates AChE).
  • Atropine: Initially 1‑2 mg IV, double dose every 5 minutes until drying of secretions.
  • Pralidoxime: 1‑2 g IV over 30 minutes, then infusion; effective if given early.
7. Cyanide
  • Toxicity: Inhibits cytochrome oxidase (complex IV), causing histotoxic hypoxia.
  • Antidote kits:
    • Nitrite‑thiosulfate: Sodium nitrite induces methemoglobinemia (binds cyanide), sodium thiosulfate converts to thiocyanate.
    • Hydroxocobalamin (Cyanokit): Directly binds cyanide to form cyanocobalamin; fewer adverse effects, first‑line.
8. Methanol & Ethylene Glycol
  • Toxicity: Metabolized by alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) to toxic acids (formic acid, oxalic acid).
  • Antidote: Fomepizole (4‑methylpyrazole) – inhibits ADH. Also ethanol infusion (competitive substrate).
  • Fomepizole dosing: 15 mg/kg load, then 10 mg/kg q12h.
  • Hemodialysis: For severe acidosis, renal failure, high levels.

10. Carbon Monoxide
  • Toxicity: Binds hemoglobin with high affinity → carboxyhemoglobin (COHb) → impaired oxygen delivery.
  • Treatment: 100% oxygen (shortens half‑life from 4‑6 hours to 90 minutes). Hyperbaric oxygen for severe poisoning (neurologic symptoms, COHb >25%).
11. Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCA)
  • Toxicity: Sodium channel blockade → QRS widening, arrhythmias; anticholinergic effects; seizures.
  • Treatment: IV sodium bicarbonate – corrects acidosis, increases serum sodium to overcome channel blockade. Goal QRS <100 ms.
  • Supportive: Benzodiazepines for seizures; lipid emulsion may be used in refractory cases.
12. Salicylates (Aspirin)
  • Toxicity: Respiratory alkalosis (early) → mixed acidosis, tinnitus, hyperthermia, pulmonary edema.
  • Treatment: Activated charcoal; urinary alkalinization (bicarbonate infusion) to enhance excretion; hemodialysis for severe toxicity (levels >100 mg/dL, acidosis, CNS symptoms).
13. Beta‑Blockers & Calcium Channel Blockers
  • Beta‑blocker toxicity: Bradycardia, hypotension, bronchospasm. Glucagon (bypasses receptor, increases cAMP) is first‑line: 5‑10 mg IV.
  • CCB toxicity: Bradycardia, hypotension, hyperglycemia. Treatment: IV calcium (calcium gluconate/chloride), high‑dose insulin euglycemia therapy (insulin + dextrose).
  • Lipid emulsion may be used for refractory cases.

15. Supportive Care & Monitoring
  • ABCs: Intubation if airway compromised, mechanical ventilation.
  • Cardiovascular: IV fluids, vasopressors (norepinephrine) for hypotension.
  • Seizures: Benzodiazepines first‑line; barbiturates if refractory.
  • Hypothermia/hyperthermia: Temperature control (antipyretics ineffective in drug‑induced hyperthermia; cooling measures).
  • Monitoring: ECG, pulse oximetry, end‑tidal CO₂, serial labs (electrolytes, drug levels, liver/renal function).
