1. Introduction to Research
Medical research is systematic investigation to generate knowledge about health and disease. It can be:
  • Basic (laboratory) research: Studies mechanisms at cellular/molecular level.
  • Translational research: Applies basic science to clinical practice (bench to bedside).
  • Clinical research: Studies involving human subjects (patients or volunteers).
  • Epidemiological research: Studies patterns, causes, and effects of health in populations.
The research cycle: Question → Hypothesis → Study design → Data collection → Analysis → Interpretation → Dissemination.
2. Research Question & Hypothesis
A good research question should be FINER: Feasible, Interesting, Novel, Ethical, Relevant.
  • PICO format: Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome (common in clinical studies).
  • Hypothesis: A testable statement predicting the relationship between variables.
    • Null hypothesis (H₀): No effect or difference.
    • Alternative hypothesis (H₁): There is an effect or difference.
3. Variables & Measurement
A variable is any characteristic that can vary among subjects.
  • Types of variables:
    • Quantitative (numeric): Continuous (height, weight) vs discrete (number of children).
    • Categorical (qualitative): Nominal (gender, blood type) vs ordinal (stage of cancer).
  • Scales of measurement: Nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio.
  • Independent variable: Manipulated or predictor.
  • Dependent variable: Outcome measured.

5. Experimental Studies (Randomized Controlled Trials)
In experimental studies, the investigator assigns interventions.
  • Randomized controlled trial (RCT): Participants randomly assigned to intervention or control group. Gold standard for efficacy.
  • Key features:
    • Randomization: Reduces selection bias, balances known and unknown confounders.
    • Blinding (masking): Single‑blind (participant), double‑blind (participant and investigator), triple‑blind (also analyst).
    • Placebo control: To control for placebo effect.
    • Intention‑to‑treat (ITT): Analyze participants in the groups they were randomized to, regardless of adherence. Preserves randomization.
    • Per‑protocol analysis: Only those who completed the protocol; may introduce bias.
6. Bias & Confounding
Bias (Systematic Error)
  • Selection bias: Systematic differences between groups (e.g., Berkson bias, non‑response bias).
  • Information bias: Errors in measurement (recall bias, interviewer bias, misclassification).
  • Lead‑time bias: Early detection falsely prolongs survival.
  • Length‑time bias: Overrepresentation of less aggressive disease in screening.
Confounding
  • A confounder is a variable associated with both exposure and outcome, and not on the causal pathway.
  • Example: age confounds the relationship between gray hair and heart disease (age causes both).
  • Control: Randomization, restriction, matching, stratification, multivariable analysis.
7. Descriptive Statistics
Measures of Central Tendency
  • Mean: Average (sensitive to outliers).
  • Median: Middle value (robust to outliers).
  • Mode: Most frequent value.
Measures of Dispersion
  • Range: Max – min.
  • Interquartile range (IQR): 75th percentile – 25th percentile.
  • Variance: Average squared deviation from mean.
  • Standard deviation (SD): Square root of variance. Describes spread in same units as data.
  • Standard error of the mean (SEM): SD/√n. Measures precision of sample mean.
8. Probability & Distributions
  • Normal (Gaussian) distribution: Bell‑shaped, symmetric; mean = median = mode. 68% within 1 SD, 95% within 2 SD.
  • Skewed distributions: Positive (right) tail, negative (left) tail.
  • Binomial distribution: Probability of binary outcomes (success/failure).
  • Poisson distribution: Rare events (counts).
9. Inferential Statistics
Inferential statistics draw conclusions about a population from a sample.
  • Estimation: Point estimate (e.g., sample mean) and confidence interval (CI) (range likely to contain true population value). 95% CI means if repeated samples taken, 95% of CIs would contain true value.
  • Hypothesis testing: Determine if observed difference is real or due to chance.
10. Hypothesis Testing
  • Null hypothesis (H₀): No effect.
  • Alternative hypothesis (H₁): There is an effect.
  • Type I error (α): Reject H₀ when true (false positive). Usually set at 0.05.
  • Type II error (β): Fail to reject H₀ when false (false negative). Power = 1 – β.
  • p‑value: Probability of observing data as extreme as or more extreme than what was observed, assuming H₀ is true. If p < α, reject H₀.
  • Statistically significant: p < 0.05 (conventional).
Common tests:
  • t‑test: compares means between two groups.
  • ANOVA: compares means among ≥3 groups.
  • Chi‑square: tests association between categorical variables.
  • Pearson correlation: measures linear relationship between continuous variables.
  • Regression: models relationship (linear, logistic).
11. Sample Size & Power
  • Power (1 – β): Probability of detecting an effect if it truly exists. Desired power usually ≥80%.
  • Sample size depends on: desired power, significance level (α), effect size, variability.
  • Larger sample size increases power and precision.
12. Research Ethics
  • Belmont Report principles: Respect for persons (autonomy, informed consent), beneficence (maximize benefits, minimize harm), justice (fair selection).
  • Informed consent: Must be voluntary, informed, and from a competent person.
  • Institutional Review Board (IRB) / Research Ethics Committee: Reviews research protocols to protect human subjects.
  • Declaration of Helsinki: Ethical principles for medical research involving human subjects.
  • Good Clinical Practice (GCP): International quality standard for conducting trials.
13. Reporting Guidelines
  • CONSORT: For RCTs.
  • STROBE: For observational studies.
  • PRISMA: For systematic reviews and meta‑analyses.
  • STARD: For diagnostic accuracy studies.
  • ARRIVE: For animal studies.
