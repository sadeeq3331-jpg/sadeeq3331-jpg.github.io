1. t‑Tests
t‑tests compare means between groups. Assumes normal distribution and equal variances (for independent t‑test).
  • One‑sample t‑test: Compares sample mean to a known population mean.
  • Independent (two‑sample) t‑test: Compares means between two independent groups.
  • Paired t‑test: Compares means from same subjects at two time points (e.g., before‑after).
2. ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)
  • One‑way ANOVA: Compares means among three or more independent groups.
  • Two‑way ANOVA: Examines effect of two factors (and their interaction).
  • Repeated measures ANOVA: Same subjects measured multiple times.
  • Post‑hoc tests (e.g., Tukey) are used after significant ANOVA to identify which groups differ.
3. Correlation (Pearson)
  • Pearson's r measures linear relationship between two continuous variables.
  • Range: -1 to +1. 0 = no linear correlation.
  • Assumes normality and homoscedasticity.
4. Mann‑Whitney U Test
  • Non‑parametric alternative to independent t‑test.
  • Compares medians or distributions of two independent groups.
  • Does not assume normality; uses ranks.
5. Wilcoxon Signed‑Rank Test
  • Non‑parametric alternative to paired t‑test.
  • Compares two related samples (e.g., before‑after).
6. Kruskal‑Wallis Test
  • Non‑parametric alternative to one‑way ANOVA.
  • Compares medians of three or more independent groups.
7. Spearman's Rank Correlation
  • Non‑parametric measure of monotonic association between two variables.
  • Based on ranks, not actual values.
8. Chi‑Square Test
  • Chi‑square test of independence: Tests association between two categorical variables (e.g., treatment and outcome).
  • Chi‑square goodness‑of‑fit: Compares observed frequencies to expected.
  • Requires expected cell count ≥5 in each cell.
9. Fisher's Exact Test
  • Used for 2×2 tables when expected cell count <5.
  • Exact p‑value (no approximation).
10. McNemar's Test
  • Compares paired categorical data (e.g., before‑after on same subjects).
  • Example: comparing diagnostic tests on same patients.
11. Linear Regression
  • Models relationship between continuous outcome and one or more predictors.
  • Equation: Y = β₀ + β₁X₁ + ... + ε.
  • R²: proportion of variance explained by model.
  • Assumptions: linearity, independence, homoscedasticity, normality of residuals.
12. Logistic Regression
  • Models binary outcome (e.g., disease yes/no).
  • Odds ratios (OR) are exponentiated coefficients.
  • No assumption of normality; maximum likelihood estimation.
13. Cox Proportional Hazards
  • Used for time‑to‑event (survival) data.
  • Hazard ratio (HR) = exp(coefficient).
  • Assumes proportional hazards over time.
14. P‑Value Interpretation
  • P‑value is the probability of obtaining results as extreme as observed, assuming null hypothesis is true.
  • P < 0.05 is conventionally "statistically significant".
  • P‑value does NOT measure magnitude of effect or clinical importance.
  • Misinterpretations: It is not the probability that null is true, nor the probability that chance alone produced the result.
15. Confidence Intervals (CI)
  • 95% CI means if study repeated many times, 95% of intervals would contain true population value.
  • CI provides range of plausible effect sizes and precision.
  • If 95% CI does not cross null value (0 for difference, 1 for ratio), p < 0.05.
16. Clinical vs Statistical Significance
  • Statistical significance: P < 0.05 (or CI excludes null). Can occur with very large sample sizes even for trivial effects.
  • Clinical significance: Effect size large enough to matter in practice (e.g., NNT low).
  • Both should be considered together.
