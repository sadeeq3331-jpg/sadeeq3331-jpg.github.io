1. Introduction to Musculoskeletal System

The musculoskeletal system provides support, movement, and protection. It consists of bones, muscles, joints, cartilage, tendons, and ligaments. It also serves as a reservoir for minerals (calcium, phosphate) and produces blood cells (bone marrow).

  • Bones: structural support, mineral storage, hematopoiesis.
  • Muscles: movement, posture, heat production.
  • Joints: allow articulation and movement.
2. Bone Structure & Physiology

Bone is a dynamic tissue composed of cells (osteoblasts, osteocytes, osteoclasts) and extracellular matrix (collagen, hydroxyapatite).

Bone Cells
  • Osteoblasts: Form bone by secreting osteoid (unmineralized matrix). Rich in alkaline phosphatase.
  • Osteocytes: Mature osteoblasts trapped in matrix; sense mechanical load.
  • Osteoclasts: Multinucleated cells derived from monocytes; resorb bone (via H⁺ and enzymes).
Bone Matrix
  • Organic: Type I collagen (90%), proteoglycans, osteocalcin, osteonectin.
  • Inorganic: Hydroxyapatite (calcium phosphate crystals) provides hardness.

Clinical pearl: Alkaline phosphatase is a marker of osteoblast activity (elevated in Paget's disease, bone metastases).

3. Skeletal Muscle Contraction

Muscle contraction is based on the sliding filament mechanism. Sarcomeres (Z‑line to Z‑line) contain actin (thin) and myosin (thick) filaments.

Excitation‑Contraction Coupling
  • Action potential travels along sarcolemma and down T‑tubules.
  • Depolarization activates voltage‑gated L‑type Ca²⁺ channels (dihydropyridine receptors) which mechanically open ryanodine receptors on sarcoplasmic reticulum → Ca²⁺ release.
  • Ca²⁺ binds to troponin C, causing tropomyosin to move, exposing myosin‑binding sites on actin.
  • Myosin heads bind actin, undergo power stroke (using ATP), and slide filaments.
Cross‑bridge cycle: Attachment → power stroke → detachment → cocking.

Clinical pearl: Rigor mortis occurs after death due to lack of ATP, preventing myosin detachment.

Memory aid: "Ca²⁺ triggers the troponin‑tropomyosin shift."

4. Neuromuscular Junction

The neuromuscular junction (NMJ) is a synapse between a motor neuron and a muscle fiber.

  • Action potential in motor neuron opens voltage‑gated Ca²⁺ channels → ACh release.
  • ACh binds nicotinic receptors on motor end plate → Na⁺ influx → end‑plate potential → muscle action potential.
  • ACh is degraded by acetylcholinesterase in synaptic cleft.

Clinical pearl: Myasthenia gravis: autoantibodies against nicotinic ACh receptors → weakness. Lambert‑Eaton syndrome: antibodies against presynaptic Ca²⁺ channels.

6. Joint Physiology

Joints are classified structurally (fibrous, cartilaginous, synovial) and functionally (synarthrosis, amphiarthrosis, diarthrosis).

  • Synovial joints: Freely movable; contain synovial fluid (ultrafiltrate of plasma with hyaluronan) for lubrication and nutrient delivery.
  • Articular cartilage: Hyaline cartilage; avascular, receives nutrition from synovial fluid.
  • Ligaments & tendons: Dense connective tissue; ligaments connect bone to bone, tendons connect muscle to bone.
7. Bone Growth & Remodeling

Bone growth occurs via endochondral ossification (long bones) and intramembranous ossification (flat bones). Remodeling (bone turnover) is a lifelong process involving osteoclasts and osteoblasts, regulated by mechanical stress and hormones (PTH, calcitonin, vitamin D, estrogen).

  • Ossification centers: Primary (diaphysis) and secondary (epiphysis). Epiphyseal plates allow longitudinal growth until puberty.
  • Wolff's law: Bone remodels in response to mechanical stress.
8. Calcium & Phosphate Homeostasis

Calcium is essential for bone mineralization, muscle contraction, nerve transmission, and blood clotting. Regulated by PTH, vitamin D, and calcitonin.

9. Exercise Physiology

Exercise stresses muscles, cardiovascular, and respiratory systems. Adaptations include increased muscle mass (hypertrophy), improved oxygen delivery, and metabolic changes.

  • Acute responses: ↑ HR, ↑ BP, ↑ ventilation, ↑ lactate.
  • Chronic adaptations: Muscle fiber type shifts, mitochondrial biogenesis, capillary density ↑, bone density ↑.
10. Aging & Musculoskeletal System

Aging leads to sarcopenia (loss of muscle mass and strength), osteoporosis (bone loss), and osteoarthritis (degenerative joint disease).

  • Sarcopenia: Decreased number and size of type II fibers; reduced satellite cell activity.
  • Osteoporosis: Imbalance favoring bone resorption; increased fracture risk.
  • Osteoarthritis: Degeneration of articular cartilage, osteophytes, joint space narrowing.
