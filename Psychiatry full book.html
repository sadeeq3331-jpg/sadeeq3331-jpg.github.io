Key Brain Regions
  • Prefrontal cortex: executive function, personality; hypoactive in schizophrenia, ADHD.
  • Amygdala: fear, emotional memory; hyperactive in anxiety, PTSD.
  • Hippocampus: memory; volume loss in PTSD, depression.
  • Nucleus accumbens: reward; dysregulated in addiction, anhedonia.
  • Hypothalamus: HPA axis; hypercortisolemia in depression.
Memory aid: “Dopamine = motivation & psychosis; Serotonin = mood & appetite; Norepinephrine = arousal & attention; GABA = calm; Glutamate = excite.”
2. General Psychopharmacology

Principles of pharmacokinetics (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion) and pharmacodynamics (receptor binding, efficacy, potency). Most psychotropic drugs are lipophilic, highly protein‑bound, and metabolized by CYP450 enzymes. Genetic polymorphisms (e.g., CYP2D6, CYP2C19) affect drug levels and response.

Receptor concepts:
  • Agonist – binds and activates
  • Antagonist – binds but does not activate; blocks endogenous ligand
  • Partial agonist – submaximal activation even at full occupancy
  • Inverse agonist – reduces baseline activity
3. Schizophrenia Spectrum
Schizophrenia: DSM‑5 Criteria
≥2 of the following for ≥1 month (at least one must be 1‑3):
  1. Delusions
  2. Hallucinations (usually auditory)
  3. Disorganized speech
  4. Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior
  5. Negative symptoms (avolition, flat affect, alogia)
Continuous signs for ≥6 months; social/occupational dysfunction.
Pathophysiology
  • Dopamine hypothesis: Mesolimbic hyperactivity (positive symptoms), mesocortical hypoactivity (negative/cognitive).
  • Glutamate hypothesis: NMDA receptor hypofunction (e.g., from PCP) leads to disinhibition of glutamate release.
  • Neuropathology: Enlarged ventricles, reduced cortical thickness, no gliosis (developmental origin).

Pearl: Antipsychotics block D2 receptors; typical antipsychotics cause EPS, atypical have lower EPS but metabolic syndrome.

4. Depressive Disorders
Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)
≥5 of the following for ≥2 weeks (with depressed mood or anhedonia):
  • Depressed mood
  • Anhedonia
  • Weight/appetite change
  • Sleep disturbance
  • Psychomotor agitation/retardation
  • Fatigue
  • Worthlessness/guilt
  • Poor concentration
  • Suicidal thoughts
Neurobiology
  • Monoamine hypothesis: Low 5‑HT, NE, DA.
  • HPA axis: Hypercortisolemia, impaired feedback (DST non‑suppression).
  • Neurotrophic hypothesis: ↓ BDNF, hippocampal atrophy; antidepressants ↑ BDNF.
SIGECAPS: Sleep, Interest, Guilt, Energy, Concentration, Appetite, Psychomotor, Suicidal.

6. Anxiety Disorders
Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Excessive worry ≥6 months, plus ≥3 of: restlessness, fatigue, concentration difficulty, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance.

Panic Disorder

Recurrent unexpected panic attacks (≥4 symptoms: palpitations, sweating, trembling, SOB, chest pain, dizziness, etc.) plus ≥1 month of worry about more attacks or change in behavior.

Social Anxiety Disorder

Marked fear of social situations involving scrutiny; avoidance.

Specific Phobia

Marked fear of a specific object/situation; immediate anxiety; avoidance.

7. OCD & Related Disorders

Obsessions: recurrent intrusive thoughts/images/urges.

Compulsions: repetitive behaviors or mental acts performed in response to obsessions.

Body Dysmorphic Disorder: preoccupation with perceived flaws in appearance.

Hoarding Disorder: persistent difficulty discarding possessions, regardless of value.

8. Trauma & Stressor‑Related
PTSD

Exposure to trauma; symptoms for >1 month: intrusion, avoidance, negative mood/cognition, hyperarousal.

Acute Stress Disorder

Symptoms 3 days – 1 month.

Adjustment Disorders

Emotional/behavioral symptoms in response to identifiable stressor within 3 months, resolve within 6 months after stressor ends.

9. Somatic Symptom Disorders
  • Somatic symptom disorder: one or more distressing somatic symptoms + excessive thoughts/behaviors.
  • Illness anxiety disorder: preoccupation with having a serious illness; few or no somatic symptoms.
  • Conversion disorder: neurological symptoms incompatible with medical condition.
  • Factitious disorder: intentional production of symptoms to assume sick role (no external incentive). Malingering has external incentive.
10. Dissociative Disorders
  • Dissociative identity disorder: two or more distinct personality states; gaps in recall.
  • Depersonalization/derealization disorder: feeling detached from self or world.
  • Dissociative amnesia: inability to recall important autobiographical information, usually traumatic.
11. Eating Disorders
Anorexia Nervosa
  • Restriction of energy intake leading to significantly low body weight.
  • Intense fear of gaining weight, disturbance in body image.
  • Subtypes: restricting, binge‑eating/purging.
Bulimia Nervosa
  • Recurrent binge eating + compensatory behaviors (purging, fasting, exercise) ≥1/week for 3 months.
  • Self‑evaluation unduly influenced by weight/shape.
Binge‑Eating Disorder
  • Binge eating without regular compensatory behaviors.
12. Sleep Disorders
  • Insomnia disorder: difficulty initiating/maintaining sleep ≥3 nights/week for ≥3 months.
  • Narcolepsy: recurrent need for sleep, cataplexy, hypnagogic hallucinations, sleep paralysis; loss of orexin neurons.
  • Obstructive sleep apnea: repeated airway collapse, snoring, daytime sleepiness.

15. Substance Use Disorders

DSM‑5 criteria (≥2 in 12 months): taken in larger amounts, desire to cut down, time spent, craving, failure to fulfill roles, social problems, hazardous use, physical/psychological problems, tolerance, withdrawal.

Alcohol
  • Intoxication: slurred speech, incoordination, stupor.
  • Withdrawal: tremor, anxiety, seizures, DTs (48‑96h after cessation).
  • Wernicke‑Korsakoff: thiamine deficiency; triad (confusion, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia) + amnesia.
Opioids
  • Intoxication: euphoria, miosis, respiratory depression.
  • Withdrawal: dysphoria, nausea, diarrhea, yawning, piloerection (not life‑threatening).
Stimulants (cocaine, amphetamines)
  • Intoxication: euphoria, hypertension, tachycardia, pupillary dilation, seizures.
  • Withdrawal: dysphoria, fatigue, increased appetite.
16. Child & Adolescent Psychiatry
Autism Spectrum Disorder
  • Persistent deficits in social communication/interaction.
  • Restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities.
  • Symptoms present in early developmental period.
ADHD

≥6 symptoms of inattention and/or hyperactivity‑impulsivity for ≥6 months, before age 12, in ≥2 settings.

Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD)

Angry/irritable mood, argumentative/defiant behavior, vindictiveness.

Conduct Disorder (CD)

Aggression, destruction of property, deceitfulness, rule violations.

20. Anxiolytics & Hypnotics
  • Benzodiazepines: enhance GABAA (increase Cl⁻ channel frequency). Uses: anxiety, panic, seizures, alcohol withdrawal. Side effects: sedation, tolerance, dependence, withdrawal, anterograde amnesia.
  • Buspirone: 5‑HT1A partial agonist; GAD, no abuse potential, delayed onset.
  • Z‑drugs (zolpidem, eszopiclone): selective at α1 subunit; for insomnia, risk of parasomnias.
21. ADHD Medications
  • Stimulants: methylphenidate (DA/NE reuptake inhibitor), amphetamine salts (increase release). Side effects: insomnia, decreased appetite, growth delay, tachycardia.
  • Atomoxetine: selective NE reuptake inhibitor; no abuse potential; hepatotoxicity risk.
  • α2‑agonists: clonidine, guanfacine – reduce hyperactivity/impulsivity; sedation, hypotension.
