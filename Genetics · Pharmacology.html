1. Introduction to Pharmacogenetics
Pharmacogenetics studies how genetic variation affects drug response (efficacy and toxicity). Pharmacogenomics is the broader genome‑wide approach.
  • Variations in genes encoding drug‑metabolizing enzymes, transporters, or targets can alter pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics.
  • Single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), insertions/deletions, copy number variants, and repeat expansions contribute.
  • Goal: personalized medicine – selecting the right drug and dose based on genotype.
2. Cytochrome P450 Polymorphisms
CYP enzymes are major Phase I drug metabolizers. Many are polymorphic, leading to poor, intermediate, extensive, or ultrarapid metabolizer phenotypes.

4. Drug Transporters
  • P‑glycoprotein (ABCB1, MDR1): Efflux transporter; affects bioavailability of digoxin, tacrolimus, HIV protease inhibitors. Polymorphisms (e.g., C3435T) may alter expression.
  • OATP1B1 (SLCO1B1): Hepatic uptake of statins (simvastatin). *5 allele reduces function → ↑ statin levels → myopathy risk.
  • OCT2 (SLC22A2): Renal uptake of metformin; variants affect metformin disposition.
5. Drug Targets/Receptors
  • β‑adrenergic receptor (ADRB1): Arg389Gly variant alters response to β‑blockers.
  • Serotonin transporter (SLC6A4): 5‑HTTLPR promoter polymorphism affects SSRI response.
  • Vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKORC1): Promoter haplotype influences warfarin dose requirement.
6. Warfarin Pharmacogenetics
  • CYP2C9 variants (*2, *3) reduce S‑warfarin clearance → lower dose needed.
  • VKORC1 promoter polymorphisms (−1639G>A) affect warfarin sensitivity; A allele → lower dose.
  • Algorithms (e.g., IWPC) combine genotype, age, weight to predict therapeutic dose; FDA suggests considering genotype.
7. Clopidogrel
  • Clopidogrel is a prodrug activated by CYP2C19.
  • Carriers of loss‑of‑function alleles (CYP2C19*2, *3) have reduced active metabolite → higher risk of stent thrombosis and cardiovascular events.
  • Alternative antiplatelets (prasugrel, ticagrelor) may be used in poor metabolizers.
8. Codeine and Opioids
  • Codeine is O‑demethylated by CYP2D6 to morphine (active).
  • Poor metabolizers (CYP2D6 loss) – little analgesia.
  • Ultrarapid metabolizers (duplications) – rapid conversion → morphine toxicity (respiratory depression, deaths in children post‑tonsillectomy). FDA black box warning.
  • Similar issues with tramadol, hydrocodone.
9. Thiopurines (6‑MP, Azathioprine)
  • Metabolism by TPMT and NUDT15.
  • TPMT deficiency leads to accumulation of thioguanine nucleotides → severe myelosuppression.
  • NUDT15 variants (common in Asians) also increase toxicity.
  • Pre‑emptive genotyping allows dose reduction (starting dose 10% of normal in TPMT deficiency).
10. Irinotecan
  • Converted to active SN‑38, glucuronidated by UGT1A1.
  • UGT1A1*28 (TA7 promoter) reduces expression → ↑ SN‑38 → ↑ neutropenia/diarrhea.
  • FDA recommends lower starting dose in homozygotes.
11. Abacavir Hypersensitivity
  • Abacavir (HIV drug) causes severe hypersensitivity reaction in HLA‑B*5701 carriers.
  • Screening for HLA‑B*5701 is mandatory before initiation; if positive, avoid abacavir.
  • Almost 100% negative predictive value.

13. Genetic Testing in Practice
  • Pre‑emptive panel testing (e.g., pharmacogenomics array) vs. reactive single‑gene testing.
  • Clinical Pharmacogenetics Implementation Consortium (CPIC) provides dosing guidelines.
  • FDA drug labels increasingly include pharmacogenetic information.
  • Examples: CYP2C19 for clopidogrel, TPMT for thiopurines, UGT1A1 for irinotecan.
14. Ethical & Legal Issues
  • Informed consent, return of results, privacy (GINA – Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act).
  • Ethnic disparities in allele frequencies.
  • Cost‑effectiveness and implementation challenges.
