1. Perinatal Asphyxia
Definition: Impairment of gas exchange leading to hypoxemia, hypercapnia, and metabolic acidosis. May result in hypoxic‑ischemic encephalopathy (HIE).
  • Causes: Umbilical cord compression, placental abruption, maternal hypotension, prolonged labor.
  • Pathology: Selective neuronal necrosis (hippocampus, Purkinje cells, basal ganglia), cerebral edema, infarction.
  • Outcome: Long‑term neurologic deficits, cerebral palsy, epilepsy.

3. Necrotizing Enterocolitis (NEC)
Definition: Ischemic necrosis of intestinal mucosa, mainly in preterm infants. Associated with formula feeding, bacterial colonization, hypoxia.
  • Pathology: Coagulative necrosis, pneumatosis intestinalis (gas in bowel wall), perforation, peritonitis.
  • Clinical: Abdominal distension, bloody stools, lethargy; X‑ray shows portal venous gas, pneumoperitoneum if perforated.
4. Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)
Cause: Surfactant deficiency (type II pneumocyte immaturity) in preterm infants. Hyaline membranes line alveoli (hyaline membrane disease).
  • Pathology: Atelectasis, eosinophilic hyaline membranes, capillary congestion.
  • Prevention: Antenatal corticosteroids accelerate surfactant production.
  • Treatment: Exogenous surfactant, CPAP.
5. Bronchopulmonary Dysplasia (BPD)
Chronic lung disease of prematurity due to oxygen toxicity, barotrauma, inflammation. Pathology: alveolar simplification, fibrosis, smooth muscle hypertrophy.
6. Retinopathy of Prematurity (ROP)
Abnormal retinal vascular development in preterm infants exposed to high oxygen. Vaso‑obliteration followed by neovascularization. Can lead to retinal detachment and blindness.

8. Gastrointestinal Anomalies
  • Tracheoesophageal fistula (TEF): Esophageal atresia with distal fistula → polyhydramnios, coughing/cyanosis with feeding.
  • Pyloric stenosis: Hypertrophy of pyloric muscle → projectile vomiting at 3‑6 weeks, olive‑shaped mass.
  • Duodenal atresia: Double bubble sign on X‑ray; associated with Down syndrome.
  • Hirschsprung disease: Aganglionosis of distal colon → failure to pass meconium, explosive stools after rectal exam.
  • Meckel diverticulum: Persistent vitelline duct; rule of 2s (2% population, 2 inches long, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2 types of ectopic tissue (gastric, pancreatic), presents by age 2).
9. Renal & Urinary Tract Anomalies
  • Renal agenesis: Unilateral (often asymptomatic) or bilateral (Potter sequence – oligohydramnios, pulmonary hypoplasia, characteristic facies).
  • Multicystic dysplastic kidney: Non‑functional cystic kidney; usually unilateral.
  • Polycystic kidney disease: Autosomal recessive (infantile) – enlarged cystic kidneys, hepatic fibrosis; autosomal dominant (adult) – later onset.
  • Posterior urethral valves: Obstructive uropathy in males → hydronephrosis, bladder wall thickening.
10. CNS Anomalies
  • Neural tube defects: Spina bifida (meningocele, myelomeningocele), anencephaly. Folic acid deficiency prevention.
  • Hydrocephalus: Impaired CSF circulation; aqueductal stenosis (X‑linked), Dandy‑Walker malformation.
  • Holoprosencephaly: Failure of forebrain division; associated with trisomy 13, maternal diabetes.
11. Cleft Lip & Palate
Failure of fusion of maxillary and medial nasal processes (lip) or palatal shelves (palate). Syndromic or isolated. Associated with Pierre Robin sequence (cleft palate, micrognathia, glossoptosis).

13. Meningitis
  • Neonates: Group B Streptococcus, E. coli, Listeria monocytogenes.
  • Infants/children: S. pneumoniae, Neisseria meningitidis, H. influenzae type b (vaccine has reduced incidence).
  • Pathology: Purulent exudate in subarachnoid space; complications: hydrocephalus, hearing loss, brain infarction.
14. Pneumonia
  • Neonatal: Group B Strep, E. coli (early onset); Chlamydia trachomatis (late onset, afebrile, staccato cough).
  • Infants/children: Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV – bronchiolitis), S. pneumoniae, Mycoplasma pneumoniae (older children).
15. Otitis Media
Common in infants due to short, horizontal Eustachian tube. Organisms: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, M. catarrhalis. Pathology: middle ear effusion, inflammation, possible perforation.
16. Viral Exanthems
  • Measles (rubeola): Prodrome (cough, coryza, conjunctivitis), Koplik spots, maculopapular rash head to toe.
  • Rubella (German measles): Mild prodrome, postauricular lymphadenopathy, rash spreads quickly.
  • Varicella (chickenpox): Vesicular rash (“dew drops on a rose petal”), pruritic, may become secondarily infected.
  • Erythema infectiosum (fifth disease): Parvovirus B19; slapped cheek rash, lacy reticular rash on extremities.
  • Roseola infantum (sixth disease): HHV‑6; high fever then rash after defervescence.
17. Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)
  • Features: Hypotonia, flat facies, upslanting palpebral fissures, simian crease, Brushfield spots, duodenal atresia, AVSD.
  • Risk: Advanced maternal age.
  • Complications: Intellectual disability, Alzheimer disease (by 40‑50 years), leukemia (AML, ALL), atlantoaxial instability.
18. Turner Syndrome (45,X)
  • Features: Short stature, webbed neck, low posterior hairline, broad chest (shield chest), cubitus valgus, lymphedema of hands/feet in newborn.
  • Complications: Coarctation of aorta, bicuspid aortic valve, ovarian dysgenesis (streak ovaries), infertility, normal intelligence.
19. Klinefelter Syndrome (47,XXY)
  • Features: Tall stature, hypogonadism, small testes, gynecomastia, decreased facial/body hair.
  • Complications: Infertility, learning disabilities, increased risk of breast cancer, osteoporosis.
20. Fragile X Syndrome
  • Genetics: CGG repeat expansion in FMR1 gene → silencing → lack of FMRP.
  • Features: Intellectual disability, long face, prominent ears, macroorchidism (after puberty), autistic features.
21. Cystic Fibrosis (CF)
  • Genetics: AR, CFTR mutation (ΔF508 most common).
  • Pathology: Defective chloride channel → thick secretions → meconium ileus, pancreatic insufficiency (malabsorption, steatorrhea), chronic lung infections (Pseudomonas, S. aureus), infertility (males).
  • Diagnosis: Sweat chloride test >60 mmol/L.
22. Respiratory Pathology
  • Croup (laryngotracheobronchitis): Parainfluenza virus; subglottic edema → barking cough, stridor.
  • Epiglottitis: H. influenzae type b (now rare due to vaccine); rapid onset, high fever, drooling, “tripod” position.
  • Bronchiolitis: RSV; small airway inflammation, wheezing, air trapping.
23. Cardiac Pathology
  • Kawasaki disease: Acute systemic vasculitis (medium vessels); fever >5 days, conjunctival injection, strawberry tongue, rash, cervical lymphadenopathy; can cause coronary artery aneurysms.
  • Rheumatic fever: Post‑streptococcal; pancarditis, subcutaneous nodules, erythema marginatum, chorea, arthritis.
24. GI Pathology
  • Intussusception: Telescoping of bowel (ileocolic); “currant jelly” stool, intermittent abdominal pain; lead point (Peyer's patch, Meckel diverticulum).
  • Appendicitis: Inflammation of appendix; peak incidence 10‑20 years.
25. Renal Pathology
  • Post‑streptococcal glomerulonephritis: Immune complex mediated after GAS infection; hematuria, hypertension, edema.
  • Minimal change disease (nil disease): Most common nephrotic syndrome in children; fusion of podocytes; responds to steroids.
  • Hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS): Usually post‑diarrheal (E. coli O157:H7); triad: microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, acute renal failure.
26. Endocrine Pathology
  • Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH): 21‑hydroxylase deficiency most common; salt‑wasting, ambiguous genitalia (46,XX virilized), hyperkalemia, hypotension.
  • Type 1 diabetes: Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells; presents with polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss, DKA.
27. Neurologic Pathology
  • Neural tube defects (covered earlier).
  • Hydrocephalus.
28. Musculoskeletal Pathology
  • Developmental dysplasia of the hip (DDH): Abnormal acetabulum; Barlow/Ortolani tests, risk factors: breech, family history.
  • Legg‑Calvé‑Perthes disease: Avascular necrosis of femoral head (4‑8 years).
  • Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE): Adolescent, obese, limping, hip/knee pain.
  • Osteogenesis imperfecta: Collagen defect; brittle bones, blue sclera, hearing loss.
29. Hematologic Pathology
  • Iron deficiency anemia: Most common anemia in children; microcytic, hypochromic; pica, pallor.
  • Sickle cell disease: HbS; vaso‑occlusive crises, splenic infarction (autosplenectomy), increased infection risk.
  • Thalassemia: Microcytic anemia; alpha or beta globin chain defects.
  • Hemophilia A: Factor VIII deficiency; X‑linked; joint bleeds.
