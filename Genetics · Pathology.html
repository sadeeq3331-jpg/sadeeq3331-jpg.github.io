2. Structural Chromosome Abnormalities
  • Translocation: Reciprocal (balanced) or Robertsonian (centric fusion; acrocentric chromosomes 13,14,15,21,22). Robertsonian translocation carriers have 45 chromosomes but phenotypically normal; risk of unbalanced offspring (e.g., Down syndrome translocation type).
  • Deletion: Loss of part of chromosome. Examples: Cri‑du‑chat (5p‑), Wolf‑Hirschhorn (4p‑), 22q11.2 deletion (DiGeorge syndrome).
  • Duplication: Extra copy of segment.
  • Inversion: Segment reversed; carriers usually normal but risk of unbalanced gametes.
  • Isochromosome: Two identical arms (e.g., isochromosome Xq in Turner).
  • Ring chromosome: Caused by deletion of both telomeres and fusion.

7. Mitochondrial Diseases
  • Maternal inheritance; heteroplasmy; tissues with high energy demand affected.
  • Examples:
    • MELAS: Mitochondrial encephalomyopathy, lactic acidosis, stroke‑like episodes.
    • MERRF: Myoclonic epilepsy with ragged red fibers.
    • Leber hereditary optic neuropathy (LHON): Acute vision loss.

9. Multifactorial Inheritance
  • Interaction of multiple genes + environment.
  • Family history shows increased risk but not Mendelian pattern.
  • Examples: Neural tube defects (folic acid prevention), cleft lip/palate, diabetes, hypertension, congenital heart defects.
10. Oncogenes & Tumor Suppressors
Oncogenes
  • Proto‑oncogenes promote growth; mutations cause gain‑of‑function (dominant).
  • Examples: RAS (point mutation), MYC (translocation), HER2 (amplification).
Tumor Suppressor Genes
  • Inhibit growth; mutations cause loss‑of‑function (recessive, but one hit inherited in hereditary syndromes). Two‑hit hypothesis.
  • Examples: RB1 (retinoblastoma), TP53 (Li‑Fraumeni), APC (FAP), BRCA1/BRCA2 (breast/ovarian).
