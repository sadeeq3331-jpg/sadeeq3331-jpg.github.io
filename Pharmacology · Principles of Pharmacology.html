1. Absorption
Absorption is the movement of a drug from its site of administration into the bloodstream.
  • Routes of administration:
    • Enteral: oral (PO), sublingual (SL), rectal (PR).
    • Parenteral: intravenous (IV), intramuscular (IM), subcutaneous (SC).
    • Other: topical, inhalation, transdermal.
  • Bioavailability (F): Fraction of administered drug that reaches systemic circulation. IV = 100%. Oral <100% due to first‑pass metabolism.
  • Factors affecting absorption: Lipid solubility, pH (ionization), blood flow, surface area, gastric emptying.
2. Distribution
  • Volume of distribution (Vd): Apparent space into which a drug distributes. Vd = dose / plasma concentration. High Vd means extensive tissue binding.
  • Protein binding: Albumin (acidic drugs), α₁‑acid glycoprotein (basic drugs). Only free drug is active.
  • Barriers: Blood‑brain barrier, placental barrier.
3. Metabolism (Biotransformation)
Metabolism converts lipophilic drugs into more water‑soluble compounds for excretion.
  • Phase I (functionalization): Oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis (cytochrome P450 enzymes). Introduces or exposes a functional group.
  • Phase II (conjugation): Glucuronidation, sulfation, acetylation, glutathione conjugation. Increases water solubility.
  • First‑pass metabolism: Drugs absorbed from gut are metabolized in liver before reaching systemic circulation (e.g., propranolol, lidocaine).
  • Prodrugs: Inactive until metabolized (e.g., enalapril → enalaprilat).
4. Excretion
  • Renal excretion: Glomerular filtration, tubular secretion, passive reabsorption. pH‑dependent ion trapping (weak acids in alkaline urine).
  • Biliary excretion: Some drugs excreted in bile; enterohepatic recirculation.
  • Other routes: Pulmonary (volatile agents), sweat, milk.
  • Half‑life (t½): Time for drug concentration to decrease by 50%. Determines dosing interval.
5. Drug‑Receptor Interactions
  • Receptor types: G‑protein coupled (GPCRs), ion channels, enzyme‑linked (tyrosine kinase), nuclear receptors.
  • Affinity: Strength of binding between drug and receptor.
  • Efficacy (intrinsic activity): Ability to activate receptor and produce a response.
6. Dose‑Response Relationships
  • Graded dose‑response: Effect increases with dose. Parameters: Emax (max effect), EC₅₀ (concentration for 50% effect).
  • Quantal dose‑response: All‑or‑none effect in population. ED₅₀ (median effective dose), TD₅₀ (median toxic dose), LD₅₀ (median lethal dose).
7. Agonists & Antagonists
  • Full agonist: Maximal efficacy.
  • Partial agonist: Submaximal efficacy even at full receptor occupancy.
  • Antagonist: No efficacy, blocks agonist.
    • Competitive (surmountable): shifts dose‑response curve right, same Emax.
    • Non‑competitive (insurmountable): decreases Emax.
  • Inverse agonist: Reduces basal receptor activity (e.g., some antihistamines).
8. Therapeutic Index & Safety
  • Therapeutic index (TI) = TD₅₀ / ED₅₀ (or LD₅₀/ED₅₀). Narrow TI = small safety margin (e.g., warfarin, digoxin, lithium).
  • Therapeutic window: Range between minimum effective concentration and minimum toxic concentration.
9. Clinical Trial Phases
  • Phase I: Healthy volunteers; pharmacokinetics, safety, dose‑ranging (20‑100).
  • Phase II: Patients with disease; efficacy, side effects (100‑300).
  • Phase III: Large multicenter trials; confirm efficacy, compare to standard (1,000‑3,000).
  • Phase IV: Post‑marketing surveillance; long‑term safety, rare adverse effects.
10. Regulatory Approval
  • FDA (USA), EMA (Europe), MHRA (UK).
  • Investigational New Drug (IND) application before human trials.
  • New Drug Application (NDA) for marketing approval.
11. Pediatric Pharmacology
  • Immature hepatic metabolism (CYP450) and renal function.
  • Higher total body water affects Vd (hydrophilic drugs).
  • Doses often based on weight (mg/kg) or body surface area.
12. Geriatric Pharmacology
  • Decreased renal function (↓ GFR), decreased hepatic metabolism.
  • Increased sensitivity to CNS drugs (benzodiazepines, opioids).
  • Polypharmacy → higher risk of drug interactions.
13. Pregnancy & Lactation
  • FDA pregnancy categories: A (safe), B (probably safe), C (risk not ruled out), D (positive evidence of risk), X (contraindicated).
  • Drugs may cross placenta or enter breast milk.
14. Adverse Drug Reactions
  • Type A (augmented): Predictable, dose‑dependent (e.g., hypotension with antihypertensives).
  • Type B (bizarre): Idiosyncratic, not dose‑related (e.g., anaphylaxis, Stevens‑Johnson syndrome).
  • Type C (chronic): Long‑term use (e.g., osteoporosis with steroids).
  • Type D (delayed): Carcinogenesis, teratogenesis.
15. Drug Interactions
  • Pharmacokinetic: Altered absorption, distribution, metabolism (CYP inhibition/induction), excretion.
  • Pharmacodynamic: Additive, synergistic, antagonistic effects at receptor level.
