1. Introduction to Skin

The skin is the largest organ of the body, accounting for about 15% of total body weight. It serves as a protective barrier against environmental insults, prevents water loss, regulates temperature, provides sensory information, and has immune and endocrine functions.

Layers of the Skin
  • Epidermis: Stratified squamous keratinized epithelium, derived from ectoderm. Avascular.
  • Dermis: Connective tissue layer derived from mesoderm; contains blood vessels, nerves, hair follicles, glands. Composed of collagen, elastin, ground substance.
  • Hypodermis (subcutaneous tissue): Loose connective tissue and adipose tissue; provides insulation and padding.

Clinical pearl: The thickness of skin varies: thickest on palms and soles (0.5‑1.5 mm epidermis), thinnest on eyelids.

2. Epidermis

The epidermis is a keratinized stratified squamous epithelium composed primarily of keratinocytes (90‑95%). It contains four major cell types and layers.

Cell Types
  • Keratinocytes: Produce keratin, form the stratified layers.
  • Melanocytes: Derived from neural crest, produce melanin, located in basal layer.
  • Langerhans cells: Dendritic antigen‑presenting cells (immune surveillance).
  • Merkel cells: Mechanoreceptors associated with tactile sensation (Merkel discs).
Layers of the Epidermis (from deep to superficial)
  • Stratum basale (germinativum): Single layer of cuboidal/columnar keratinocytes attached to basement membrane; contains melanocytes and Merkel cells; site of mitotic activity.
  • Stratum spinosum: Several layers of polyhedral keratinocytes with desmosomes (spiny appearance); Langerhans cells present.
  • Stratum granulosum: 2‑3 layers of flattened cells containing keratohyalin granules (filaggrin) and lamellar bodies (lipid secretion).
  • Stratum lucidum: Clear layer present only in thick skin (palms, soles).
  • Stratum corneum: Multiple layers of anucleated, flattened, fully keratinized cells (corneocytes) embedded in lipid matrix; provides barrier function.

Memory aid: "Come Let's Get Sun Burned" – Corneum, Lucidum, Granulosum, Spinosum, Basale.

Clinical pearl: In psoriasis, hyperproliferation leads to thickened epidermis, retention of nuclei in stratum corneum (parakeratosis).

3. Dermis & Hypodermis

The dermis provides structural support and nourishment to the epidermis. It is composed of collagen (type I and III), elastin, and ground substance (proteoglycans, hyaluronic acid).

Layers of the Dermis
  • Papillary dermis: Loose connective tissue, forms dermal papillae, contains capillaries, sensory nerve endings (Meissner corpuscles).
  • Reticular dermis: Dense irregular connective tissue, thicker, contains collagen bundles, elastic fibers, hair follicles, glands, blood vessels, nerves.
Cells of the Dermis
  • Fibroblasts: Produce extracellular matrix.
  • Mast cells, macrophages, lymphocytes.
Hypodermis (Subcutaneous Fat)
  • Adipose tissue arranged in lobules separated by fibrous septa. Contains larger blood vessels and nerves. Functions: insulation, energy storage, cushioning.

Clinical pearl: Striae (stretch marks) occur when dermis tears due to rapid stretching (pregnancy, growth spurts, Cushing syndrome).

4. Skin Appendages: Hair, Nails, Glands
Hair Follicles
  • Derived from epidermis, extend into dermis/hypodermis. Hair grows in cycles: anagen (growth), catagen (regression), telogen (resting).
  • Hair shaft: medulla, cortex, cuticle. Associated with sebaceous gland and arrector pili muscle.
Nails
  • Hard keratin plates produced by nail matrix. Nail plate, nail bed, hyponychium, eponychium (cuticle).
Sebaceous Glands
  • Holocrine glands secreting sebum (lipids) into hair follicle. Function: lubrication, waterproofing, antimicrobial. Androgens stimulate sebum production.
Sweat Glands
  • Eccrine glands: Widely distributed, open directly onto skin surface. Produce hypotonic sweat for thermoregulation (cholinergic sympathetic).
  • Apocrine glands: Found in axilla, anogenital region; open into hair follicles; produce milky, odorless secretion that becomes malodorous after bacterial action. Begin functioning at puberty.

Clinical pearl: Alopecia (hair loss) can result from anagen or telogen effluvium, autoimmune (alopecia areata), or scarring. Acne vulgaris involves sebaceous gland overactivity and follicular plugging.

5. Pigmentation & Melanocytes

Melanocytes are neural crest‑derived cells residing in the stratum basale and hair follicles. They produce melanin and transfer it to keratinocytes via dendrites.

Melanin Types
  • Eumelanin: Brown‑black pigment.
  • Pheomelanin: Yellow‑red pigment (higher in redheads).
Melanogenesis
  • Tyrosine → DOPA → dopaquinone → melanin (via tyrosinase). Stimulated by UV radiation (α‑MSH binds MC1R).
Distribution
  • Melanin packaged in melanosomes transferred to keratinocytes, forming a supranuclear cap that protects DNA from UV damage.

Clinical pearl: Albinism: deficiency of tyrosinase; vitiligo: autoimmune destruction of melanocytes; freckles (ephelides): increased melanin in keratinocytes, not melanocyte number.

6. Barrier Function & Immunology

The skin provides a physical, chemical, and immunological barrier.

  • Physical barrier: Stratum corneum (tight junctions, corneodesmosomes, lipid matrix).
  • Chemical barrier: Antimicrobial peptides (defensins, cathelicidin), acidic pH, sebum (fatty acids), sweat (lysozyme).
  • Immunological barrier: Langerhans cells (antigen presentation), dermal dendritic cells, macrophages, mast cells, and lymphocytes. Cytokine production.

Clinical pearl: Atopic dermatitis is associated with impaired barrier function (filaggrin mutations).

7. Thermoregulation & Circulation

Skin plays a key role in regulating body temperature through blood flow and sweating.

  • Arteriovenous anastomoses (glomus bodies) in acral skin regulate heat loss.
  • Eccrine sweat gland secretion (sympathetic cholinergic) cools via evaporation.
  • Vasodilation increases heat loss; vasoconstriction conserves heat.

Clinical pearl: Anhidrosis (absence of sweating) can cause hyperthermia; hyperhidrosis is excessive sweating.

8. Vitamin D Synthesis

The skin is the site of vitamin D synthesis. 7‑dehydrocholesterol (provitamin D₃) in keratinocytes is converted to cholecalciferol (vitamin D₃) by UVB radiation (290‑315 nm).

  • Vitamin D₃ then undergoes hydroxylation in liver (to 25‑OH‑D) and kidney (to active 1,25‑(OH)₂‑D).

Clinical pearl: Vitamin D deficiency causes rickets in children and osteomalacia in adults. Sunlight exposure is essential for adequate vitamin D synthesis.

9. Wound Healing

Wound healing is a complex process involving overlapping phases: hemostasis, inflammation, proliferation, and remodeling.

  • Hemostasis: Platelet aggregation, clot formation, release of growth factors (PDGF, TGF‑β).
  • Inflammation: Neutrophils and macrophages remove debris and pathogens; macrophages also release cytokines.
  • Proliferation: Re‑epithelialization (keratinocyte migration), angiogenesis, granulation tissue formation (fibroblasts, collagen), wound contraction (myofibroblasts).
  • Remodeling: Collagen remodeling (type III replaced by type I), scar maturation.

Clinical pearl: Keloids are excessive scar formation beyond wound margins, more common in darker skin. Hypertrophic scars are raised but confined to wound.

10. Aging & Photoaging

Intrinsic aging (chronological) and extrinsic aging (photoaging) affect skin differently.

  • Intrinsic aging: Thinning of epidermis, flattening of dermo‑epidermal junction, decreased collagen, elastin degradation, decreased sebaceous and sweat gland activity.
  • Photoaging: Caused by UV radiation; leads to solar elastosis (accumulation of abnormal elastic fibers), wrinkles, dyspigmentation, telangiectasias, premalignant lesions (actinic keratoses).

Clinical pearl: Sun protection prevents photoaging and skin cancers. Topical retinoids can partially reverse some photoaging changes.
