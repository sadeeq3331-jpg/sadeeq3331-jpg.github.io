1. Anatomy of the Eye
Layers of the Eyeball
  • Fibrous tunic (outer): sclera (white) and cornea (transparent).
  • Vascular tunic (uvea): iris, ciliary body, choroid.
  • Nervous tunic (inner): retina.
Anterior Segment
  • Cornea: avascular, receives nutrients from aqueous humor and tears; 5 layers (epithelium, Bowman's, stroma, Descemet's, endothelium).
  • Anterior chamber: between cornea and iris, filled with aqueous humor.
  • Posterior chamber: between iris and lens.
  • Iris: controls pupil size; muscles: sphincter pupillae (parasympathetic, miosis) and dilator pupillae (sympathetic, mydriasis).
  • Ciliary body: produces aqueous humor; contains ciliary muscle (accommodation).
  • Lens: biconvex, transparent, changes shape for accommodation.
Posterior Segment
  • Vitreous humor: gel filling posterior cavity.
  • Retina: layers: photoreceptors (rods and cones), bipolar cells, ganglion cells; macula (central vision) and fovea (highest acuity).
  • Optic disc: blind spot, where optic nerve exits.
Layers from outside in: "SCLera, Choroid, Retina" – like a sandwich.
2. Physiology of Vision
Refraction

Light is bent by cornea (most power) and lens. Accommodation: ciliary muscle contraction relaxes zonules, lens becomes more rounded (for near vision). Presbyopia: loss of lens elasticity with age.

Phototransduction
  • Rods: dim light (scotopic), rhodopsin (opsin + retinal), monochrome.
  • Cones: color vision (photopic), three types (red, green, blue).
  • In dark: cGMP high, Na⁺ channels open (depolarized). Light activates opsin, activates transducin → activates PDE → ↓ cGMP → channels close → hyperpolarization → less glutamate release.

Night blindness: from vitamin A deficiency (retinal needed for rhodopsin).

4. Conjunctiva & Cornea
Conjunctivitis
  • Viral: watery discharge, preauricular lymphadenopathy (adenovirus).
  • Bacterial: purulent discharge, injected conjunctiva (Staph, Strep, Haemophilus).
  • Allergic: itching, watery, cobblestone papillae.
Corneal Disorders
  • Keratitis: corneal inflammation; bacterial (contact lens wear → Pseudomonas), viral (HSV dendritic ulcer).
  • Corneal ulcer: painful, photophobia, fluorescein uptake.
  • Arcus senilis: lipid deposit; normal in elderly, in young suggests hyperlipidemia.
5. Cataract

Opacity of the lens. Risk factors: age, diabetes, smoking, UV light, corticosteroids. Symptoms: gradual vision loss, glare, diminished color perception. Treatment: surgical extraction with intraocular lens implant.

6. Glaucoma

Optic neuropathy often associated with elevated intraocular pressure (IOP).

7. Retinal Disorders
  • Diabetic retinopathy: microaneurysms, hemorrhages, exudates, neovascularization (proliferative).
  • Age‑related macular degeneration (AMD): drusen, geographic atrophy (dry) or choroidal neovascularization (wet).
  • Retinal detachment: flashes, floaters, curtain; emergently repair.
  • Retinitis pigmentosa: night blindness, tunnel vision, bone‑spicule pigmentation.
8. Uveitis & Optic Nerve

Uveitis: inflammation of uvea; anterior (iritis) – painful, photophobia, ciliary flush; associated with HLA‑B27, sarcoid, TB. Treatment: steroids.

Optic neuritis: painful eye movement, vision loss, afferent pupillary defect; often demyelinating (multiple sclerosis).

Papilledema: bilateral optic disc swelling from increased intracranial pressure.

10. Anatomy of the Ear
  • External ear: pinna, external auditory canal.
  • Middle ear: tympanic membrane, ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes), Eustachian tube, oval window, round window.
  • Inner ear: cochlea (hearing), vestibule and semicircular canals (balance).
11. Hearing & Balance
Hearing mechanism

Sound waves → tympanic membrane → ossicles amplify → stapes pushes oval window → pressure wave in cochlea → deflects basilar membrane → hair cells (inner hair cells signal, outer hair cells amplify) → auditory nerve.

Balance

Semicircular canals sense rotation; utricle and saccule sense linear acceleration (otoliths).

13. Otitis Media & Externa
  • Otitis externa: “swimmer's ear”; Pseudomonas, Staph; pain with manipulation; treat with topical antibiotics (neomycin, ciprofloxacin drops).
  • Acute otitis media: purulent middle ear infection; fever, otalgia, bulging TM; common in children; organisms: Strep pneumo, H. influenzae, Moraxella; treat with amoxicillin.
  • Otitis media with effusion: fluid without infection; watchful waiting.
  • Mastoiditis: complication of OM; tenderness over mastoid; requires IV antibiotics +/- surgery.
14. Meniere's & Vertigo

Meniere's disease: endolymphatic hydrops; episodic vertigo, fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus, aural fullness. Treatment: low salt, diuretics, betahistine.

Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV): canalithiasis; brief vertigo with head movement; Dix‑Hallpike test; treat with Epley maneuver.

Vestibular neuritis: acute prolonged vertigo, no hearing loss; viral; treat with steroids, vestibular suppressants (meclizine, diazepam).

15. Anatomy of the Nose
  • Nasal cavity lined by respiratory epithelium (ciliated pseudostratified).
  • Olfactory epithelium in superior nasal cavity.
  • Paranasal sinuses: frontal, maxillary, ethmoid, sphenoid; lined by mucosa, drain via ostia.
16. Sinusitis

Inflammation of sinuses; acute (<4 weeks) vs chronic (>12 weeks). Symptoms: facial pain, purulent nasal discharge, congestion. Organisms: Strep pneumo, H. influenzae, anaerobes (chronic). Treatment: nasal saline, intranasal steroids, antibiotics if bacterial (amoxicillin‑clavulanate). Complications: orbital cellulitis, meningitis.

17. Allergic Rhinitis

Type I hypersensitivity (IgE). Symptoms: sneezing, itching, watery rhinorrhea, congestion. Signs: allergic shiners, nasal crease, pale boggy turbinates. Treatment: antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine), intranasal steroids (fluticasone), leukotriene antagonists, immunotherapy.

18. Pharynx & Larynx
  • Nasopharynx: adenoids, Eustachian tube opening.
  • Oropharynx: tonsils, base of tongue.
  • Hypopharynx: pyriform sinuses.
  • Larynx: vocal cords (true and false), epiglottis; innervation: recurrent laryngeal nerve (all except cricothyroid – superior laryngeal).
19. Pharyngitis & Tonsillitis
  • Viral: most common; rhinovirus, adenovirus, EBV (exudates, fatigue).
  • Group A Strep (GAS): fever, tonsillar exudates, tender cervical nodes, no cough; treat with penicillin or amoxicillin to prevent rheumatic fever.
  • Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy): complication; trismus, uvula deviation; ENT referral.
20. Obstructive Sleep Apnea

Recurrent upper airway collapse during sleep; snoring, daytime sleepiness, apneas. Risk factors: obesity, macroglossia, retrognathia. Diagnosis: polysomnography. Treatment: CPAP, weight loss, mandibular advancement device, surgery (UPPP).

21. Neck Masses & Salivary Glands
  • Lymph nodes: reactive, infectious (TB), metastatic.
  • Branchial cleft cyst: anterior to SCM, painless.
  • Thyroglossal duct cyst: midline, moves with swallowing.
  • Salivary glands: parotid (Stensen's duct), submandibular (Wharton's duct).
  • Sialadenitis: inflammation (usually parotid), often from dehydration or sialolithiasis. Bacterial: Staph aureus, treat with antibiotics, hydration, massage.
  • Mumps: painful parotitis; vaccine‑preventable.
